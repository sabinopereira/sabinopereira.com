# CHAPTER 3

Adrian kept his thumb on the seal.

The Ace embossed on the envelope was gold, simple, almost elegant. It had no crown, sword, or ornament.

It did not need one.

Marcel watched his son from across the table.

Celeste kept one hand near Adrian's arm without touching him. Tomás continued staring at his own envelope, closed beside his plate. Nico broke off a piece of toasted bread and dipped it into the broth.

“That's a strong line,” he said. “Maybe you should let it settle before you open that.”

Adrian looked at him.

“Do you do that with everything?”

“Only the things that frighten me.”

“And this frightens you?”

Nico put the bread in his mouth.

“It isn't my envelope.”

Adrian turned to the Dealer.

“Why have I never had to ask for a seat?”

“Open it.”

“You could answer.”

“I could.”

“But you won't.”

“No.”

Adrian exhaled through his nose.

“Then the envelope isn't evidence. It's theater.”

The Dealer did not react.

Adrian broke the seal.

The wax remained attached to the flap. He removed five pages and aligned them on the table before he began to read.

The first was a letter of recommendation bearing the logo of the university he had attended. He recognized the signature at the bottom.

He read the opening lines.

Moved to the second page.

A table.

Applicants' names.

Scores.

Interviews.

Notes.

On the third page, he found his own name.

It was highlighted in blue.

Beside it, an instruction:

**ADMIT. EXTERNAL PRIORITY.**

Adrian read it again.

Celeste watched his face.

“What is it?”

He did not answer.

He turned to the fourth page.

A chain of messages.

The first came from a member of the university's board. The second from a consultant. The third from an address whose sender had been concealed.

Marcel leaned back.

“You don't have to read that aloud.”

Adrian raised his eyes.

“Do you know what it is?”

“I can imagine.”

“That isn't what I asked.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

The Dealer spoke.

“He knows.”

Adrian looked at his father.

“Since when?”

Marcel did not answer.

Celeste closed her eyes.

Adrian saw.

“You too.”

She opened them.

“Adrian.”

“Did you know?”

“Not everything.”

“How much?”

Celeste took her time.

“Enough.”

Adrian set down the pages.

Not forcefully.

Precisely.

Tomás looked at the table of names.

“Is that the admissions file?”

“Yes.”

“Was it manipulated?”

Adrian looked at the messages again.

“That's what I'm trying to understand.”

The Dealer corrected him.

“It was.”

Adrian turned.

“I haven't finished.”

“The word does not change at the bottom of the page.”

“Perhaps not for you.”

“For anyone.”

Nico watched the Dealer.

“Admirable confidence in language.”

Adrian picked up the messages again.

He read.

“‘The Voss applicant is to be considered regardless of his final score.’”

He stopped.

His voice was lower when he read the next line.

“‘The pledged contribution will be formalized once admission is confirmed.’”

Tomás looked at Marcel.

“A donation.”

“It wasn't an exchange,” Marcel said.

The Dealer replied, “It was.”

Marcel turned to him.

“Families fund universities every year.”

“Yes.”

“That doesn't mean they buy places.”

“In this case, it did.”

Adrian moved to the next page.

There was a list of applicants.

Four names were marked. One had the highest score in the entire process.

Beside it, an instruction:

**MOVE TO WAITLIST.**

Adrian traced the row with his finger.

“What happened to this one?”

The Dealer asked, “Which one?”

“Daniel Rocha.”

“He was not admitted.”

Adrian looked up.

“Because of me?”

“In part.”

“What does that mean?”

“The university could have admitted both of you.”

“But it didn't.”

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because another applicant was moved above him after your admission.”

“Who?”

“A minister's son.”

Tomás gave a quiet laugh.

“A whole line of aces.”

No one answered.

Adrian looked at Daniel's name again.

“What happened to him?”

“He attended another university.”

“Which one?”

“A public one.”

“Then what?”

The Dealer hesitated for a fraction of a second.

Adrian noticed.

“Then what?”

“He dropped out in his second year.”

“Why?”

“He worked nights.”

Adrian tightened his grip on the page.

“Family?”

“A sick mother.”

Celeste looked away.

Adrian turned to her.

“Did you know about him?”

“No.”

“But you knew someone had lost a place.”

“I knew your admission had been facilitated.”

Adrian looked at the document.

“Facilitated.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

“That was the word we used.”

“Who?”

“Me. Your father. The lawyers.”

“Why?”

She opened her eyes.

“So we could live with the decision.”

Marcel interrupted.

“Don't be dramatic.”

Celeste turned to him.

“No.”

“It was a university admission.”

“No.”

“Thousands of families do the same thing.”

Adrian looked at his father.

“How many people were moved?”

Marcel did not answer.

The Dealer answered for him.

“Three applicants lost direct positions in the process.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“All because of him?”

“Two because of the place. One because of the subsequent reordering.”

Adrian scanned the names.

“And you called this an opportunity.”

Marcel replied, “It was an opportunity.”

“For me.”

“Naturally.”

Adrian released a short breath.

The word seemed to return to him altered.

“Naturally.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“You aren't finished.”

Adrian looked at the fifth page.

He had set it aside.

He turned it over.

It was a timeline.

University.

First internship.

First position.

International fellowship.

Entry into a foundation.

Appointment to a board.

Leadership award.

Beside every milestone was a name, a call, a donation, or an intervention.

Adrian read in silence.

The first line surprised him.

So did the second.

At the third, he sat straighter.

When he reached the end, he set down the page.

Nico left the bread on his plate.

Tomás looked at him.

“It wasn't only the university.”

Adrian did not answer.

The Dealer spoke.

“No.”

Celeste leaned toward her son.

“Adrian.”

“No.”

“Let me explain.”

“No.”

“There is context.”

Adrian looked at her.

“Of course.”

There was no anger in the word.

That made Celeste draw back more than if he had shouted.

Adrian pointed to the timeline.

“The internship.”

Marcel answered.

“You were qualified.”

“Who was removed?”

“No one.”

The Dealer corrected him.

“One candidate.”

Marcel exhaled.

“She was given another position.”

“Temporary.”

Adrian looked at the Dealer.

“Name.”

“Leonor Matias.”

“And then?”

“Her contract was not renewed.”

“Why?”

“The budget was transferred to your department.”

Adrian closed his eyes.

When he opened them, he pointed to another line.

“The position at the foundation.”

Celeste spoke.

“That was different.”

“How?”

“You had already worked on similar projects.”

“Who had been selected?”

She did not answer.

The Dealer said, “Helena Duarte.”

Adrian looked at his mother.

“You knew her.”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“We worked together.”

“And you took the position from her.”

Celeste clasped her hands.

“I convinced the board they needed a younger figure.”

“Me.”

“Yes.”

“More media-friendly.”

“Yes.”

“Easier to present.”

Celeste lowered her eyes.

“Yes.”

Adrian looked at the timeline.

“Then it wasn't even about ability.”

Marcel replied, “Ability is never enough.”

Everyone looked at him.

Marcel continued.

“The world rewards presence, confidence, connections. Not talent alone.”

Nico tilted his head.

“And pledged contributions.”

Marcel ignored him.

“Adrian did the work.”

Adrian raised his eyes.

“After you put me there.”

“Yes.”

“So in your mind, I earned it.”

“Yes.”

“Because I performed well in a role you bought for me.”

“Because you demonstrated ability.”

Adrian gave a small laugh.

“After you removed the people who might have demonstrated more.”

Marcel clenched his jaw.

“You don't know that they were better.”

“Neither did you.”

“I knew you.”

Adrian nodded.

“Exactly.”

The answer remained on the table.

Adrian picked up the timeline again.

“The award.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Marcel replied, “It wasn't bought.”

The Dealer spoke.

“Not directly.”

Adrian turned to him.

“Explain.”

“Two members of the judging panel had pending contracts with the Voss Group.”

Marcel answered.

“They were not instructed.”

“They received suggestions.”

“That isn't the same thing.”

“To them, it was.”

Adrian set down the page.

“How many things did I do on my own?”

No one answered.

This time, he did not search for a particular face.

“How many?”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“I don't know.”

“Of everything in here.”

“I cannot measure it.”

“Then what is the dossier for?”

“To show the interventions.”

“It doesn't show what I did afterward.”

“No.”

“It doesn't show whether I worked.”

“No.”

“Or whether I was competent.”

“No.”

Adrian took a deep breath.

“Then it doesn't prove I'm a fraud.”

Marcel straightened.

“Exactly.”

Adrian turned toward him.

The speed with which his father seized on the sentence seemed to repel him.

“It doesn't prove that I'm not one either.”

Marcel went still.

Celeste spoke.

“You worked.”

“I know.”

“You studied.”

“I know.”

“You spent entire nights…”

“I know.”

Adrian tightened his grip on the pages.

“That's the problem.”

Celeste frowned.

“What is?”

“I remember.”

He looked at the documents.

“I remember studying. The interviews. The presentations. Every time I thought I had earned something.”

His voice lost some of its strength, but he continued.

“And now I don't know where to put those memories.”

Nico answered.

“They don't disappear.”

Adrian looked at him.

“Maybe they should.”

“Why?”

“Because the path was bought.”

“That changes the path.”

“And the result.”

“Yes.”

“Then what?”

Nico ran his thumb along the edge of the spoon.

“It doesn't automatically turn every hour of work into a lie.”

Adrian studied him.

“Are you trying to help?”

“Don't spread it around.”

Marcel spoke.

“He's right.”

Adrian turned at once.

“Don't use that.”

“Use what?”

“Any sentence that lets me keep everything.”

Marcel leaned back.

“You don't need to destroy your life over decisions made when you were eighteen.”

“I was eighteen.”

“Exactly.”

“Not eight.”

Celeste looked at him.

“You didn't know.”

Adrian nodded.

“No.”

“Then it isn't your fault.”

He was silent for a moment.

“When does it become my fault?”

Celeste frowned.

“What do you mean?”

“I didn't know on the first day.”

He ran a finger along the timeline.

“Or the second. Or when I got the internship.”

He looked at his parents.

“But how many doors have to open without explanation before ignorance becomes a choice?”

No one answered.

Adrian continued.

“I never asked why the director knew my name before the interview. I never asked why an internship appeared after a dinner. I never asked why certain people laughed before I finished a sentence.”

Marcel replied, “That is influence.”

“Yes.”

Adrian looked at the list.

“And I liked it.”

The words came quietly.

Tomás raised his eyes.

Adrian went on.

“I liked walking into a room where people already knew me. I liked believing my name arrived first because I was important.”

Marcel opened one hand.

“You are.”

Adrian looked at him.

“You don't know that.”

Marcel's face changed.

Barely.

Adrian saw it.

“Maybe I am. Maybe I have talent. Maybe I've done good work.”

He set down the timeline.

“But I have never had to find out without protection.”

Nico looked at the Ace on the envelope.

“That's the advantage of a high card.”

Adrian followed his gaze.

“What is?”

“It starts the game with value before it has done anything.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“It can also be worth one.”

Nico looked at him.

“Depending on the rules.”

“Yes.”

Adrian ran a finger over the gold figure.

“And who chose the rules?”

The Dealer replied, “Your parents, sometimes.”

“And the other times?”

“People who wanted something from them.”

Marcel spoke.

“That is how the world works.”

Adrian looked at him.

“Is that sentence an explanation or a defense?”

“A description.”

“It seems to serve both purposes.”

Tomás picked up his water glass.

“Can I ask you something?”

Adrian looked at him.

“You just did.”

Tomás did not smile.

“When you walk into a room, can you tell who didn't get in because you were there?”

Adrian went still.

Tomás pointed to the table of names.

“I couldn't.”

Adrian lowered his eyes.

“No.”

“Can you now?”

Adrian looked at Daniel Rocha's name.

“Not completely.”

Tomás nodded.

“But now you know someone exists.”

“Yes.”

The Dealer asked, “Do you want the dossier released now?”

Celeste answered before him.

“No.”

Adrian turned.

“I haven't answered yet.”

“I know.”

“Then don't answer for me.”

She pressed her lips together.

Marcel spoke.

“If you release it now, the university will open an investigation before we can verify the documents. The newspapers will reduce everything to these pages.”

Adrian looked at him.

“And if it stays on the table?”

“We have time.”

“For what?”

Marcel hesitated.

It was enough.

“To prepare,” he said.

“To control.”

“It isn't the same thing.”

“For you, it never is.”

Celeste brought her hand closer.

She touched his arm.

Adrian did not pull away.

“You don't have to decide while you're in shock.”

He looked at her.

“Is that what you told yourself when you canceled the call?”

Celeste removed her hand.

Silence fell.

Adrian closed his eyes.

“I'm sorry.”

“No.”

He opened them.

“No what?”

“Don't apologize just because the sentence hurt me.”

Celeste took a breath.

“Ask whether it's true.”

Adrian held her gaze.

“Is it?”

She waited.

“In part.”

Nico released a quiet breath.

Celeste continued.

“I told myself I needed time. That a decision made in shock could destroy everything.”

She looked at Marcel.

“The truth is, I wanted the shock to pass before choosing whatever protected me.”

Adrian absorbed the answer without looking away.

The Dealer remained still.

Celeste turned back to her son.

“So I won't tell you to wait for your own sake.”

“Then what?”

“Wait for the people whose names are on those pages.”

Adrian looked at them.

Daniel.

Leonor.

Helena.

Others who were still only lines.

“If I release it now, will they receive it?”

The Dealer replied, “Yes.”

“All of them?”

“Those who have been identified.”

“Do the documents expose anything about them?”

“Yes.”

Adrian tightened his grip on the table.

“What?”

“The circumstances of Daniel's mother. Leonor's confidential evaluations. Helena's private messages.”

“So to confess what was done for me, I publish their lives.”

“Yes.”

“If it stays on the table?”

“The material may be reviewed before any release.”

“By whom?”

“Its fate will be decided at the end.”

Adrian looked at the empty chair.

“By one person.”

“In part.”

Adrian raised his eyes.

“In part?”

The Dealer did not explain.

Nico watched him.

Adrian returned to the pages.

“It stays.”

Marcel exhaled.

Adrian looked at him.

“Not to protect me.”

“It doesn't matter.”

“It does.”

“Not to the outcome.”

Adrian placed the pages inside the envelope.

“That's why you never know when a decision belongs to you.”

Marcel fell silent.

Adrian set the dossier beside his plate.

“It stays on the table until their names can be separated from mine.”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“Even knowing someone else may decide to release everything?”

“Yes.”

“Or conceal it?”

Adrian looked at the chair at the end of the table.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Adrian ran his thumb over the Ace.

“Because I've spent my entire life benefiting from decisions made while the people affected weren't in the room.”

He lowered his hand.

“I won't do it again just to look brave.”

Tomás looked at his own envelope.

Nico gave a slight nod.

Marcel watched his son.

“And what will you do afterward?”

Adrian looked at him.

“I don't know.”

“That isn't an answer.”

“It's the only one you didn't prepare for me.”

Samuel approached to clear the plate.

The broth remained untouched.

“Are you finished, sir?”

Adrian looked at the bowl.

“I never started.”

Samuel waited.

Adrian picked up the spoon.

He tasted the broth.

It was hot and too salty.

He ate another spoonful.

Marcel watched him.

“You don't need to prove anything.”

Adrian did not look up.

“Maybe this is the first thing I'm doing without needing to.”

He ate slowly.

He did not finish it all.

When he set down the spoon, Samuel removed the bowl.

The envelope remained before him.

Nico pointed to the Ace.

“Worth one or eleven?”

Adrian ran a finger over the figure.

“Today?”

“Today.”

Adrian thought.

“One.”

Marcel closed his eyes.

Nico leaned back.

“It's a start.”

The side doors opened.

The third course arrived in the service elevator.

Fish.

Brown butter.

Herbs.

Samuel placed the plate before Celeste.

Then he removed a black envelope from the sideboard.

A Queen appeared on its cover.

Celeste looked at it.

She did not touch it.

Adrian kept his eyes on his own dossier.

Marcel picked up his glass.

Tomás watched Celeste.

Nico broke off another piece of bread.

The Dealer began to shuffle.

Celeste ran a finger over the seal.

“My son is still trying to understand whether anything in his life is real.”

The Dealer did not raise his voice.

“Your dossier explains how many lives you made unreal to protect his.”

# CHAPTER 6

Nico looked at the blue envelope.

Everyone else had received thick paper sealed with black wax.

His had no seal.

The flap was merely folded, as if someone had already opened it and closed it again without much care.

The envelope rested on the face-down blue card. The two surfaces were exactly the same color.

Nico picked up his spoon.

Dessert was a sphere of dark chocolate accompanied by a sliver of candied orange. He touched it with the tip of the spoon.

The shell broke with a crack.

Inside was a white cream.

Tomás watched him.

“Are you really going to eat?”

Nico tasted a spoonful.

“I've spent the evening watching all of you lose your appetite. Someone has to respect the kitchen.”

Marcel leaned back.

He had recovered some of his control. His face was composed again, though he kept his hands beneath the table.

“Do you always do that?”

Nico looked at him.

“Do what?”

“Turn everything into a joke.”

“No.”

Nico studied his plate.

“Sometimes I turn it into silence. But no one buys a ticket for that.”

Adrian did not smile.

Celeste kept her eyes on the blue envelope.

Samuel remained by the wall, the box containing Artur's watch stored in the sideboard behind him.

The Dealer set down the deck.

“Open it.”

Nico continued eating.

“No.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

Nico looked at him.

“Don't get excited. It isn't courage yet.”

The Dealer kept his voice low.

“You may refuse.”

“And the dossier is released.”

“Yes.”

“To the press, the authorities, and the people affected.”

“Yes.”

Nico broke off another piece of chocolate.

“It's nice to see a rule survive more than one course.”

Then he pushed the envelope away a few inches.

“So I won't open it.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“Why?”

Nico looked at him.

“Because they want me to.”

“That's your reason?”

“It's a reason.”

The Dealer replied, “Not the real one.”

Nico tasted the dessert again.

“I'm beginning to think you have trouble accepting rejection.”

“The envelope has already been opened.”

The spoon stopped.

Nico looked at the folded flap.

Then at the Dealer.

“By whom?”

There was no answer.

Celeste lowered her eyes.

Nico saw.

“You.”

She raised them.

“No.”

“But you know.”

Celeste waited a moment.

“I know who had access.”

“That isn't what I asked.”

Nico put down the spoon.

“How long have you known what's in here?”

“Part of it?”

“How long?”

Celeste took a deep breath.

“Years.”

The word came without drama.

Perhaps that was why it struck harder.

Nico ran his tongue along the inside of his cheek.

“Years.”

“Yes.”

“And you never said anything.”

“No.”

“Why?”

Celeste looked at the envelope.

“Because I didn't know whether telling you would protect you or destroy you.”

Nico gave a quiet laugh.

“So you decided for me.”

She received the sentence in silence.

Adrian looked at his mother.

Nico continued.

“One hundred twenty-three records, and you still found room for one more.”

“I didn't record you.”

“No?”

“No.”

“Then what did you do?”

“I kept information.”

Nico looked around.

“That's different.”

No one answered.

Marcel wore a slight smile.

Nico saw.

“Don't look so pleased. This doesn't make you any less guilty.”

Marcel replied, “I don't need it to.”

“That's what everyone says when the comparison suits them.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“Open it.”

Nico pulled the envelope toward him.

He ran his thumb along the flap.

“How much time do I have?”

“Enough.”

“You keep measuring time the way a priest measures guilt.”

“I don't know how a priest measures guilt.”

“With interest.”

No one laughed.

Nico opened the envelope.

Inside were three objects.

A photograph.

A newspaper clipping.

A folded sheet of paper.

He removed the photograph first.

It was old.

It showed a small stage beneath white spotlights. A younger Nico held a microphone before an almost empty audience. His hair was longer, his suit too large, and his confidence now looked borrowed.

In the left corner, partly concealed by the wings, stood a woman.

Nico drew the photograph closer.

Only her profile was visible.

Even so, he recognized her.

His hand tightened on the paper.

Adrian saw.

“Who is she?”

Nico turned the photograph facedown.

“No one.”

The Dealer replied, “Sara Almeida.”

Nico raised his eyes.

The change was small.

The humor disappeared before he could stop it.

“Don't say her name.”

“You didn't ask me not to.”

“Not yet.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Tomás watched her.

“Did you know her?”

Celeste replied, “Yes.”

Nico turned.

“You knew her?”

“A little.”

“How little?”

“Two meetings.”

“When?”

“After the publication.”

Nico went still.

Marcel answered.

“The foundation.”

Nico looked at him.

Marcel leaned back.

“I remember. That was when you became famous.”

“That was when people began to recognize me.”

“Same thing.”

“No.”

“It is to anyone who needs a stage.”

Nico did not answer.

He removed the newspaper clipping.

The headline occupied almost half the page.

**YOUNG COMEDIAN EXPOSES FAVORITISM NETWORK AT CULTURAL FOUNDATION**

Beneath it was a photograph of him leaving a building, surrounded by microphones.

He read the first paragraph.

Then the second.

The article described an investigation launched after internal documents from an arts funding foundation were released. Several administrators had used public funds to benefit relatives, friends, and affiliated companies.

It called Nico a whistleblower.

An independent voice.

A man who had refused money to remain silent.

Nico read that sentence twice.

Tomás noticed.

“What's wrong?”

Nico set down the clipping.

“Almost everything that looks good in print.”

He removed the folded page.

It was a copy of three bank transfers.

The first had occurred three days before publication.

The second six months later.

The third a year after that.

The beneficiary was Nico Saint.

The source was concealed behind a company he did not recognize.

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“So you had a price.”

Nico did not look up.

“Shut up.”

Marcel smiled.

“The difference between you and us…”

The Dealer interrupted him.

“Nico did not receive money to remain silent.”

Marcel's smile faded.

Nico looked at the Dealer.

“Say the rest.”

“You can.”

“Say it.”

“No.”

Nico struck the table with his hand.

The plate shifted.

“You've spent the evening presenting facts about everyone else.”

“Yes.”

“Then present this one.”

The Dealer held his gaze.

“You were paid to tell only part of the truth.”

Silence fell.

Nico closed his eyes.

Tomás leaned back.

Adrian looked at the transfers.

Celeste looked at no one.

Marcel smiled again.

Nico gave a small laugh.

“I should have charged more.”

The sound died on its own.

Even he did not believe the line.

He opened his eyes.

“The documents were real.”

“Yes.”

“The corruption existed.”

“Yes.”

“Say that.”

“I just did.”

Nico tightened his grip on the page.

“What wasn't published?”

“The link between the foundation and a larger financial network.”

Marcel went still.

Celeste looked at him.

Tomás noticed.

Adrian asked, “What network?”

Nico looked at the photograph again.

“The foundation moved money.”

“How much?” Tomás asked.

“Small amounts. Grants. Contracts. Exhibitions. Events.”

“Money laundering?”

“Part of it.”

Marcel spoke.

“That was never proven.”

The Dealer looked at him.

“It was.”

Marcel fell silent.

Nico continued.

“It looked like petty corruption. Directors hiring relatives. Friends funding friends.”

Tomás leaned closer.

“But it was a front.”

“Yes.”

“For whom?”

Nico looked at Marcel.

He understood.

“Careful.”

Nico exhaled.

“Sara found links to companies in the Voss Group.”

Adrian looked at his father.

Celeste kept her face still.

Marcel replied, “A lie.”

The Dealer corrected him.

“No.”

Nico continued.

“It wasn't only the group. There were campaigns. Intermediary companies. Judges. Journalists.”

Tomás looked at the Dealer.

“And him?”

Nico studied the man at the head of the table.

“She found references to someone who organized the connections.”

The Dealer did not move.

“He had no name,” Nico said. “Only a role.”

Adrian looked at him.

“The Dealer.”

“Yes.”

The rain seemed to move closer to the windows.

Nico picked up the photograph again.

“Sara wanted to turn over everything.”

Tomás pointed at the transfers.

“And they paid you to reduce the story.”

“They said that if we published only the foundation documents, she would be protected.”

“Who said that?”

“A voice on the phone.”

“Name?”

“I don't know.”

The Dealer spoke.

“True.”

Marcel laughed.

“Convenient.”

Nico looked at him.

“At the time, you were only a name in the documents.”

“And now?”

“Now you're a man who closed a door.”

Marcel fell silent.

Adrian asked, “Did you know the story was incomplete when you published it?”

Nico did not answer.

“Nico.”

“Yes.”

“You knew.”

“Yes.”

“And you published it anyway.”

Nico put down the photograph.

“Yes.”

Tomás watched him.

“Because you believed she would be protected?”

Nico hesitated.

“Because I needed to.”

“That isn't what I asked.”

Nico looked at him.

Tomás knew that answer.

Nico lowered his eyes.

“Yes.”

Tomás nodded.

The Dealer did not intervene.

Nico continued.

“We published the smaller version. The foundation collapsed. Three people were charged. For a few weeks, they turned me into some kind of hero.”

Marcel looked at the clipping.

“And you let them.”

“Yes.”

“You gave interviews.”

“Yes.”

“Accepted awards.”

“Yes.”

“Rose higher.”

Nico smiled without humor.

“Yes.”

Marcel leaned forward.

“Then what is the difference between you and Tomás?”

Tomás turned toward him.

Nico replied, “He wanted a seat.”

“And you wanted a stage.”

Nico fell silent.

The Dealer spoke.

“There is another difference.”

Marcel looked at him.

“What?”

The Dealer indicated the photograph.

“Nico believed the agreement would save Sara.”

“And it didn't?” Adrian asked.

Nico tightened his grip on the photograph.

“Three days after publication, she disappeared.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Adrian looked at her.

“You knew?”

“I found out later.”

“How?”

“I received a message.”

Nico turned.

“From whom?”

“A woman.”

“Name.”

“She didn't use one.”

“When?”

“Four days after the disappearance.”

Nico went still.

“What did it say?”

Celeste waited.

“That Sara was alive.”

Nico's chair creaked.

He leaned toward the table.

“You knew.”

“I didn't know whether it was true.”

“But you knew.”

“I received a message.”

“And you said nothing.”

“No.”

“For seventeen years.”

“Yes.”

Nico moved closer to her.

“And you called it protection.”

Celeste did not look away.

“Yes.”

The word struck her as hard as it struck him.

Nico returned to his place.

His hand trembled.

He put it under the table.

Too late.

Everyone had seen.

“Is she alive?” he asked.

The Dealer replied, “I don't know.”

Nico looked at him.

“Is that true?”

“Yes.”

“Do you know what happened?”

“I know part of it.”

“Which part?”

“Her disappearance was arranged.”

“By whom?”

“I don't know.”

“A lie.”

“No.”

“Then how do you know?”

The Dealer looked at Celeste.

She understood before the others.

So did Nico.

“The prosecutor.”

Celeste lowered her eyes.

Adrian frowned.

“The woman in the transcript?”

Nico turned to her.

“Did she send the message?”

“No.”

“But she helped.”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“She created documents. An identity. A route out of the country.”

Nico went still.

“For Sara?”

Celeste took a breath.

“I don't know.”

He gave a bitter laugh.

“Everyone knows parts.”

The Dealer replied, “That is how the system protects itself.”

“By dividing the truth.”

“Yes.”

“So no one can destroy it alone.”

“Yes.”

Nico pointed to the envelope.

“And this is a part too.”

“Yes.”

“What's missing?”

“What happened afterward.”

“Who knows?”

The Dealer did not answer.

Nico stood.

Samuel moved slightly away from the wall.

Nico saw.

“I'm not going to do anything.”

Samuel remained alert.

“Not yet.”

Nico looked at him.

“Do you know something?”

“Yes.”

“What?”

Samuel hesitated.

The Dealer did not stop him.

“Sara entered this house.”

The room went still.

Celeste raised her eyes.

Marcel stopped breathing for an instant.

Tomás looked around.

Adrian leaned forward.

Nico did not move.

“When?”

“Six months ago.”

“Why?”

“To speak with the owner.”

Nico turned to the Dealer.

“You said the house wasn't yours.”

“It isn't.”

“Then whose is it?”

The Dealer remained silent.

Nico turned back to Samuel.

“She's alive.”

“Six months ago, she was.”

The answer brought no relief.

It brought vertigo.

Nico stepped closer.

“Did you speak to her?”

“Yes.”

“Did she ask about me?”

Samuel hesitated.

Nico saw.

“Did she?”

“Yes.”

“What did you tell her?”

“The truth.”

“Which truth?”

“That you were alive.”

Nico went still.

“Only that?”

“No.”

Samuel looked at Celeste.

Then at the Dealer.

Finally, at Nico.

“That you still believed she was dead.”

Nico closed his eyes.

For several seconds, no one spoke.

When he opened them, he looked at Celeste.

“Why didn't you tell me?”

“Sara asked me not to.”

“You saw her?”

“No.”

“Then how do you know?”

“The message came through the prosecutor.”

Nico rubbed his face.

“She asked you to let me go on believing she was dead.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Celeste did not answer.

The Dealer spoke.

“Because she still did not trust you.”

The sentence struck with precision.

Nico looked at him.

“After seventeen years?”

“Especially after seventeen years.”

Marcel released a quiet breath.

Nico turned.

“Say it.”

“What?”

“The line you're saving.”

Marcel smiled.

“Perhaps she knew you had a price.”

Nico stepped closer.

Samuel moved.

Nico stopped.

He looked at Marcel.

“She knew.”

Marcel's smile weakened.

“I told her before publication.”

Adrian spoke.

“She knew you accepted the money?”

“Yes.”

“And she agreed?”

Nico closed his eyes.

“No.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“What happened?”

“We argued.”

Nico sat again.

“She wanted to publish everything. I told her they would kill her.”

“And she?”

“She said that perhaps some truths only existed if someone was willing to die for them.”

Tomás lowered his eyes.

Nico studied the photograph.

“I told her it was a stupid thing to say.”

Adrian asked, “Was it?”

Nico waited.

“I still think it was.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“Do you want me to stop?”

Nico gave a small laugh.

“No.”

“If you say yes, the dossier is released.”

“I know.”

“If you continue, it remains on the table.”

“I know.”

“Then choose.”

Nico looked at the transfers.

The clipping.

The photograph.

“Continue.”

Marcel leaned back.

“Of course.”

Nico looked at him.

“It isn't to protect my reputation.”

“No?”

“No.”

“Then why?”

Nico ran his thumb along the photograph's edge.

“Because her name is in here.”

Celeste watched him.

“If this is released without context, the people who helped her could be exposed.”

The Dealer replied, “True.”

Tomás looked at him.

“Then no dossier concerns only the person who receives it.”

“No.”

“Not even mine?”

“No.”

Tomás looked at his closed envelope.

Adrian did the same.

Celeste gripped hers.

Marcel studied the pages beside his plate.

Nico exhaled.

“That is the real rule.”

The Dealer did not answer.

“We aren't deciding only what happens to us.”

“No.”

“Every confession drags in someone who isn't sitting here.”

“Yes.”

Nico smiled without humor.

“Finally, a table that resembles the world.”

The Dealer asked, “Do you want to continue?”

“Yes.”

“Even knowing someone else will decide the fate of the archive?”

Nico looked at the nameless chair.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because I spent seventeen years choosing the version of the truth I could bear.”

He placed the photograph inside the envelope.

“I won't decide alone again.”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“Continue.”

Nico picked up the spoon.

The dessert was beginning to lose its shape. White cream spread across the plate.

He tasted it.

He grimaced.

“It's too sweet.”

Samuel replied, “It was prepared that way.”

Nico looked at him.

“For me?”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Samuel kept his face still.

“Because sugar hides bitterness for a few seconds.”

Nico gave a quiet laugh.

“Finally someone admits the metaphor.”

Samuel approached to clear the plates.

When he reached him, he looked at the nearly untouched dessert.

“Are you finished, sir?”

Nico looked at the broken chocolate.

“No.”

He ate another spoonful.

Then another.

Not because he was hungry.

Because the plate was his.

When he finished, he put down the spoon.

Samuel removed the china.

The blue envelope remained over the card.

The Dealer picked up the deck.

“One thing remains.”

Nico looked at the card.

“I assumed as much.”

“Turn it over.”

Nico ran his fingers along the edge.

He did not lift it.

“Did Sara put it here?”

“No.”

Nico looked at Samuel.

“You did.”

“Yes.”

“On his orders?”

“No.”

The Dealer watched Samuel.

He did not look surprised.

Only attentive.

Nico rested his arms on the table.

“Then why?”

Samuel replied, “Because everyone else arrived knowing the character they were playing.”

Nico looked at Marcel.

Celeste.

Adrian.

Tomás.

“And me?”

“You arrived convinced you weren't playing one.”

Nico smiled.

“Fair.”

“No.”

Samuel stepped closer.

“Only true.”

Nico placed two fingers on the card.

All evening, he had provoked the others into opening envelopes.

Now no one pressured him.

He turned over the card.

The Joker smiled up at him.

Red and black.

Bells on his cap.

A painted tear beneath his left eye.

At the bottom, a single word.

**JOKER**

Tomás exhaled.

Marcel smiled.

“Predictable.”

Nico studied the card.

“No.”

Marcel tilted his head.

“No?”

Nico ran his thumb over the figure.

“A Joker has no fixed value.”

The Dealer replied, “Yes.”

“It can be removed.”

“Yes.”

“Or replace any card.”

“Yes.”

Nico placed it in front of the envelope.

“Then it isn't an identity.”

“No.”

“What is it?”

The Dealer looked at him.

“A possibility.”

Nico fell silent.

Samuel collected the final plate.

The side doors closed.

For the first time since dinner began, another course did not immediately arrive.

The table stood empty except for glasses, envelopes, cards, and fragments of wax.

The Dealer set down the deck.

“Dessert is over.”

Marcel looked at the nameless chair.

“Then it's time for coffee.”

“Yes.”

Celeste watched Samuel.

So did Nico.

Samuel put his hands behind his back.

He untied the apron.

Folded it carefully and placed it on the sideboard.

Beneath it, he wore a white shirt and dark waistcoat.

Without the apron, he looked older.

And familiar.

Marcel recognized him first.

The color drained from his face.

“No.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Good evening, Marcel.”

Celeste stood.

Adrian looked between the two men.

Tomás pushed back in his chair.

Nico slipped the Joker into his pocket.

The Dealer placed his hands on the table.

“Serve the coffee.”

Samuel picked up the box containing Artur's watch.

He walked to the nameless chair.

Placed the box before him.

And sat down.

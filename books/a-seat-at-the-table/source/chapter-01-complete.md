# CHAPTER 1

At seven forty-three, Samuel removed a knife from the table.

He held it for several seconds.

The table had eight chairs, three along each side and one at either end. Six places had been set for dinner. At the head of the table stood a plate, a glass, and a deck of cards still inside its box. At the opposite end, there was no china.

Only a single card lying face down.

An extra set of silverware rested on the sideboard, reserved for coffee. The knife was not supposed to be there.

Nor was it supposed to be in his hands.

Samuel ran his thumb along the seam between the blade and the silver handle. The metal had been polished until it reflected the room's amber light and part of the face of the man holding it.

A face none of the guests was meant to recognize.

He opened the bottom drawer of the sideboard and placed the knife inside.

He did not lock it.

He still did not know whether he was hiding it from the guests or from himself.

He returned to the table.

The napkins had been folded into narrow rectangles. The glasses stood in perfect lines. The distance between the plates had been measured twice. Not because Samuel cared about etiquette, but because his instructions had been precise, and in that house even the smallest alteration seemed capable of being interpreted as a choice.

He approached the empty chair at the end of the table.

The card was still face down.

The phone in his waistcoat pocket vibrated.

A message from an unknown sender.

**Do not turn over the card.**

Samuel looked up.

A small black camera sat above the doorframe. He had noticed it when he arrived four hours earlier. He had not known whether it was on.

Now he knew.

He put away his phone.

Rain struck the windows with an almost soothing regularity. The house stood far from the road, concealed by old walls and trees that had grown too tall for the garden. Samuel had first seen it six months earlier, on a morning beneath a clear sky.

It had looked abandoned then.

Now every room on the main floor was lit.

The kitchen operated on the lower level. No sound reached the dining room. The dishes would rise in a service elevator, already plated, at times scheduled to the minute. Samuel only had to carry them to the table.

He had not been given names.

Only figures.

King.

Queen.

Ace.

Jack.

Joker.

Watcher.

Dealer.

The word assigned to Samuel had appeared on a card hidden in his apron pocket.

**WATCHER**

Observer.

Not a guest.

Not a participant.

The kind of word that pretended to describe a role when it was really giving an order.

At seven fifty-one, he heard the first car.

Samuel straightened a glass that was already straight.

He walked into the foyer.

The doorbell rang.

When he opened the door, Marcel Voss was standing on the other side.

He wore a black overcoat over a dark suit. Rain glistened on his shoulders. Behind him, a driver held an umbrella and waited beside a long car with its engine running.

Marcel looked at Samuel.

He did not recognize him.

Samuel had prepared himself for that moment for years. He had imagined surprise. Fear. Perhaps a word spoken before Marcel could conceal it.

He had not imagined indifference.

“Good evening, sir.”

Marcel stepped inside.

Samuel closed the door.

“My phone stopped working after we passed the gate.”

“Yes, sir.”

“Why?”

“I don't know.”

Marcel turned toward him.

His gaze traveled over Samuel's waistcoat, apron, and face. It paused only long enough to conclude that Samuel did not matter.

“Do you work here?”

“Tonight.”

“For whom?”

“The host.”

Marcel looked around.

Old photographs covered the foyer wall. They showed no people, only the house across different decades. Smaller trees. Open windows. A garden that no longer existed.

“Who owns the property?”

“I don't know, sir.”

Marcel exhaled through his nose.

“Naturally.”

He removed his coat and held it out.

When Samuel took the fabric, he recognized the fragrance.

Dry wood.

Tobacco.

A note of citrus.

The same scent that had lingered in the corridor after Marcel closed the door.

For twenty years, Samuel had feared forgetting his brother's voice.

It had never occurred to him that the body could preserve smells with such fidelity.

Marcel entered the dining room.

He stopped before the table.

Beside each plate lay a small dark-blue card. There were no names. Only figures stamped in gold.

Marcel picked up the nearest card.

The King.

He turned it over.

The back was blank.

“Is this mine?”

“Yes, sir.”

“How do you know?”

“I was given the seating order.”

Marcel looked toward the chair at the opposite end.

“And that seat?”

“I don't know.”

“You don't know much.”

“No, sir.”

The beginning of a smile appeared on Marcel's face.

“That may prove useful.”

He sat down.

Samuel returned to the foyer with the coat.

He placed it inside a closet, apart from the other hangers.

He did not know why.

Perhaps to avoid touching it again.

At eight oh-two, Celeste Voss arrived.

She stepped out of the car beneath a transparent umbrella. Under her coat she wore a dark-green dress, and she held a small handbag close to her body.

Before entering, she looked at the facade.

Not with curiosity.

With recognition.

“Good evening,” Samuel said.

Celeste raised her eyes to him.

For an instant, Samuel thought she recognized him.

The expression vanished before it could fully take shape.

“Good evening.”

She handed him her coat.

In the foyer, she approached the oldest photograph of the house. It showed the facade before the north wing had been built.

“This place was closed for years.”

“I don't know, ma'am.”

“Who reopened it?”

“I don't know.”

Celeste looked at him.

“Did you start today?”

“This afternoon.”

“And they hired you without telling you who owned the property?”

“Yes, ma'am.”

She studied his face.

“Have we met?”

Samuel felt the presence of the wooden box inside the dining room sideboard, only a few yards away.

“No, ma'am.”

Celeste remained silent for another second.

Then she entered the dining room.

Marcel had already opened a bottle of water.

“You're early,” she said.

“Someone had to be.”

Celeste found the Queen at the place opposite her husband.

“How subtle.”

She sat down.

Marcel gestured toward the head of the table.

“The host hasn't arrived yet.”

Celeste looked at the deck.

“And the other chair?”

“He doesn't know.”

Samuel stood by the door.

Celeste turned to him.

“Whose house is this?”

“I don't know, ma'am.”

Marcel smiled.

“You see?”

Celeste placed her handbag beside her plate.

“He may be the only sensible person here tonight.”

Samuel noticed that she was still looking at the face-down card.

“Isn't there a place for the host?” she asked.

Marcel pointed toward the head of the table.

“There is.”

“Then who is that one for?”

Samuel withdrew before he had to answer.

In the foyer, his phone vibrated again.

**Switch the Ace and Jack cards.**

Samuel stood still.

The original arrangement had placed the Ace beside Marcel and the Jack beside Celeste. He did not know who would arrive first or which of the two places mattered more.

He returned to the dining room.

Celeste watched him approach the table.

Samuel switched the cards.

“What are you doing?” she asked.

“Correcting the seating.”

“Who made the mistake?”

Samuel aligned the Ace with the plate.

“I did.”

“Are you certain?”

“Yes, ma'am.”

Celeste did not believe him.

But she did not press him.

At eight eleven, Adrian arrived alone.

He wore a blue suit without a tie, and his hair was still damp from the rain. He embraced his mother and shook his father's hand.

The gesture between the two men was brief and too formal to look familial.

Samuel directed him to the Ace.

Adrian picked up the card.

“A little obvious.”

Marcel replied, “Perhaps it's simply accurate.”

Adrian sat beside his mother.

The switch had placed him far from his father.

Samuel still did not understand why.

At eight seventeen, Tomás Sousa arrived.

He entered the foyer speaking on his phone.

“Yes, I'm listening.”

He stopped.

Looked at the screen.

The call had ended.

“I lost the signal.”

Samuel took his coat.

“Phones don't work inside the property.”

Tomás looked up.

“Was that in the invitation?”

“I don't know, sir.”

Tomás smiled.

“They must have hired all of you from the same school.”

He entered the dining room.

He recognized Marcel before looking at anyone else.

His face changed. A small movement, too quick to seem conscious.

“Mr. Voss.”

Marcel did not stand.

“Tomás.”

He said the name as if recognizing a signature on an old document.

Tomás greeted Celeste and Adrian. Then he found the Jack on the opposite side of the table.

He sat down.

Samuel served water.

Tomás looked at the empty chair.

“Are we expecting someone else?”

Marcel replied, “It appears so.”

Adrian counted the glasses.

“Six places have been set.”

Celeste looked at Samuel.

“And him?”

Marcel answered before Samuel could.

“He isn't a guest.”

Samuel finished pouring without letting the bottle touch the glass.

At eight twenty-four, he heard another car.

Nico Saint entered the house laughing to himself.

His pale coat was soaked across the shoulders. In one hand he held a small box of matches.

Samuel looked at it.

“Where did you find that?”

“By the entrance.”

Nico showed him the box.

It had no brand. Only the image of an empty chair printed in blue.

“A souvenir.”

“No one was at the entrance.”

Nico slipped the matches into his pocket.

“That answer is considerably more interesting than it should be.”

He handed over his coat and entered the dining room.

Marcel did not try to hide his displeasure.

“You.”

Nico opened his arms.

“It's nice to be recognized.”

Celeste kept her eyes on him for a moment.

Nico noticed.

“Celeste.”

“Nico.”

“It's been a long time.”

“Not long enough.”

“That could mean two things.”

“Choose the one that hurts less.”

Nico searched for his place.

Five cards were visible.

The sixth was not.

He found the chair between Marcel and Tomás.

“It seems I've been forgotten.”

The phone in Samuel's apron pocket vibrated.

He did not need to read the message.

He went to the sideboard, took out a blue card, and placed it face down before Nico.

Marcel watched.

“The rest of us got figures.”

Nico touched the edge of the card.

“Perhaps I got instructions.”

“Aren't you going to look?”

Nico looked at Samuel.

“May I?”

“Later.”

“Did the host say that?”

Samuel thought of the woman who had been in the house six months earlier.

The way she folded napkins into perfect triangles.

The small scar near her jaw.

The question she had asked while washing her hands in the kitchen.

“He'll notice,” Samuel had said.

“Who?”

“Nico.”

The woman had looked at the napkin.

“He notices everything he later pretends not to have seen.”

Samuel returned to the present.

“Those were my instructions.”

Nico drew back his hand.

“Then I shall wait obediently. It's one of my best-known qualities.”

Samuel poured his water.

As he tilted the bottle, he caught a faint scent of orange blossom.

It came from his own hands.

Or the napkin.

Perhaps from someone who had been in the room before the guests arrived.

Nico was watching him.

“Everything all right?”

“Yes, sir.”

Samuel stepped away.

The Dealer had not yet arrived.

Even so, someone was already controlling the house.

At eight thirty, the side doors opened.

The service elevator delivered the first course.

Six small bowls.

Samuel carried them to the table.

A clear mushroom broth, a film of green oil, and a single dark leaf at the center.

He placed a bowl before each guest.

The sixth went to the head of the table.

Marcel looked at it.

“He's coming.”

“Were you told?” Celeste asked.

“Yes, ma'am.”

“By whom?”

Samuel did not answer.

The lights flickered.

Once.

Then again.

Nico looked at the ceiling.

“If this is immersive theater, I'd like to state for the record that I never signed a release.”

Tomás smiled.

He did not quite laugh.

In the distance, the front door opened.

No one had rung the bell.

Footsteps crossed the foyer.

Slow.

Regular.

The man entered the dining room without removing his coat.

He was tall and thin, perhaps a little over sixty. His gray hair was combed back, and he wore dark leather gloves.

He did not apologize for being late.

He greeted no one.

He walked to the head of the table.

He removed his gloves and placed them beside the deck.

Only then did he look at the guests.

“Good evening.”

Marcel leaned back.

“Are you the host?”

“Tonight.”

“And the owner?”

The man looked at the empty chair.

“No.”

Celeste watched him.

“What should we call you?”

He rested two fingers on the card box.

“Dealer.”

Nico gave a quiet laugh.

“Of course.”

The Dealer sat down.

Samuel poured his water.

As he drew near, he spoke so softly that only the Dealer could hear.

“The knife has been removed.”

The Dealer did not look up.

“By order?”

“No.”

“Then why?”

Samuel looked at Marcel.

“A precaution.”

“Against whom?”

Samuel did not answer.

The Dealer understood.

“Keep it.”

Samuel returned to the wall.

The man's gaze moved across the places.

King.

Queen.

Ace.

Jack.

Nico's hidden card.

The empty chair.

“Before we begin,” he said, “there are three rules.”

Marcel exhaled.

“Naturally.”

The Dealer ignored him.

“First: each course will be accompanied by a dossier.”

Adrian looked at his bowl.

There was no envelope yet.

“Second: any guest may refuse to open their own dossier.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“And what happens then?”

“Its contents are immediately released to the people and institutions listed inside.”

The room went still.

Nico's smile disappeared.

“And if we open them?” Celeste asked.

“The dossier remains in this house until dinner is over.”

Marcel looked at the empty chair.

“And after that?”

“The fate of the archive will be decided.”

“By whom?”

“Not yet.”

Marcel smiled without humor.

“That isn't an answer.”

“It is the answer currently available.”

Tomás pointed toward the nameless chair.

“And the third rule?”

The Dealer removed the deck from its box.

“No dossier belongs only to the person whose name appears on its cover.”

Celeste lowered her eyes.

Adrian noticed.

Marcel asked, “What does that mean?”

“That every revelation will have consequences for people who are not at this table.”

Samuel thought of his daughter.

His brother.

The woman who smelled of orange blossom and controlled a house without appearing in any of its photographs.

The Dealer began to shuffle.

“You may leave the table at any time.”

Tomás looked at the door.

“And leave the property?”

“No.”

“Then we can't leave.”

“You may leave the table.”

“What's the difference?”

The Dealer set down the cards.

“You will find out.”

Nico picked up his spoon.

“I don't care for rules written in the future tense.”

Marcel did not touch the soup.

“And if no one takes part?”

The Dealer looked toward the empty chair.

“At dawn, the archive will no longer be under my control.”

The first sign of genuine interest appeared on Marcel's face.

Celeste saw it too.

“And who gets it?” she asked.

The Dealer picked up the deck again.

“That is why we are having dinner.”

For several seconds, they heard only the rain.

Samuel opened the sideboard.

He removed an envelope sealed with black wax.

The first.

On the cover was the figure of a Jack.

Tomás stopped breathing.

Samuel placed the envelope beside his bowl.

“Why me?” he asked.

The Dealer shuffled once.

He did not raise his voice.

“Because you were the first to accept the invitation.”

Tomás looked at the seal.

“Does that matter?”

“Everything looks small until we understand the order.”

Nico tasted the soup.

He grimaced.

“Mushrooms.”

No one looked at him.

Samuel returned to the wall.

Tomás ran his thumb over the wax.

“What's in here?”

The Dealer set down the deck.

“The reason you are sitting at this table.”

Tomás broke the seal.

# CHAPTER 7

Samuel sat in the nameless chair.

For several seconds, no one spoke.

That end of the table had dominated the entire evening without needing to be occupied. Now, with a body before it, it looked less like a seat of power than a place of witness.

Marcel remained motionless.

The color had left his face, but his posture remained straight, as though his body still believed it could deny what his eyes had already recognized.

Celeste was the first to say the name.

“Samuel.”

He looked at her.

“Good evening, Celeste.”

Adrian looked between them.

“You know him?”

Samuel placed his hands on the table.

They were the same hands that had poured wine, aligned silverware, and removed plates. Hands they had all watched for hours without considering that they belonged to someone.

Nico remained silent.

Tomás studied Samuel's face, searching for a resemblance he had overlooked.

Marcel finally spoke.

“You're dead.”

Samuel looked at him.

“No.”

The answer was simple.

Marcel shook his head.

“I saw the report.”

“You saw the report they gave you.”

“It was confirmed.”

“By people who worked for you.”

“You took money.”

Samuel did not react.

“You did,” Marcel repeated.

“Yes.”

“Then you agreed to disappear.”

“I bought time.”

“That's an elegant way to put it.”

Samuel inclined his head slightly.

“Writing that I had died was elegant.”

Adrian looked at his mother.

“Who is he?”

Celeste did not answer.

Samuel did.

“My name is Samuel Leal.”

The surname changed the room.

Tomás let out a breath.

Nico looked at the wooden box before him.

Adrian went still.

“Artur Leal.”

“He was my brother.”

Marcel pressed his fingers against the table.

Adrian looked at his father.

“You knew he had a brother?”

Marcel did not answer.

Samuel continued.

“He did.”

“And what happened to you?”

“I was removed.”

Tomás frowned.

“Removed how?”

“From the company. From the records. From the lives of the people who knew me.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“You were paid.”

“I was threatened.”

“You received money.”

Samuel looked at him.

“So did you.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

Samuel turned to Adrian.

“After my brother's death, they told me it had been an accident. A fall. A door closed too soon. An ambulance called too late.”

Celeste lowered her eyes.

“I didn't believe them.”

Marcel replied, “Because you needed someone to blame.”

Samuel placed one hand on the box.

“Because I knew my brother.”

“That isn't evidence.”

“No.”

Samuel opened the box.

He removed Artur's broken watch.

Placed it in the center of the table.

“So I looked for evidence.”

The Dealer watched from the opposite end.

Samuel did not look at him.

“I began with the emergency report. Then the corridor footage. Then the security guard's records.”

Adrian looked at his father.

“What security guard?”

Marcel replied, “I don't know.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Luís Ferreira found Artur alive.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Marcel went still.

Samuel continued.

“He called emergency services at ten twenty-six.”

Adrian turned to his mother.

“The call you canceled.”

Celeste nodded.

“Yes.”

The word came quietly.

Samuel watched her.

“It was canceled three minutes later.”

Tomás asked, “From her phone?”

“Yes.”

Celeste held the envelope against her body.

“I believed Artur was already dead.”

“Because Marcel told you.”

She did not deny it.

Marcel leaned forward.

“We didn't know how serious it was.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Artur spoke.”

Marcel went still.

“He called for you.”

Nico watched his face.

“He asked for help.”

Marcel did not answer.

“You knew.”

“No.”

“You knew.”

“No.”

“You heard the recording.”

Marcel stood.

“We've already seen the recording.”

“You saw part of it.”

The Dealer spoke for the first time since Samuel sat down.

“The complete audio was recovered later.”

Samuel turned to him.

“By you.”

“Yes.”

Samuel released a short breath.

It did not become a laugh.

“You need to understand something.”

He looked around the table.

“The Dealer did not stumble upon the truth.”

Nico leaned in.

“Then?”

Samuel looked at the man at the head of the table.

“He bought it.”

Tomás asked, “The recording?”

“Yes.”

“From whom?”

“The security guard.”

Marcel exhaled.

“Of course.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Luís tried to sell the copy because he was afraid.”

“Because he wanted money.”

“Because he had a daughter.”

Marcel fell silent.

Samuel turned to the Dealer.

“How much did you pay?”

“Fifty thousand.”

“And what did you do with the recording?”

“Kept it.”

“For twenty years.”

“Yes.”

“Without giving it to the police.”

“Yes.”

“Without looking for me.”

“Yes.”

“Without telling anyone Artur was alive when the door was closed.”

“Yes.”

Nico watched the Dealer.

“Why?”

Samuel had asked the question, but everyone waited for the answer.

The Dealer looked at Marcel.

“Because the recording had value.”

Tomás gave a bitter laugh.

“It always ends there.”

The Dealer did not react.

Samuel spoke.

“Value for what?”

“Negotiation.”

“With whom?”

“Marcel.”

Marcel went still.

Celeste looked at her husband.

Adrian took a deep breath.

Samuel asked, “You used my brother's death to control him?”

“Yes.”

Marcel answered before anyone could continue.

“He tried to blackmail me.”

The Dealer corrected him.

“I did not try.”

“You played part of the audio.”

“Yes.”

“You demanded access.”

“Yes.”

“That is blackmail.”

“Yes.”

The admission left Marcel with no reply.

Nico touched the Joker inside his pocket.

“That's how you made yourself indispensable.”

The Dealer looked at him.

“In part.”

Samuel closed his eyes.

“Artur died, and each of you found a way to profit from what remained.”

Celeste raised her head.

“I didn't profit.”

Samuel turned to her.

“You protected the family.”

“Yes.”

“Preserved your position.”

She did not answer.

Samuel looked at Marcel.

“You preserved the company.”

Then at the Dealer.

“You bought influence.”

Finally, he looked at his own hands.

“And I preserved my anger.”

No one spoke.

Samuel picked up the cup of coffee before him.

The liquid was dark and untouched.

“I spent twenty years imagining this table.”

Marcel leaned back.

“How dramatic.”

Samuel did not react.

“In some versions, you confessed. In others, you asked for forgiveness.”

“And in all of them, you were the hero?”

“No.”

The answer seemed to surprise him.

Samuel continued.

“In most of them, I killed you.”

Celeste went still.

Adrian looked at Samuel.

Tomás glanced toward the empty space between the silverware.

Nico understood before the others.

Samuel reached inside his waistcoat.

He removed the steak knife.

Placed it on the table.

The blade reflected the candlelight.

Marcel did not move.

“So that was why.”

Samuel looked at the knife.

“Yes.”

Celeste spoke.

“You removed it so you could use it.”

Samuel took his time before answering.

“I removed it because I knew that if it remained in front of me all evening, I might use it.”

Nico watched him.

“So you hid it to keep yourself from attacking Marcel.”

Samuel looked at him.

“I hid it to give myself a chance not to.”

Marcel laughed.

“How noble.”

Samuel took the handle.

Celeste stood.

So did Adrian.

Tomás pushed back his chair.

The Dealer remained still.

Samuel raised the knife.

Marcel opened his arms.

“Do it.”

“Marcel,” Celeste said.

He ignored her.

“This is why you came. Twenty years. A dead brother. A threatened daughter.”

Samuel breathed slowly.

Marcel continued.

“Do it.”

Nico spoke.

“Don't give him that.”

Samuel looked at him.

“What?”

“The only ending he can understand.”

Marcel laughed.

“He's afraid.”

Nico did not look away from Samuel.

“If you kill him, Marcel ends up on the floor and you remain standing with a knife.”

Samuel tightened his grip.

“Every truth that came before will be used to explain your crime.”

Marcel stopped smiling.

Samuel looked at the blade.

“And if I don't?”

“He leaves here knowing he failed to turn you into a version of himself.”

Samuel looked at Marcel.

Then at the broken watch.

His hand trembled.

He turned the knife around.

Placed it on the table with the handle facing Marcel.

“I spent twenty years thinking I wanted you dead.”

Marcel studied the blade.

“The truth is, I wanted my brother alive.”

The silence was absolute.

Samuel pushed the knife to the center of the table.

“Nothing I do to you can give me that back.”

Marcel looked at him.

“Then what is tonight for?”

Samuel took a deep breath.

“To stop living in that corridor.”

The Dealer placed a hand on the deck.

“Are you finished?”

Samuel turned.

“No.”

For the first time, the answer sounded neither obedient nor defiant.

It sounded like a decision.

He opened Artur's box.

Removed several documents.

Placed them on the table.

“There's more.”

Celeste studied the pages.

“What?”

“Artur wasn't investigating only the Voss Group.”

Marcel went still.

Nico leaned forward.

“He discovered the companies were part of a larger network.”

Tomás looked at the Dealer.

“The same network as the foundation.”

“Yes.”

Adrian watched Marcel.

“You knew?”

Marcel did not answer.

Samuel answered for him.

“He knew there was something above them.”

“But he didn't know who.”

“Not completely.”

Samuel looked at the Dealer.

“Artur used one word in his notes.”

He removed a page.

At its center was a diagram of companies, accounts, and connections.

At one end, a single word:

**DEALER**

Tomás exhaled.

Adrian looked at the man at the head of the table.

Celeste went still.

Nico smiled without humor.

“So the title already existed.”

Samuel nodded.

“Artur didn't know the name. Only the role.”

Marcel spoke.

“That doesn't prove it's him.”

The Dealer replied, “It is.”

The admission removed all movement from the room.

Samuel looked at him.

“Say it again.”

“It is me.”

“You organized the connections.”

“Some.”

“The companies.”

“Some.”

“The payments.”

“Some.”

“The threats.”

The Dealer waited a second.

“Yes.”

Samuel tightened his grip on the page.

“Photographs taken outside my daughter's school?”

The Dealer held his gaze.

“Yes.”

Samuel's hand closed.

The knife remained in the center of the table.

He did not touch it.

“Did Marcel ask for that?”

“He asked for protection.”

“And you decided that meant threatening a child.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because it would work.”

Samuel released a short breath.

“And it did.”

“Yes.”

“I disappeared.”

“Yes.”

“Artur was denied justice.”

“Yes.”

“And you gained access to his empire.”

The Dealer looked at Marcel.

“Yes.”

Samuel remained silent.

Then he set down the diagram.

“This table wasn't prepared only to reveal what we have done.”

Nico watched the Dealer.

Samuel continued.

“It was prepared because the system is at risk.”

The Dealer did not answer.

Marcel straightened.

“What risk?”

Samuel looked at the chair in which he sat.

“Someone found the archive.”

Tomás frowned.

“What archive?”

“The complete archive.”

Celeste looked at the envelopes.

“The dossiers?”

“More than that.”

Samuel pointed to the diagram.

“Accounts. Intermediaries. Payments. Evidence. Names of people who don't even know they're part of it.”

Adrian went pale.

Marcel looked at the Dealer.

“Who?”

Samuel did not answer immediately.

Nico knew.

“A woman.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Nico clenched his hand inside his pocket.

“Sara.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Yes.”

The confirmation brought no relief.

Nico moved closer.

“You spoke with her six months ago.”

“Yes.”

“Does she control this house?”

“In part.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“In part.”

“The house belongs to a holding company.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“Of course.”

“Sara controls that company through third parties.”

Nico looked around.

“Then the house is hers.”

“She controls the space.”

“For what?”

Samuel looked at the Dealer.

“To keep him here.”

The Dealer remained still.

Marcel turned toward him.

“You didn't know?”

“I knew the house was available.”

“You didn't know who controlled it.”

“No.”

Nico watched his face.

For the first time, the Dealer did not seem to be hiding an answer.

He seemed to be calculating its absence.

Samuel continued.

“Sara allowed the dinner to happen because she needed everyone in the same room.”

Celeste asked, “Why?”

“Because while the Dealer controls this table, he doesn't control everything else.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“What's happening outside this house?”

Samuel looked at his brother's watch.

“I don't know.”

The Dealer spoke.

“You are going beyond what was asked of you.”

Samuel turned.

“I spent twenty years doing what I was asked.”

His voice did not rise.

“It's over.”

The Dealer watched him.

Samuel continued.

“The chair isn't a throne.”

Marcel looked at it.

“Then what is it?”

“A test.”

Celeste frowned.

Samuel placed his hands on the chair's arms.

“Whoever sits here reveals what they are willing to protect.”

Adrian looked at the envelopes.

“And decides what happens to the archive?”

“No.”

The Dealer replied, “They will have limited authority.”

Nico looked at him.

“Limited how?”

“The person may protect one dossier.”

Tomás exhaled.

“One.”

“Yes.”

“And the rest?”

“They will be decided later.”

Marcel looked at the chair.

Calculation returned to his face.

Nico saw.

“You're still counting.”

Marcel looked at him.

“No.”

“Yes.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“Coffee is not over.”

Nico turned.

“What's left?”

The Dealer looked at Samuel.

“Him.”

Samuel went still.

“I've spoken.”

“You spoke about your brother.”

“And about you.”

“You haven't spoken about what you did to reach that chair.”

The silence changed.

Samuel looked at the Dealer.

Nico watched him.

So did Celeste.

Tomás leaned forward.

Marcel smiled.

“Finally.”

Samuel tightened his hands.

The Dealer continued.

“You were not only a victim.”

“I know.”

“Then say it.”

Samuel looked at the knife.

Then at Artur's box.

Finally, at Nico.

“I helped Sara prepare tonight.”

Nico went still.

“How?”

“I switched the cards.”

Marcel looked at the chair.

“Why?”

“To keep you close to it.”

Marcel frowned.

“That benefited me.”

“Yes.”

“Then why?”

Samuel looked at the Dealer.

“Because he needed to believe he still controlled the choice.”

The Dealer remained silent.

Samuel continued.

“I removed the knife. Placed the card before Nico. Changed the order of two courses.”

Celeste asked, “On Sara's orders?”

“Some of it.”

“And the rest?”

Samuel took a deep breath.

“Mine.”

Tomás watched him.

“You manipulated the table too.”

“Yes.”

“For revenge.”

“In part.”

“And called it justice.”

Samuel closed his eyes.

“Yes.”

The Dealer removed an envelope from beneath the deck.

He placed it before Samuel.

The seal was black.

Samuel looked at the cover.

“Everyone has a dossier,” the Dealer said.

“And if I don't open it?”

“It is released.”

“Who is hurt besides me?”

The Dealer replied, “Your daughter.”

Samuel went still.

The knife remained within reach.

Nico watched him.

Samuel touched the seal.

“Then we continue.”

He broke the wax.

Inside was a single photograph.

A young woman was leaving a building with a child by the hand.

Samuel closed his eyes.

Marcel watched him.

“Now you know.”

Samuel did not answer.

The Dealer spoke.

“Your daughter believes you died.”

Adrian went still.

Tomás looked away.

Samuel tightened his grip on the photograph.

“It was the only way.”

“It was the way you chose.”

“To protect her.”

“Yes.”

Samuel raised his eyes.

“Don't use that word against me.”

“I didn't.”

The Dealer looked at Celeste.

Samuel understood.

The same word.

The same defense.

Protection.

He lowered the photograph.

“She has a daughter.”

“Yes.”

“My granddaughter.”

“Yes.”

His voice failed.

The first break of the night.

“Is she safe?”

“For now.”

“And if the dossier is released?”

“She discovers you're alive.”

Samuel closed his eyes.

“And the men who threatened her?”

“Some of them discover it too.”

Nico stepped closer.

“Then your dossier isn't only yours either.”

Samuel looked at him.

“No.”

He returned the photograph to the envelope.

The Dealer asked, “Do you want it to remain on the table?”

Samuel looked at the chair.

For years, he had wanted to be seen.

Now he understood the price of being found.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Samuel ran a hand over his brother's box.

“Because I've made enough decisions in my daughter's name.”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“Continue.”

Samuel placed the envelope beside the watch.

The coffee had gone cold.

He drank it anyway.

The bitterness seemed fair.

The Dealer picked up the deck again.

“Now you know the function of the chair.”

Marcel looked at the seat.

“Who decides who sits there?”

“All of you.”

Nico studied the table.

“We've reached the vote.”

“No.”

“What's the difference?”

“A vote counts numbers.”

The Dealer set down the deck.

“This choice will count reasons.”

The side doors opened.

No food entered.

Only a server carrying an empty tray.

At its center lay six small blank cards.

One was placed before Marcel.

Then Celeste.

Adrian.

Tomás.

Nico.

Samuel.

The Dealer spoke.

“Each of you will write one name.”

Marcel picked up the pen at once.

Nico did not touch his.

“Can we write our own name?”

“Yes.”

“Yours?”

“Yes.”

“Sara's?”

The Dealer waited a second.

“Yes.”

Nico smiled.

“Then she was never truly outside the table.”

The Dealer did not answer.

Samuel looked at the closed door.

Sara might be somewhere in that house.

She might be miles away.

Even so, the faint scent of orange blossom seemed trapped in the napkins.

Nico finally picked up the pen.

The Dealer continued.

“Do not choose who deserves power.”

He looked at the guests.

“Choose who should decide what may be protected.”

Marcel began to write.

Celeste remained motionless.

Adrian stared at the blank card.

Tomás gripped the pen.

Samuel watched the photograph of his daughter.

Nico touched the Joker in his pocket.

Then he wrote a name.

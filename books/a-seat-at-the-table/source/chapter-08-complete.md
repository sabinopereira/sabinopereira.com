# CHAPTER 8

Marcel finished first.

He folded the card in half and placed the pen on the table.

He looked at no one.

Celeste had not yet written anything.

Adrian held the tip of his pen above the paper. Tomás rolled his between his fingers. Samuel looked at the photograph of his daughter. Nico had written a name but had not folded his card.

The Dealer waited.

The rain had weakened. It no longer struck the windows with force. It ran down the glass in thin, almost silent lines.

Marcel leaned back.

“How much time do we have?”

“As much as necessary.”

“That answer is no longer useful.”

“It is.”

“Not to me.”

The Dealer looked at the folded card before him.

“You've already chosen.”

“Choosing quickly does not mean choosing badly.”

Nico tilted his head.

“Sometimes it only means the person had decided before hearing the question.”

Marcel did not turn.

“That may also be competence.”

“Of course.”

Nico touched his own card.

“Or hunger.”

Celeste finally wrote.

Without visible hesitation.

She folded the paper once and left it beside the Queen's envelope.

Adrian looked at her.

“Did you already know?”

“No.”

“Then how did you decide so quickly?”

“I didn't decide quickly.”

“It took you less than a minute.”

Celeste put down the pen.

“Some decisions take years and need only one sentence to be written.”

Adrian lowered his eyes.

Tomás still had not written.

Marcel watched him.

“Don't complicate it.”

Tomás laughed.

“Coming from you, that almost sounds tender.”

“Choose whoever can prevent the destruction.”

“Of what?”

“The company.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“He didn't ask what should be saved.”

“It's the only question that matters.”

“To you.”

Marcel looked at his blank card.

“You are in no position to lecture anyone about self-interest.”

Tomás nodded.

“That's why I'm trying to recognize it.”

The Dealer did not interrupt.

Adrian wrote.

His hand stopped halfway through the name.

Then continued.

He folded the paper.

Samuel still had not moved.

Nico looked at him.

“You don't have to write now.”

The Dealer spoke.

“He does.”

Nico turned.

“You said we had as much time as necessary.”

“He does.”

“What if the necessary time is a week?”

“It isn't.”

Samuel picked up the pen.

“It's all right.”

Nico watched him.

“You don't have to obey him.”

Samuel set the tip against the paper.

“I'm not obeying him.”

“Then whom?”

Samuel looked at the photograph.

“The person I've spent too long avoiding.”

He wrote.

Folded the card and placed it on the envelope.

Tomás was last.

He looked at Celeste.

Then at Marcel.

He wrote quickly, as though slowness might reveal more than the name.

The Dealer extended a hand.

“Nico.”

Nico kept the card before him.

“You want to collect them?”

“Yes.”

“In a particular order?”

“No.”

Nico smiled.

“A shame. I wanted to know whether placement on the tray was also a psychological assessment.”

The Dealer did not react.

Nico folded the card and handed it to him.

Samuel collected the others.

He placed them in the center of the table before the deck.

The Dealer mixed them without opening them.

Marcel watched.

“Why?”

“So the order does not appear to be a hierarchy.”

“But you will read them all.”

“Yes.”

“Then an order will exist.”

“Yes.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

Nico looked at him.

“It must be difficult living in a world where you can't even control the symbolism.”

The Dealer opened the first card.

He read the name silently.

Then looked at Marcel.

“It's yours.”

Marcel did not move.

“Read it.”

The Dealer pushed the paper toward him.

Marcel opened it.

The name was written in firm letters.

**MARCEL**

Tomás exhaled.

Adrian closed his eyes.

Celeste remained still.

The Dealer asked, “Why?”

Marcel set down the card.

“Because I am the most qualified person.”

Nico gave a quiet laugh.

“We managed to get an answer even worse than the name.”

Marcel ignored him.

“I know the network. I know the companies, the people, the risks, and the consequences.”

Celeste looked at him.

“You know what affects you.”

“I know what keeps thousands of people employed.”

Tomás spoke.

“You keep using workers as a shield.”

“They are a real consequence.”

“They were real when their names were in my report too.”

Marcel turned.

“Don't compare the two.”

“Why?”

“Because I built something.”

Tomás looked at the card.

“And that gives you the right to protect it?”

“It gives me responsibility.”

Adrian spoke.

“That isn't the same as authority.”

Marcel looked at his son.

“It is exactly the same.”

Adrian shook his head.

“That's what you've told me my entire life.”

Marcel tightened his grip on the card.

“And you benefited from it.”

“Yes.”

The answer came without defense.

“That's why I no longer accept the explanation.”

The Dealer asked, “If you sit in the chair, which dossier do you protect?”

Marcel looked at him.

“I am not required to decide now.”

“No.”

“Then I won't.”

Nico leaned forward.

“You know.”

Marcel turned.

“No.”

“You know.”

“No.”

“Your own.”

The silence confirmed it before Marcel answered.

“Naturally.”

Celeste lowered her eyes.

The Dealer collected the card.

He opened the second.

Looked at Celeste.

“It's yours.”

He pushed it toward her.

She opened it.

**SAMUEL**

Samuel went still.

Marcel released a quiet breath.

“Why?”

Celeste did not look at him.

She looked at Samuel.

“Because he spent twenty years with a reason to kill and did not kill.”

Samuel clenched his jaw.

“That doesn't make me fit.”

“No.”

Celeste kept her eyes on him.

“But it means you know the difference between wanting justice and wanting relief.”

Samuel looked at the knife in the center of the table.

“I nearly didn't.”

“Nearly counts too.”

Marcel laughed.

“You choose the man who blames you for his brother's death.”

Celeste turned.

“No.”

“Then why?”

“I choose the person before whom I cannot pretend my decisions were abstract.”

Samuel looked away.

Celeste continued.

“He knows the cost of being protected without consent.”

The word remained between them.

Protection.

Samuel looked at the photograph of his daughter.

“And if I protected my own dossier?”

“I would understand.”

“You shouldn't.”

“I didn't say I would approve.”

Samuel raised his eyes.

Celeste folded the card.

“I said I would understand.”

The Dealer collected it.

He opened the third.

Looked at Adrian.

“It's yours.”

Adrian opened the paper.

**NICO**

Nico raised his eyebrows.

“At last, institutional recognition.”

Adrian did not smile.

The Dealer asked, “Why?”

Adrian looked at Nico.

“Because he doesn't trust his own certainty.”

Marcel exhaled.

“Is that a qualification?”

“Today, it may be the only one.”

Nico ran his thumb along the edge of the table.

“You're assigning me a virtue I would probably turn into a career.”

“I know.”

“Then why?”

Adrian looked at the dossiers.

“Everyone here knows what they want to protect.”

“And I don't?”

“You do.”

Adrian leaned slightly closer.

“But you're afraid of the reason.”

Nico fell silent.

Adrian continued.

“My father believes that knowing the consequences gives him the right to decide. My mother believes that being able to endure them makes her responsible for them.”

Celeste absorbed the sentence without reacting.

“Samuel believes he should pay for what he did.”

Samuel lowered his eyes.

“Tomás is still trying to understand whether confession is another way to rise.”

Tomás pressed his lips together.

Adrian looked at Nico.

“You're the only one who seems to distrust your own story while telling it.”

Nico touched the pocket where he kept the Joker.

“That could also be cowardice.”

“It could.”

“And you still chose me.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Adrian looked at the Ace before him.

“Because I've spent my life surrounded by very certain people.”

Nico nodded slowly.

“Fair.”

The Dealer collected the card.

He opened the fourth.

Looked at Tomás.

“It's yours.”

Tomás opened it.

The name appeared in small letters.

**CELESTE**

She raised her eyes.

Marcel smiled without humor.

“Of course.”

Tomás looked at him.

“It wasn't because of you.”

“Naturally.”

The Dealer asked, “Why?”

Tomás ran his fingers over the paper.

“Because she has held power over secrets before.”

Celeste watched him.

“That's a reason not to choose me.”

“Perhaps.”

“Then?”

Tomás took a deep breath.

“She also knows what fear does to decisions.”

Nico tilted his head.

“We all do.”

Tomás shook his head.

“Not in the same way.”

He looked at Celeste.

“She spent years using information and convincing herself she could measure the damage.”

Celeste did not look away.

“She failed.”

“Yes.”

“Then why?”

“Because she knows she failed.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“What a demanding standard.”

Tomás turned.

“You still call the desire to control responsibility.”

Then he looked back at Celeste.

“She called what she did cowardice.”

Celeste went still.

Tomás continued.

“I don't know if that's enough. But it's closer to the truth than anything I've heard from you tonight.”

Marcel clenched his jaw.

The Dealer asked, “Which dossier do you believe Celeste would protect?”

Tomás looked at Adrian.

Then Marcel.

Finally, his own page.

“I don't know.”

Celeste spoke.

“You do.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“Which one?”

“You didn't choose the person you trust most.”

“No.”

“You chose the one whose fear you understand.”

Tomás fell silent.

Celeste inclined her head slightly.

“Because you hope to influence it.”

Tomás tightened his grip on the paper.

“Maybe.”

“Then your choice is still ambition.”

The sentence was not cruel.

It was exact.

Tomás waited.

“Yes.”

Celeste nodded.

“Thank you.”

He frowned.

“Why?”

“For not pretending it was trust.”

The Dealer collected the card.

He opened the fifth.

Looked at Samuel.

“It's yours.”

Samuel opened it.

**SARA**

Nico went still.

The Dealer watched him before turning back to Samuel.

“Why?”

Samuel placed the card beside the photograph of his daughter.

“Because she found the archive.”

“That doesn't make her fit.”

“No.”

“Then?”

“She understands the cost of keeping it hidden.”

Nico released a quiet breath.

“And does she understand the cost of publishing it?”

Samuel looked at him.

“I don't know.”

“Then you're choosing someone who isn't even here.”

“She is more present than she appeared.”

Nico closed his hand inside his pocket.

Samuel continued.

“Sara built part of this evening. Found the connections. Bought control of the house. Risked returning.”

“And manipulated us.”

“Yes.”

“Just like him.”

Nico indicated the Dealer.

Samuel looked toward the other end of the table.

“Yes.”

“Then what's the difference?”

Samuel waited.

“She still seems to believe the system can be dismantled.”

The Dealer spoke.

“That is not necessarily a virtue.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Not to you.”

Marcel leaned closer.

“She wants revenge.”

Samuel turned.

“That too.”

“And you chose her.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

Samuel ran his thumb over the photograph of his daughter.

“Because I know what happens when we pursue justice while pretending anger doesn't exist.”

Nico watched him.

“And if she protects her own dossier?”

“There is no dossier for her on the table.”

The Dealer replied, “There is.”

Everyone looked at him.

Nico went still.

“You said every guest had one.”

“I did not say only the guests had one.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“How many are there?”

The Dealer did not answer.

Samuel looked at Sara's card.

“Does she know?”

“Yes.”

“And is she available for this decision?”

“In part.”

The phrase irritated Nico.

“I'm beginning to hate those two words.”

The Dealer collected the card.

One remained.

He opened it.

Looked at Nico.

“It's yours.”

Nico did not ask him to hand it over.

The Dealer placed it on the table.

The name was written as a single word.

**DUARTE**

The Dealer did not react.

Marcel looked at him.

“Duarte.”

So did Celeste.

Adrian repeated the name silently.

Tomás leaned forward.

“Finally.”

Nico studied the Dealer.

“Is that your name?”

“Yes.”

“Your full name?”

“No.”

“Naturally.”

The Dealer touched the deck.

“Why me?”

Nico leaned back.

“Because you built this.”

“That isn't an answer.”

“It's the part you can hear without interrupting.”

The Dealer waited.

Nico removed the Joker from his pocket.

Placed it before him.

“If someone sits in that chair, they can protect only one dossier.”

“Yes.”

“But you have access to the rest.”

“In part.”

“You know where the copies are.”

“Some.”

“You know the names.”

“Some.”

“You know the mechanisms.”

“Yes.”

Nico leaned forward.

“Then choosing someone else to protect one sheet while you keep the map is theater.”

The room went still.

The Dealer did not answer.

Nico continued.

“Marcel wants to control the outcome. Celeste wants to prevent further harm. Adrian wants someone who doubts. Tomás wants someone he can influence. Samuel wants someone who still believes the system can be dismantled.”

He looked at all of them.

“I choose the person who should be forced to dismantle it.”

The Dealer held his gaze.

“That is not choosing who should decide.”

“It is.”

“How?”

“I choose you because no decision will be real while you can hide behind the role.”

Marcel smiled.

“And you believe he would cooperate?”

“No.”

Nico looked at Duarte.

“That's why I wouldn't give him freedom.”

“What would you do?”

“Put you in the chair.”

The Dealer did not move.

“Force you to protect one dossier in public.”

Celeste understood.

“To reveal his priority.”

Nico nodded.

“Exactly.”

Tomás looked at the Dealer.

“Which one would you choose?”

Duarte remained silent.

Nico smiled without humor.

“That is the first question tonight you truly don't want to answer.”

The Dealer collected the card.

He placed all six before the deck.

Marcel spoke.

“There is no majority.”

“It wasn't a vote,” Duarte replied.

“Then what was it for?”

“To show the division.”

Adrian looked at the names.

Marcel.

Samuel.

Nico.

Celeste.

Sara.

Duarte.

No repeats.

No consensus.

“And now?” he asked.

Duarte turned to the chair occupied by Samuel.

“Anyone may sit there.”

Samuel did not move.

“I'm already sitting.”

“The chair has not yet been activated.”

Tomás gave a quiet laugh.

“Of course it hasn't.”

Nico looked at Duarte.

“What's missing?”

“A voluntary choice.”

Samuel placed his hands on the arms of the chair.

“And if I stay?”

“You assume the role.”

“Without another decision?”

“Yes.”

Marcel stood.

“Then get out.”

Samuel looked at him.

“No.”

Marcel took a step closer.

“You weren't chosen.”

“No one was.”

“I was the only person with sufficient experience.”

Nico spoke.

“You were chosen by yourself.”

“It was a valid vote.”

“It was an autobiography.”

Marcel approached the chair.

Samuel did not stand.

Celeste watched them both.

“Marcel.”

He did not stop.

“Samuel does not understand the scope of the archive.”

Samuel looked at him.

“You do?”

“More than you.”

“Is that what you told yourself before closing the door?”

Marcel went still.

The blow required no volume.

Celeste stood.

She walked to the nameless chair.

Samuel looked at her.

“No.”

Marcel turned.

“What are you doing?”

Celeste placed a hand on the backrest.

“Making a choice.”

“You weren't chosen.”

“I was.”

Tomás lowered his eyes.

Celeste continued.

“And even if I hadn't been, he said anyone may sit.”

Marcel moved closer.

“You don't know what you'll do.”

“That's the first decent reason for it to be me.”

Samuel slowly stood.

The chair was free.

Celeste looked at him.

“Why?”

Samuel held the photograph of his daughter.

“Because if I stay, I choose her.”

“Your dossier.”

“Yes.”

“And you believe that would be wrong.”

“No.”

Samuel took a deep breath.

“But it would continue the same decision I made twenty years ago without asking her.”

Celeste understood.

Samuel stepped away.

Marcel tried to move between them.

Adrian stood.

“Dad.”

Marcel looked at him.

“No.”

Adrian placed himself between them.

He did not touch him.

“Not this time.”

Marcel went still.

Celeste sat in the nameless chair.

Nothing happened.

No light.

No sound.

The house did not react.

Tomás looked at the ceiling.

“Was something supposed to happen?”

Duarte replied, “It already did.”

Nico watched Celeste.

“What?”

“She sat knowing she can protect only one dossier.”

Celeste looked at the envelopes scattered across the table.

Her own.

Adrian's.

Tomás's.

Marcel's.

Nico's.

Samuel's.

Each contained absent people.

Careers.

Children.

The dead.

Survivors.

Lives reduced to paper.

Marcel remained standing.

“Choose mine.”

Celeste did not look at him.

“No.”

“Think of the group.”

“I am.”

“Thousands of jobs.”

“No.”

“Adrian.”

Their son closed his eyes.

Celeste continued without looking at him.

“Don't use him.”

“He's your son.”

“He's also a person.”

Marcel clenched his fists.

“Celeste.”

She placed her hands on the table.

Duarte asked, “Which dossier do you protect?”

Nico touched the Joker.

Tomás watched Celeste as though the name he had written still bound him to her.

Samuel held the photograph of his daughter.

Adrian remained between his parents.

Celeste looked across the envelopes.

She stopped at the blue one.

Nico saw.

The humor disappeared from his face.

“No.”

Celeste looked at him.

“Why?”

“Because I didn't ask you to.”

“I know.”

“Then no.”

Duarte did not intervene.

Celeste placed her hand on the blue envelope.

“I protect Nico Saint's dossier.”

The decision remained in the room.

Nico took his hand away from the Joker.

Marcel laughed.

“After everything.”

Celeste did not turn.

“Yes.”

Adrian watched her.

“Why his?”

Celeste looked at Nico.

“Because it is the only dossier whose immediate release could expose someone who is still in hiding.”

Nico clenched his jaw.

“Sara.”

“Yes.”

“You don't have the right.”

“No.”

“Then remove your hand.”

Celeste did not move.

“I can't.”

“You won't.”

“Both.”

Nico stood.

“You spent years deciding for her. Now you're deciding for me.”

“Yes.”

The answer stopped him.

Celeste continued.

“I won't pretend this is different merely because I'm doing it in front of witnesses.”

Nico looked at the envelope beneath her hand.

“Then why are you doing it?”

Celeste waited.

“Because I still believe she's alive.”

“That belongs to you.”

“Yes.”

“And you're using my dossier to protect your hope.”

Celeste received the sentence.

“Yes.”

Nico gave a small laugh.

There was no humor in it.

“At least you've learned to confess quickly.”

Duarte spoke.

“The protection has been registered.”

Tomás looked at him.

“What exactly does that mean?”

“The dossier may not be released as a complete public unit.”

Nico turned.

“As a unit?”

“Facts that exist in other documents may still be used.”

“Then it isn't protected.”

“It is limited.”

“Limited by whom?”

“By the rule.”

Nico pointed at Celeste.

“She decided to protect something she can't actually protect.”

Duarte replied, “Yes.”

Celeste looked at him.

“You knew.”

“Yes.”

“And you let us believe the chair gave us more power.”

“I did not lie.”

Nico exhaled.

“No. You only let hope do the work.”

Duarte did not answer.

Marcel looked at the chair.

“Then this doesn't control the archive.”

“No.”

“It protects only one dossier.”

“Yes.”

“And the rest?”

“Remains to be decided.”

Marcel smiled.

Calculation returned to his face.

Nico saw.

“Don't look so pleased. You've just discovered the throne is a chair.”

Marcel looked at him.

“A chair can still be occupied.”

Celeste removed her hand from the blue envelope.

“It has been.”

Duarte turned to her.

“The role requires a second decision.”

Celeste frowned.

“What?”

“Choose one person to leave the room.”

Silence fell.

Nico looked at Duarte.

“Why?”

“Because protection has a cost.”

“For whom?”

“For the person protected.”

Celeste went still.

Duarte continued.

“The person whose dossier was protected must leave before the archive is opened.”

Nico laughed.

This time, the humor returned only to break.

“Of course.”

Celeste looked at him.

“I didn't know.”

“I know.”

Nico took the Joker from his pocket.

Placed it before her.

“Keep this.”

Celeste did not touch the card.

“Nico.”

“You protected me.”

“That isn't what I did.”

“No.”

He pushed the Joker a few inches toward her.

“But that's the story you chose.”

Duarte spoke.

“You must leave.”

Nico looked at the door.

Then at Samuel.

“Is she in this house?”

Samuel replied, “I don't know.”

“But she was.”

“Yes.”

Nico turned back to Celeste.

“When you understand that you can't decide for someone simply because you're afraid to lose them, open the envelope.”

Celeste looked at the blue paper.

“If I do, the protection ends.”

“I know.”

“And it may be released.”

“I know.”

Nico smiled, tired.

“At last we're both saying the same thing.”

He took the coat Samuel brought him.

Before leaving, he returned to the table.

He collected the Joker.

Celeste watched him.

“I thought it was staying.”

Nico slipped the card into his pocket.

“So did I.”

He looked at Duarte.

“Don't confuse a door with a choice again.”

Duarte held his gaze.

Nico left the room.

His footsteps crossed the foyer.

The front door did not open.

Celeste remained seated.

Her hand rested beside the blue envelope.

For several seconds, no one spoke.

Then she pulled it toward her.

And opened it again.

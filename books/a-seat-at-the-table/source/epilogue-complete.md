# EPILOGUE

The first box left the house at 9:12 in the morning.

It was not delivered to the police.

Samuel refused.

Duarte did not argue.

Neither did Sara.

Artur's box went to a law firm specializing in whistleblower protection. Inside were the broken watch, the recording, the hallway images, the emergency-call logs, and a partial copy of the transfers Artur had been investigating before he died.

The inventory bore six signatures.

Marcel's was not among them.

Two additional copies were made.

One reached a prosecutor through an intermediary who did not know its contents.

The other was divided among three journalists so that none could publish the complete story alone.

It was an imperfect solution.

That was why they trusted it a little more.

When they left the house, sunlight was already brightening the wet road.

No car waited for them.

Marcel had taken the only driver.

Tomás looked at the open gate.

“And now?”

No one answered.

The question seemed too large for that morning.

Duarte sat on the front steps. His face was gray, his lips nearly colorless. Sara called a medical team waiting a few miles away.

He did not protest.

Perhaps he lacked the strength.

Perhaps because he no longer controlled the decision.

Nico remained by the door.

The Joker was in his pocket.

Sara came to stand beside him.

For several seconds, they did not look at each other.

Samuel was on the phone with the lawyers. Celeste and Adrian waited near the road. Tomás held his envelope tight against his chest as though someone might still tear it away.

“Are you going to disappear again?” Nico asked.

Sara looked at the trees.

“I don't know.”

“That's a terrible answer.”

“I learned from you.”

He breathed out.

“No.”

“No what?”

“Don't make this light.”

Sara nodded.

“All right.”

Nico ran a hand over his face.

He had not slept in more than twenty-four hours. His clothes clung to him, the taste of chocolate still lingered in his mouth, and a headache seemed to begin behind his eyes.

“I would have gone with you.”

Sara waited before answering.

“Back then?”

“Yes.”

“You don't know that.”

“No.”

He looked at her.

“But I would have tried.”

Sara kept her eyes on the road.

“That's what I was afraid of.”

“That I would try?”

“That you would do something terrible convinced it was for me.”

Nico exhaled.

“I did.”

“I know.”

“So did you.”

“I know.”

The ease with which they both admitted it brought no comfort.

Nico looked at his hands.

“Can we do anything with this?”

Sara turned toward him.

“With what?”

“Us.”

She was silent.

“I'm not asking you to answer now.”

“I know.”

“I'm also not saying I don't want an answer.”

“I know.”

Nico laughed, exhausted.

“You're still irritating.”

“You're also older.”

“We established that.”

Sara studied him.

“I don't want to go back to what we were.”

“Neither do I.”

“It wasn't healthy.”

“I disagree. We ate extremely well.”

Sara almost smiled.

Almost.

“I want to find out who you are now.”

The humor left his face.

“So do I.”

She touched his hand briefly.

It was not forgiveness.

It was not a promise.

It was contact.

Then she moved away to help Duarte stand.

---

Tomás honored the twenty-four hours.

With twenty-three minutes left, he walked alone into a newspaper office carrying a folder of documents and the contact information for Marta Freitas's lawyer.

He did not ask them to call her.

He did not ask where she lived.

He handed over a signed statement, the access logs, and a full account of the false complaint.

The journalist asked why he had decided to talk.

Tomás answered, “Because I got caught.”

The man waited for more.

Tomás continued.

“I'd like to say it was remorse. It wasn't. At first, it was fear.”

“And then?”

Tomás looked at the folder.

“Then I realized I'd spent years calling my fear of being insignificant ambition.”

The statement was published three days later.

Marta's name did not appear.

She refused to speak with Tomás.

Six months later, she accepted a settlement from the company, formal restoration of her license, and a public declaration of innocence.

She did not forgive him.

Tomás did not ask again.

---

Adrian recorded a second statement.

The first, made in the archive, seemed too clean.

In the new one, he used no set, music, or communications team. He placed his phone on a table, sat in front of a white wall, and spoke for twenty-seven minutes.

He explained the admission.

The internship.

The foundation.

The award.

He did not claim he had achieved everything alone.

He did not claim nothing belonged to him either.

“I don't know how to completely separate the work I did from the doors other people closed so I could pass through,” he said. “So I won't ask anyone to believe my version before hearing from the people who were left on the other side.”

He resigned from every board and executive position.

He did not establish a foundation.

He did not lead the investigation.

Over the following year, he worked with a small team to identify the candidates and employees harmed by his family's interventions.

He refused to be the spokesperson.

Some called him a coward.

Others, a repentant heir.

Many believed he was preparing a return.

Perhaps he was.

He still did not know.

---

Celeste spoke for fourteen hours.

The prosecutor listened to the first six without interrupting.

They did not meet at a police station or a courthouse. The meeting took place in an unmarked office in a building where neither woman would be recognized.

Celeste handed over the records.

She explained the codes.

She identified the eighteen uses.

When she reached the investigation she had destroyed, she closed the folder.

“I don't know how to say this without turning the confession into an attempt at forgiveness.”

The prosecutor answered, “Then don't try.”

Celeste reopened the folder.

She spoke.

At the end, the woman asked, “Why did you cancel the ambulance?”

Celeste waited.

“Because my husband said Artur was dead.”

“And you believed him?”

“I chose to believe him.”

The prosecutor nodded.

“That's different.”

“Yes.”

“Worse?”

Celeste looked at her hands.

“Yes.”

The prosecutor did not tell her whether she had survived despite Celeste or partly because of her.

She offered no such simplification.

Celeste hired a lawyer whose career she had helped destroy twelve years earlier.

The woman agreed to represent her.

She charged double.

---

Samuel wrote to his daughter on a rainy afternoon.

He began six times.

In the first version, he explained the threats.

In the second, he spoke of Artur.

In the third, he tried to justify his disappearance.

In the fourth, he asked for forgiveness.

In the fifth, he deleted everything.

In the sixth, he wrote:

**I am alive. I chose to let you believe otherwise because I was afraid. They told me you would be safe that way. Maybe you were. I don't know what I took from you in your own name without asking. I don't expect you to answer. But I will not hide from you again.**

The reply arrived nine days later.

It contained three words.

**I need time.**

Samuel printed it.

He kept it in his brother's box.

Four months later, he met his granddaughter for the first time in a public park.

His daughter did not embrace him.

She did not leave either.

They sat on a bench while the child played near a tree.

Samuel did not speak of Artur.

He did not speak of Marcel.

He asked which cartoons his granddaughter liked.

He listened to the entire answer.

---

The investigation into Artur was opened twenty years and four months after his death.

Marcel Voss was not charged with murder.

The fall had been accidental.

He was charged with failure to render aid, obstruction, fraud, coercion, and conspiracy connected to illicit financial operations.

His lawyers challenged the authenticity of the recordings.

Then the chain of custody.

Then they argued that any attempt to hold him accountable would destroy thousands of jobs.

Some politicians repeated the line.

So did some journalists.

The Voss Group lost contracts, investors, and access to credit. Several subsidiaries were sold. Others changed their names. Executives who had served Marcel for decades claimed they had never known about the internal mechanisms.

Many were telling the truth.

That did not make them innocent.

Marcel remained free while awaiting trial.

The portion of the archive he carried from the house contained contacts, containment operations, and enough information to delay proceedings.

Not enough to stop them.

For months, no one knew where he lived.

Then he was photographed leaving a restaurant alone.

He looked smaller.

The photograph was published as if that meant something.

---

Rui Mendes tried to leave the country.

He was found in a hotel near the airport with two phones, three passports, and a reservation under someone else's name.

In his first statement, he claimed he had protected Nico.

In the second, he said he had obeyed Marcel.

In the third, he blamed Duarte.

In the fourth, he explained that Sara was a threat and Nico was incapable of managing his own life.

In every version, Rui was the person who had prevented a greater harm.

When shown the message sent in Nico's name, he said he did not remember it.

Then he said it had been necessary.

Nico watched part of the interrogation through a recording.

He turned it off before the end.

He never visited Rui.

---

Duarte Vale delivered seven sequences.

One each week.

He did not give them to Sara.

Or to Nico.

Each sequence was divided among different people. Some opened nodes of the archive. Others revealed only inventories. One led to an empty server.

Duarte claimed not to know why.

No one entirely believed him.

He died before the trial.

In his room, they found one suitcase, two dark suits, medication, an empty notebook, and the box from a deck of cards.

The cards were gone.

For weeks, Sara suspected Marcel.

Samuel suspected Sara.

Nico suspected Duarte, even dead.

Celeste said perhaps they had never existed.

No one could prove anything.

---

The archive was not published in full.

Nor was it preserved as Duarte wanted.

It was divided.

Some cases reached the courts.

Others reached newspapers.

Others went to the people directly affected.

There were serious investigations.

There were quiet settlements.

There were journalists who protected sources and others who sold exclusives.

Prosecutors who moved forward.

Prosecutors who warned suspects.

Witnesses who spoke.

Witnesses who disappeared.

Innocent people were exposed.

Guilty people escaped.

The system lost strength.

It did not die.

Perhaps systems like that never died.

Perhaps they only changed addresses, vocabulary, and owners.

But it no longer existed in one place.

No one could close a door and believe the entire truth remained on the other side.

---

Sara began using her own name again.

Not publicly.

Not yet.

She worked with different teams, each with limited access to a set of cases. She did not choose alone. Nor did she pretend she had stopped influencing the choices.

In one meeting, someone asked whether she was simply building a slower version of Duarte's system.

Sara answered, “Maybe.”

The person waited for her to defend herself.

She did not.

---

Nico refused to write a book.

He refused the documentary series.

He refused three exclusive interviews and an offer to turn the dinner into a spectacle.

He agreed to record one conversation.

Uncut.

No music.

No audience.

It lasted one hour and twenty minutes.

He spoke about the foundation.

The money.

The partial version of the truth.

Sara.

Rui.

The night at the house.

He did not reveal names that might still put people at risk.

At the beginning of the recording, he explained:

“I spent many years believing that telling only part of the truth was either a lie or survival. Sometimes it was one. Sometimes the other. Most of the time, it was both.”

The interviewer asked whether he considered himself guilty.

Nico answered, “Yes.”

“Of what?”

He thought.

“Of preferring a story in which I was still the right person.”

“And now?”

“Now I prefer stories where that hasn't been decided yet.”

The conversation was published without a title.

Even so, it received millions of views.

For several weeks, they called him brave again.

Nico did not correct everyone.

He did not accept any awards either.

It was progress.

Not purity.

---

One year after the dinner, he found a blue envelope in his mailbox.

It had no return address.

It was not sealed.

Inside was a blank card.

On the reverse, written by hand, was a question:

**IF NO ONE WERE WATCHING, WHO WOULD YOU BE?**

Nico recognized the handwriting.

He took the card inside.

He placed it on the kitchen table and watched it as the afternoon lost its light.

He tried to write a joke.

Then another.

He crossed them both out.

He thought of Sara.

Of Artur.

Of Samuel removing the knife.

Of Celeste opening the envelope she had chosen to protect.

Of Adrian before the white wall.

Of Tomás admitting that his first remorse had been getting caught.

Of Duarte, who never raised his voice because he had built a world in which he did not need to.

At last, Nico wrote:

**I STILL DON'T KNOW.**

He looked at the answer.

Then added:

**BUT I THINK I AM WHAT I DO WHEN NO ONE WILL APPLAUD.**

He set down the pen.

There were no cameras.

No audience.

No one waiting for a better line.

The kitchen was dark.

Nico remained seated.

Alone.

And he did not make a joke.

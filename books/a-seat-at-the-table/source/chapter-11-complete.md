# CHAPTER 11

Duarte began with the worst-case scenario.

“If Sara distributes the archive tonight, some evidence will be preserved.”

Nico remained seated sideways between the table and the door.

“Some.”

“Yes.”

“And the rest?”

“It may lose context, chain of custody, or legal value.”

Sara crossed her arms.

“The team knows what it's doing.”

“Part of the team.”

“I chose competent people.”

“You chose people you trust.”

“Isn't that the same thing?”

“No.”

Sara pressed her lips together.

Nico looked at Duarte.

“Continue.”

Duarte let his eyes travel over the envelopes.

“Some journalists will publish quickly to prevent the material from being suppressed. Others will hold information back to negotiate exclusivity.”

Tomás lowered his eyes.

“Some lawyers will protect victims. Others will pursue settlements.”

Celeste kept her hands on the transcript.

“Prosecutors?”

“Some will act. Others will warn the people under investigation.”

Marcel let out a breath.

“Finally.”

Sara turned.

“Finally what?”

“Someone admits disclosure isn't justice.”

“I didn't say it wasn't necessary.”

“You don't need to. You just described chaos.”

Nico looked at Marcel.

“You call anything that no longer answers your phone chaos.”

Marcel did not react.

Nico turned back to Duarte.

“Who gets hurt first?”

“The people with the fewest resources.”

“Why?”

“Because they don't have legal teams, political relationships, or time to control the narrative.”

Tomás touched his envelope.

“Marta.”

“Yes.”

“She could be treated as an accomplice before anyone understands she was framed.”

“Yes.”

Adrian looked at the Ace's dossier.

“Daniel, Leonor, and Helena could be exposed.”

“Yes.”

Celeste spoke.

“The prosecutor.”

“Yes.”

Nico looked at Sara.

“And you.”

She did not answer.

Duarte did.

“Yes.”

Nico ran his thumb along the Joker.

“Best-case scenario.”

Duarte inclined his head.

“The network loses its ability to coordinate a response. People who believed they were isolated realize other evidence exists. Witnesses come forward. Companies can no longer treat each case as a separate incident.”

Samuel studied his brother's watch.

“Artur stops looking like a man who fell alone.”

“Yes.”

“And Marcel?”

Duarte looked at him.

“He loses part of his ability to control the process.”

Marcel replied, “Part.”

“Yes.”

“Not all.”

“No.”

Marcel almost smiled.

Nico saw it.

“Don't worry. We'll get to your little reserve of power.”

The smile vanished.

Nico faced Duarte.

“Now your plan. Worst-case scenario.”

Duarte did not answer immediately.

Sara exhaled.

“That part he knows well.”

Duarte looked at her.

“If the system is preserved, it continues to be used.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“By whom?”

“By whoever has access.”

“You.”

“While I'm alive.”

“After that?”

“Other people.”

“Chosen by you.”

“Yes.”

Nico leaned forward.

“To do what?”

“Prevent greater abuses. Negotiate exits. Contain crises.”

Celeste gave a small laugh.

“That's how I began.”

Duarte looked at her.

“I know.”

“No.”

Celeste tightened her grip on the Queen's envelope.

“If you knew, you wouldn't call the same mechanism containment.”

Duarte said nothing.

Nico insisted.

“Worst case.”

Duarte looked at the deck.

“The archive becomes more efficient.”

The sentence froze the room.

Adrian spoke.

“More efficient at what?”

“Influencing decisions.”

“Blackmailing people.”

“Yes.”

“Destroying them.”

“Yes.”

“And that's acceptable?”

“No.”

“But you were willing to do it.”

“Yes.”

Adrian stepped away from the table.

“Why?”

Duarte watched him.

“Because a system controlled by people who know the cost may be less dangerous than the same power dispersed among people who do not.”

Celeste shook her head.

“Everyone believes they know the cost when someone else is paying it.”

Duarte did not argue.

Nico said, “Best case.”

“Some truths remain available when they're needed. Dangerous people remain constrained. Victims can be protected without public exposure. Institutions avoid collapses that would also harm innocent people.”

Marcel nodded.

“That is responsibility.”

Nico looked at him.

“No. It's the version in which you continue to exist.”

Marcel leaned in.

“And what is your version?”

“I'm trying to find out.”

“While time runs out.”

“Yes.”

“That's irresponsibility.”

Nico smiled without humor.

“Possibly.”

Sara stepped closer.

“We know the extremes.”

“No,” Nico said. “We know the advertising for each extreme and their respective funerals.”

Sara was silent.

Nico stood and walked to the table.

The dossiers lay among empty glasses, cards, and scraps of wax. They looked smaller than the lives they contained.

“How many cases are in the archive?”

Duarte answered, “I don't know the exact number.”

“Estimate.”

“More than four hundred primary clusters.”

Tomás breathed out.

“People?”

“Thousands.”

“Connections?”

“Tens of thousands.”

Nico looked at Sara.

“And you wanted to distribute all of it.”

“Not at once.”

“But quickly.”

“Before it could be destroyed.”

“By him?”

“By anyone.”

Nico nodded.

“Then we do the opposite.”

Marcel laughed.

“Hide it.”

“No.”

“That's what you said.”

“I said we don't publish everything at once.”

Sara frowned.

“That gives the network time.”

“Yes.”

“To destroy evidence.”

“That too.”

“To threaten witnesses.”

“Yes.”

“Then it doesn't work.”

Nico looked at her.

“Publishing everything threatens witnesses too.”

Sara went still.

“There is no version without risk,” he continued. “So we stop pretending the solution is to choose the risk that makes us look better.”

Duarte watched him.

“What's the proposal?”

Nico picked up Tomás's envelope.

He did not open it.

“We open the archive case by case.”

Tomás reached toward it.

Nico let go.

“I'm not deciding for you.”

“It looked like you were.”

“Get used to correcting me.”

Tomás withdrew his hand.

“Each case is separated before disclosure. We identify who might be harmed, who needs protection, and what evidence exists elsewhere in the archive.”

Sara shook her head.

“That could take years.”

“It will.”

“Duarte is dying.”

“We don't need him alive for everything.”

Duarte spoke.

“You need me for the sequences.”

Nico turned.

“Then you start now.”

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because there is no structure.”

“We create one.”

Duarte almost smiled.

“Exactly.”

Nico understood.

“And then someone controls it.”

“Yes.”

The room fell silent.

Nico looked at them all.

“Then no one controls everything.”

Marcel exhaled.

“A sentence isn't a system.”

“No.”

Nico pointed at the dossiers.

“Each case goes to a different group. People affected by it, lawyers, journalists, and someone capable of challenging the others.”

Celeste leaned closer.

“Who chooses?”

“Not me alone.”

“Sara?”

“Not alone.”

“Duarte?”

“No.”

“Then who?”

Nico drew a breath.

“We begin with the people already connected to the case.”

Marcel laughed.

“The victims will conduct investigations?”

“Some of them.”

“That's revenge.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Sometimes.”

Marcel fell silent.

Samuel continued.

“And sometimes what you call impartiality is just enough distance not to see the blood.”

Nico nodded.

“Each group needs people who disagree with one another.”

Tomás frowned.

“A committee.”

“Don't call it that. Committees have a supernatural ability to turn urgency into furniture.”

Despite everything, Adrian smiled. Barely.

Nico continued.

“One representative of the people affected. One lawyer. One journalist or investigator. Someone with no direct connection to the case. And access only to the material they need.”

Sara watched him.

“That's still distributing the archive.”

“Yes.”

“To chosen people.”

“Yes.”

“Then how is it different from my plan?”

Nico looked at her.

“Because you had already chosen everyone.”

Sara went still.

“And because each group can say no to the others.”

Duarte spoke.

“Fragmentation.”

“Yes.”

“It creates inconsistency.”

“Yes.”

“Contradictory decisions.”

“Yes.”

“Leaks.”

“Yes.”

“Corruption.”

“Probably.”

Marcel laughed.

“Excellent system.”

Nico turned.

“It's worse than yours at maintaining control.”

“It's worse at everything.”

“No.”

Nico stepped toward him.

“It's worse at pretending one person can be good enough to decide for everyone.”

Marcel held his gaze.

Nico returned to the table.

“We're not building a perfect machine. We're making sure no single person can lock it in a room.”

Samuel looked at the broken watch.

Duarte ran his fingers over the deck.

“And when the groups disagree?”

“They disagree.”

“When one wants to publish and another doesn't?”

“They record their reasons.”

“And who decides?”

Nico was silent.

Marcel smiled.

“There it is.”

Nico looked at him.

“Yes.”

“You don't have an answer.”

“No.”

“Then you don't have a plan.”

“I have the beginning of one.”

“That isn't enough.”

Nico moved toward the head of the table.

“It's enough to keep you, her, or him from taking everything tonight.”

Sara watched him.

“And tomorrow?”

“Tomorrow we make the next decision.”

“That's all?”

“Yes.”

Sara let out a breath.

“It took us seventeen years to get here.”

“And you want to solve it in forty minutes.”

“I want to keep it from disappearing.”

“So do I.”

Nico looked at Duarte.

“How much time?”

“Thirty-one minutes.”

“Then we choose the first cases.”

Duarte did not move.

“On what basis?”

“Urgency.”

“Defined by whom?”

Nico looked around the table.

“Everyone.”

Marcel spoke.

“I won't participate in an improvised tribunal.”

Nico nodded.

“All right.”

“You have no authority to continue without me.”

“I have a chair and a voice. It's worked for less qualified people.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

Nico pointed at Artur's box.

“First case.”

Samuel went still.

“Artur.”

Duarte watched him.

“Why?”

“There's a risk evidence will be destroyed. One victim is dead. Witnesses are alive. Marcel has partial access.”

Sara nodded.

“Agreed.”

Celeste said, “Agreed.”

Adrian looked at his father.

“Yes.”

Tomás kept his eyes on the watch.

“Yes.”

Samuel waited.

“Yes.”

Everyone looked at Marcel.

He did not answer.

Nico turned to Duarte.

“Is that enough?”

“It wasn't a vote.”

“Good. Then we don't need him.”

Marcel stood.

“You will not use private documents to build a case against me.”

Samuel replied, “The case already exists.”

“Not legally.”

“Then we make it legal.”

Marcel looked at him.

Samuel did not look away.

Nico asked, “Who identifies the material?”

Samuel placed his hand on the box.

“I do.”

Celeste added, “I can provide context for the companies and the internal decisions.”

Samuel looked at her.

There was no forgiveness.

There was usefulness.

Sara spoke.

“My team can confirm the financial links.”

Nico looked at Duarte.

“And you?”

He paused.

“I can provide the first sequence.”

“Will you?”

“If we agree on the others.”

“No.”

Duarte tilted his head.

Nico continued.

“You don't trade Artur for future cooperation.”

“You need me.”

“Yes.”

“Then I have negotiating power.”

“Yes.”

Nico stepped closer.

“And every minute you use that power will be entered into your own case.”

Duarte held his gaze.

“My case.”

“Second.”

The room went still.

Sara watched Nico.

“Duarte.”

“If he refuses to cooperate.”

Duarte asked, “And if I cooperate?”

“The case still exists. Only its urgency changes.”

“Then you offer no immunity.”

“No.”

“Reduction?”

“I'm not a judge.”

“Protection?”

Nico looked at his trembling fingers.

“Medical treatment, if it depends on us. Security, if there's a risk. Nothing concerning responsibility.”

Duarte was silent.

“Second case,” Nico repeated. “Duarte Vale.”

Celeste raised her eyes.

“Vale.”

Duarte looked at her.

“Yes.”

Marcel leaned back.

“Finally, a last name.”

Nico ignored him.

“Third: Rui Mendes.”

Sara nodded at once.

“Yes.”

Celeste looked at the messages.

“There's a current risk to Nico and Sara.”

Tomás added, “He could also destroy evidence.”

“Yes.”

Adrian asked, “And after that?”

Nico looked at the Ace's dossier.

Adrian understood.

“Me.”

Nico did not answer.

Adrian pulled the envelope toward himself.

“Fourth.”

Celeste turned.

“You don't have to.”

“I do.”

“Not now.”

Adrian looked at her.

“That's what you said before.”

Celeste closed her mouth.

“My case is simpler. The university can confirm the documents. The people who were harmed can be contacted before anything is published.”

Marcel spoke.

“You'll destroy your name.”

Adrian looked at him.

“Maybe.”

“Everything you built.”

“I don't know what I built.”

“So you'll hand your life to journalists to find out?”

Adrian tightened his grip on the envelope.

“I'll start by saying what I know without using other people as scenery.”

Nico watched him.

“You want to record a statement?”

“Yes.”

“Without publishing the candidates' names.”

“Yes.”

“And hand the documents to an independent team.”

“Yes.”

Nico nodded.

“Fourth.”

Tomás touched his dossier.

“And me?”

Nico looked at him.

“You decide.”

Tomás laughed softly.

“Convenient.”

“No.”

Nico moved closer.

“I don't know whether Marta wants to hear from you, see you accused, or never encounter your name again.”

Tomás lowered his eyes.

“Then?”

“Twenty-four hours.”

Marcel laughed.

“An indulgence.”

“No.”

Nico looked at Tomás.

“Time to turn over the documents, make sure Marta is contacted by someone she chooses, and prepare a confession that doesn't expose the employees.”

“What if I run?”

“You don't leave alone.”

Tomás looked at Samuel.

Samuel said, “I'll stay with you.”

Tomás swallowed.

“And if I refuse?”

“Duarte discloses it.”

Duarte said, “Yes.”

Tomás looked at the envelope.

“Twenty-four hours.”

“Yes.”

“That's the maximum?”

“It's more than you gave Marta.”

Tomás closed his eyes, then nodded.

“All right.”

Nico turned to Celeste.

She was already holding the Queen's dossier.

“You don't have to ask.”

Marcel looked at her.

“Celeste.”

“No.”

She opened the envelope.

“The records will be handed over.”

Adrian moved closer.

“All of them?”

Celeste looked at the pages.

“Not publicly.”

Nico nodded.

“First, separated according to the people affected.”

“Yes.”

“The prosecutor decides what can be used concerning her.”

“Yes.”

“And the six people you called protected?”

Celeste drew a breath.

“They're contacted before any decision.”

“By whom?”

“Sara.”

Sara went still.

“Me?”

Celeste looked at her.

“I don't trust myself to choose.”

Sara received the sentence.

“All right.”

Marcel exhaled.

“You're distributing private information like children dealing cards.”

Nico turned toward him.

“And you?”

“What?”

“Your case is already inside Artur's.”

“Not only there,” Duarte said.

Marcel looked at him.

“Be quiet.”

Duarte did not react.

Nico asked, “What else?”

“Obstruction. Coercion. Fraud. Containment operations.”

Marcel stood.

“I won't participate.”

Nico nodded.

“Noted.”

“That means nothing.”

“It means you refused.”

“I have no obligation to help my own prosecution.”

“No.”

Nico held his gaze.

“You also have no right to stop the others.”

Marcel stepped closer.

“Do you think this ends with me?”

“No one said it did.”

“The Group has boards, investors, governments, contracts.”

“I know.”

“Remove me and someone else steps in.”

“Probably.”

“Then what is the point?”

Nico looked at Artur's box.

“Because someone else taking your place doesn't make your decision any less real.”

Marcel fell silent.

“We're not saving the world. We're stopping you from using its size as an excuse.”

Duarte looked at the watch.

“Twenty-four minutes.”

Sara moved toward the head of the table.

“We need the sequence.”

Duarte remained still.

Nico looked at him.

“The first one.”

“Four.”

Sara picked up a sheet of paper.

“Continue.”

“Eleven.”

She wrote it down.

“Twenty-three.”

Samuel looked at the broken watch. Its hands showed 10:23.

Nico noticed.

“Artur.”

Duarte did not answer.

Sara asked, “Is that all?”

“No.”

“Password?”

Duarte looked toward the dining-room door.

“Door.”

Nico let out a breath.

“Of course.”

Sara wrote the word.

Duarte continued.

“The sequence opens the first level. Not the complete archive.”

“Enough for Artur?”

“Yes.”

“And for the others?”

“Partially.”

Nico looked at everyone.

“Then we begin.”

Duarte placed his hand on the deck.

“First, one condition.”

Sara laughed.

“No.”

“Without it, I provide no more sequences.”

Nico watched him.

“What is it?”

“The archive cannot be destroyed.”

Marcel smiled.

Sara shook her head.

Duarte continued.

“You may copy, distribute, and investigate. But the original remains.”

“Why?” Adrian asked.

“Because interpretations change. Witnesses lie. Institutions fail. Without the original, whoever controls the first narrative controls everything.”

Celeste looked at her transcripts.

“He's right.”

Sara turned.

“Keeping the original creates a center again.”

“Yes,” Celeste said. “But destroying it turns the first people who receive copies into the new center.”

Nico ran a hand over his face.

“Perfect. Even destruction is a form of archive.”

Duarte watched him.

“Yes.”

Nico looked at Sara.

“We don't destroy it tonight.”

She tightened her grip on the pen.

“And after that?”

“Someone can propose it later.”

“Who decides?”

“I don't know.”

Sara exhaled.

“We still have no answer.”

“Yes.”

Nico pointed to the sheet with the sequence.

“But we have the next decision.”

Duarte removed the deck from its box and placed it at the center of the table.

“Then no one takes it.”

Marcel studied the cards.

“A symbolic gesture.”

“Yes,” Nico said.

“Symbols prevent nothing.”

“No.”

Nico placed the Joker beside the deck.

“But they tell us when we start lying about what we're doing.”

Sara headed toward the door. Samuel picked up Artur's box. Adrian took his envelope. Tomás held his against his chest. Celeste gathered the Queen's pages and Sara's messages.

Marcel did not move.

Nico looked at Duarte.

“Can you manage the stairs?”

“Yes.”

“Was that pride or medical information?”

“Both.”

Nico offered him a hand.

Duarte looked at it but did not take it. He stood on his own. His fingers trembled more visibly.

Nico did not comment.

Sara opened a side door. Beyond it was a narrow corridor Samuel had not used once during dinner.

A light came on along the floor.

Duarte looked through the opening.

“You altered the access.”

Sara replied, “Yes.”

“Without knowing what was on the other side.”

“Yes.”

“Reckless.”

“Probably.”

Nico stepped closer.

“It's nice to see you learning the same word.”

Sara entered the corridor. Samuel followed. Celeste and Adrian went after him.

Tomás hesitated.

He looked at Marcel.

“Are you coming?”

Marcel remained beside the table.

“No.”

Tomás nodded. He did not try to persuade him. He followed the others.

Duarte stopped at the doorway and looked at the deck in the center of the table. Then at Marcel.

“Don't touch it.”

Marcel smiled.

“An order?”

“A warning.”

“What's the difference?”

Duarte looked at him.

“The consequence.”

He entered the corridor.

Nico was last.

Marcel watched the nameless chair, the deck, the dossiers that had already been carried away.

“Do you think you lost on purpose?” he asked.

Nico stopped.

“No.”

“Then what?”

“I think I stopped trying to win alone.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“Same thing.”

Nico looked at him.

“Only to someone who believes every game needs a winner.”

He entered the corridor.

The door began to close.

Before it disappeared, Nico saw Marcel move toward the table.

Not toward the deck.

Toward the empty chair.

The door closed between them.

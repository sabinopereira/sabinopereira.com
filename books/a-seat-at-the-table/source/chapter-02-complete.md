# CHAPTER 2

Tomás broke the seal.

The black wax split into three pieces. One fell into the soup and floated among the mushrooms.

Tomás watched it for several seconds.

“I assume it isn't edible.”

Nico raised his spoon.

“Depends how hungry you are.”

No one laughed.

Tomás opened the envelope.

He removed four pages held together by a paper clip. He recognized the letterhead on the first before reading the title.

He drew the pages closer to his chest.

A subtle gesture.

Instinctive.

As though he could still hide their contents after opening the envelope in front of everyone.

Marcel noticed.

“What is it?”

Tomás did not answer.

He turned to the second page.

Then the third.

On the last, his breathing changed.

He folded the pages in half and set them beside his plate.

The Dealer watched him.

“Read.”

Tomás looked up.

“No.”

“You may refuse.”

“I already opened it.”

“Refusing to read it does not close the envelope.”

Tomás leaned back in his chair.

“That wasn't in the rules.”

“I will not read the dossier.”

“Then who will?”

“You.”

Tomás gave a small laugh.

“How generous.”

“It is an opportunity.”

“For what?”

“To avoid being summarized by someone else.”

Nico dipped a piece of bread into the soup.

“Given the company, that isn't a terrible offer.”

Marcel looked at him.

Nico ate the bread.

Tomás unfolded the pages again.

The paper trembled slightly in his hands. Not enough to be obvious. Enough for him to notice.

“Operational risk report,” he read. “Team restructuring. Potential conflicts of interest. Financial exposure associated with acquisition projects.”

He stopped.

Adrian looked at him.

“Did you write it?”

“Part of it.”

Marcel answered.

“No.”

Tomás looked up.

Marcel continued.

“He compiled the appendices.”

“I contributed to the report.”

“You compiled the appendices.”

The correction was delivered with the calm of someone adjusting a number in a spreadsheet.

Tomás tightened his grip on the paper.

“That too.”

He resumed reading.

“The document includes performance reviews, internal investigations, and recommendations for the removal of twenty-six employees.”

Celeste set down her spoon.

Tomás turned the page.

“Three weeks before it was presented to the board, excerpts were sent to an external address.”

Adrian frowned.

“By whom?”

Tomás kept his eyes on the page.

The Dealer touched the deck.

“Continue.”

“I don't need you to lead me.”

“Then move forward.”

Tomás read.

“The address belonged to a consulting firm retained by a competitor of the Voss Group.”

The room grew quieter.

Marcel did not move.

Tomás felt the man's eyes on him but refused to meet them.

“The documents allowed them to anticipate layoffs, acquisitions, and changes in leadership.”

Nico tilted his head.

“Useful information.”

Tomás clenched his jaw.

“Yes.”

“Expensive?”

Tomás did not answer.

The Dealer answered for him.

“He received no money.”

Marcel looked at Tomás.

The lack of payment seemed to offend him more than the leak.

“Then what did you get?”

Tomás turned to the last page.

A chain of messages covered almost the entire sheet.

He knew them by heart.

A false name.

An address that would disappear months later.

Promises worded carefully enough that they could never be collected.

Celeste was watching him.

“What did they offer you?”

Tomás ran his thumb along the edge of the page.

“A meeting.”

Marcel gave a quiet laugh.

“You sold a report for a meeting.”

“No.”

“What else?”

“A position.”

“Where?”

Tomás did not answer.

Marcel understood.

“In our group.”

Adrian looked at Tomás.

“But you already worked for the group.”

“Not directly,” Marcel said.

Tomás turned toward him.

“I worked for one of your companies.”

“A subsidiary.”

“It's the same thing to the person receiving the paycheck.”

“Not to the person making decisions.”

The sentence remained between them.

Tomás looked down at the messages again.

“They said there was a position in strategy.”

Marcel almost smiled.

“What level?”

“Director.”

“You had no experience.”

“They said I did.”

“Who?”

“A man named Álvaro.”

The Dealer spoke.

“His name wasn't Álvaro.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“I know.”

“When did you find out?”

“Later.”

“How much later?”

“When he stopped responding.”

“And you continued sending information.”

Adrian looked at Tomás.

“You continued?”

Tomás waited a moment.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

The answer came before he could make it sound more respectable.

“Because I needed to prove I was useful.”

Marcel leaned back.

“Useful to someone who didn't exist.”

“Someone existed.”

“Not enough to hire you.”

Tomás felt the blow.

Not in his face.

In his stomach.

He set down the pages.

“You were never going to hire me.”

Marcel opened one hand.

“You don't know that.”

“I do.”

“No.”

Tomás leaned forward.

“I spent six years on a floor where everyone knew what time you arrived, where you ate lunch, and which words we were supposed to use when asking for approval.”

Marcel watched him without expression.

“We knew who could use the private elevator. Who had to wait. Who could email you directly and who had to go through four people to ask a question.”

Adrian lowered his eyes.

Tomás continued.

“And you didn't know my name until I walked into this room.”

Marcel did not deny it.

Tomás released a short breath.

“Exactly.”

Celeste spoke.

“You still handed other people over.”

Tomás looked at her.

“Yes.”

“People who had nothing to do with your ambition.”

“I know.”

“Did you know then?”

He looked at the list of employees.

“I knew their names were there.”

“That isn't what I asked.”

Tomás pressed his hands against the table.

“I didn't think of them as people.”

No one moved.

Tomás continued.

“They were rows. Assessments. Risks. Potential reductions.”

Nico looked at Celeste.

“Records.”

She lowered her eyes.

Tomás ran a finger over one of the names.

“I had coffee with some of them. I knew who had children. Who was paying off a house. Who had just gone through a divorce.”

Adrian asked, “And you sent it anyway.”

“Yes.”

“For the position.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“Because I wanted to stop being someone who could disappear without anyone noticing.”

Marcel answered.

“So you sacrificed people to feel important.”

Tomás went still.

The urge to defend himself rose whole inside him.

He pushed it away.

“Yes.”

The Dealer did not speak.

Nico set his spoon on the plate.

The admission seemed to occupy more space than the pages.

Tomás returned to the messages.

He read.

“‘Continue sending relevant material. We are assessing the best way to integrate you into a decision-making structure.’”

He turned the page.

“‘The information received demonstrates strategic maturity.’”

Farther down:

“‘Your name is being discussed.’”

His voice failed on the last word.

Adrian noticed.

“Was it?”

Tomás laughed without humor.

“No.”

The Dealer answered.

“It was.”

Everyone looked at him.

Tomás frowned.

“What?”

“Your name was discussed.”

“By whom?”

“The competitor.”

Marcel looked at the Dealer.

“To hire him?”

“No.”

Tomás went still.

The Dealer continued.

“To decide whether you were still useful.”

“And?”

“They concluded you were not.”

The words were spoken without cruelty.

Perhaps that was what made them cruel.

Tomás took a deep breath.

“When?”

“After the third delivery.”

“But there were five.”

“Yes.”

“Then why did they keep responding?”

“Because you kept sending information.”

Tomás closed his eyes.

The Dealer went on.

“The final two responses were automated.”

Tomás opened his eyes.

“No.”

“Yes.”

“They referred to things I had written.”

“Extracted from your messages.”

Tomás looked at the correspondence again.

He read one sentence.

Then another.

What had once seemed like attention now revealed itself as nothing more than repetition.

Marcel exhaled.

“You were used by an automated system.”

Nico looked at him.

“Don't make it sound too funny. You do the same thing, only with real people.”

Marcel did not answer.

Tomás set down the pages.

“I believed it.”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“Yes.”

“You knew?”

“From the beginning.”

Tomás looked up.

“You knew they were using me.”

“Yes.”

“And you did nothing.”

“No.”

“Why?”

“I wanted to see how far you would go.”

Tomás gave a small laugh.

“An experiment.”

“A measurement.”

“Of what?”

“The distance between your ambition and your loyalty.”

Tomás looked at Marcel.

Then at the pages.

“And what was the result?”

“There was no loyalty.”

Marcel smiled.

Tomás turned to him at once.

“Not to you.”

The smile disappeared.

Tomás continued.

“I wasn't loyal to anyone. I was loyal to the idea of getting close.”

“That isn't loyalty,” Marcel said.

“I know.”

Tomás ran his fingers along the last page.

“Now.”

The Dealer indicated the reverse side.

“You aren't finished.”

“I've read it.”

“No.”

Tomás turned the page over.

There was a table containing twenty-six names.

Beside each, a consequence.

Fired.

Transferred.

Investigated.

Contract not renewed.

One name had been marked in red.

**MARTA FREITAS**

Tomás froze.

Celeste noticed.

“Who is she?”

He did not answer.

The Dealer spoke.

“An analyst on Tomás's team.”

Adrian leaned forward.

“What happened?”

Tomás kept staring at the name.

“Marta realized someone had accessed the reports.”

“Did she find out it was you?”

“She suspected me.”

“And?”

Tomás did not answer.

The Dealer continued.

“Tomás informed management that Marta was copying confidential documents.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Adrian drew slightly away from the table.

Nico watched Tomás without a trace of humor.

Marcel asked, “Was it true?”

Tomás looked at him.

“She had copies.”

“Then it was.”

“She was investigating the leak.”

“Without authorization.”

Tomás clenched his jaw.

“Yes.”

Marcel opened one hand.

“Then you didn't lie.”

The Dealer corrected him.

“He did.”

Marcel turned.

“She had documents.”

“Tomás stated that Marta intended to sell them.”

The room fell silent.

Celeste looked at Tomás.

“Did you say that?”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because she was close.”

“To proving it was you.”

“Yes.”

Adrian frowned.

“What happened to her?”

Tomás ran his thumb over the name.

“She was fired.”

“Only that?”

The Dealer answered.

“She was reported for unlawful access and attempted sale of information.”

Tomás closed his eyes.

“Was she convicted?” Adrian asked.

“No.”

“Then what?”

“She settled.”

“Why, if she was innocent?”

Tomás let out a slow breath.

“Because she couldn't afford to defend herself.”

Nico looked at the fragment of wax floating in the soup.

Tomás continued.

“She lost her professional license. Moved to another city.”

Celeste asked, “Did she have a family?”

“A son.”

The word barely emerged.

“How old?”

“Seven.”

Celeste clasped her hands.

Tomás looked at the marked name.

“I knew.”

Adrian leaned closer.

“What?”

“That she had a son.”

“And you did it anyway.”

“Yes.”

Tomás raised his eyes.

“You don't need to keep asking. The answer isn't going to improve.”

The Dealer remained still.

Tomás turned to him.

“Is she alive?”

“Yes.”

“Do you know where she is?”

“Yes.”

“Does she work?”

“Yes.”

“What does she do?”

“She works in the administration office of a nursing home.”

Tomás closed his eyes.

“She was brilliant.”

“Yes.”

“Better than me.”

“Yes.”

The answer came quickly.

Tomás opened his eyes.

“Was that why?”

The Dealer did not answer.

Tomás understood.

He had not reported Marta only because she was close to the truth.

He had chosen her because as long as she remained on the team, the comparison would remain possible.

“I wanted her position too.”

No one spoke.

Tomás folded the list carefully.

Adrian watched him.

“Are you going to find her?”

Tomás gave a small laugh.

“And say what?”

“The truth.”

“So I can feel better?”

“I don't know.”

“Neither do I.”

Celeste spoke.

“She deserves to know.”

“Maybe she already does.”

“It isn't the same.”

“No.”

Tomás rubbed his face.

“If I appear now, I'm entering her life again because I need something.”

Nico tilted his head.

“Then don't ask for anything.”

Tomás looked at him.

“Not even forgiveness?”

“Especially not forgiveness.”

“Then what do I do?”

“Tell her what you did.”

“And after that?”

“She decides whether there is an after.”

Tomás fell silent.

The Dealer asked, “Do you want the dossier released now?”

Tomás looked at the pages.

“Will Marta receive it if I say yes?”

“Yes.”

“The company?”

“Yes.”

“The authorities?”

“Yes.”

“The press?”

“Where applicable.”

Marcel spoke.

“Don't be stupid.”

Everyone looked at him.

Marcel continued.

“If you release it now, you destroy any chance of mounting a defense.”

Tomás studied him.

“Are you advising me?”

“I'm telling you to think.”

“About myself.”

“Naturally.”

Tomás laughed.

“That's how I got here.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

Adrian spoke.

“If you leave it on the table, someone else decides at the end.”

“I know.”

“You may lose control.”

Tomás looked at him.

“That's also how I got here.”

The Dealer waited.

Tomás read Marta's name once more.

Then he placed the pages back inside the envelope.

The fragment of wax was still floating in the soup.

“It stays on the table.”

Adrian frowned.

“Why?”

Tomás took a deep breath.

“Because I'm still thinking about what happens to me.”

No one spoke.

“If I release it now, part of me will say I was brave.”

Nico nodded.

“And you don't trust that part.”

“No.”

Tomás set the envelope beside his plate.

“I want it to stay here until I can tell the difference between a confession and a performance.”

The Dealer inclined his head.

“You may not be able to.”

“I know.”

“Someone else may release it.”

“I know.”

“Or hide it.”

Tomás looked at the empty chair.

“I know.”

The Dealer placed a hand on the deck.

“Then continue.”

Tomás picked up his spoon.

The soup was cold.

He removed the piece of wax with his fingers and placed it beside the envelope.

He tasted the soup.

He grimaced.

Nico looked at him.

“Bad?”

“Cold.”

“That's what happens when you leave something waiting too long.”

Tomás studied him.

“Did you prepare that one?”

“Unfortunately not.”

Tomás ate another spoonful.

Then one more.

He did not finish.

Samuel approached.

“Are you finished, sir?”

Tomás looked at the bowl.

“Yes.”

Samuel removed it.

When he reached for the envelope, Tomás placed a hand over it.

“It stays.”

Samuel let go.

“Yes, sir.”

Tomás studied his face.

“Did you know her?”

Samuel went still.

“Marta.”

“No, sir.”

“Then why did you look at me like that?”

Samuel held his gaze.

“Like what?”

Tomás searched for a word.

Guilt.

Disgust.

Pity.

He found none that said less about him than it did about Samuel.

“It doesn't matter.”

Samuel walked away.

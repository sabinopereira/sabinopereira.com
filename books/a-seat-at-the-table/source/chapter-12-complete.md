# CHAPTER 12

The corridor descended.

Not in a straight line.

It turned twice inside the wall, narrow enough to force them into single file. A white light came on along the floor as Sara passed and went dark several yards behind them.

The walls had no windows, pictures, or doors.

The house quickly disappeared.

Only footsteps remained, along with Duarte's breathing and Artur's box in Samuel's hands.

Nico came last.

Before the door closed, he had seen Marcel move toward the nameless chair.

Not the deck.

The chair.

It bothered him more than it should have.

“How much time?” he asked.

Sara did not stop.

“Twenty-two minutes.”

Duarte answered behind her.

“Twenty-one.”

“It's reassuring that the illness hasn't affected your need to correct people.”

“It hasn't.”

His voice was heavier.

Duarte kept one hand near the wall without quite leaning on it. The descent shortened his breath, but he refused to slow down.

Nico moved closer.

“You can use my shoulder.”

“No.”

“It was an offer, not a metaphor.”

“Keep walking.”

Sara reached the end of the corridor.

A metal door filled the entire wall. It had no handle, visible lock, or keypad.

Only a rectangle of dark glass at eye level.

Samuel set Artur's box on the floor.

“Is this it?”

“Yes,” Duarte said.

Sara removed a small electronic plate from her pocket and pressed it against the wall.

Nothing happened.

She tried again.

“They changed the reader.”

Duarte stepped closer.

“No.”

“It doesn't recognize the key.”

“Because it isn't a key.”

Sara turned.

“It worked at the exterior entrance.”

“The exterior entrance belongs to the house.”

Duarte looked at the door.

“This doesn't.”

Tomás ran his fingers over the wall.

“The archive is outside the property?”

“Legally, yes.”

“Physically?”

“No.”

Tomás exhaled.

“Of course.”

Adrian studied the dark glass.

“What opens it?”

Duarte did not answer.

Samuel understood.

“A person.”

“Yes.”

“Biometrics?”

“In part.”

Nico closed his eyes.

“I swear that phrase will outlive us all.”

Duarte brought his face close to the glass.

The surface lit up. A white line passed over his eyes.

Then a word appeared:

**VOICE**

Duarte stood motionless.

Sara looked at him.

“Say the password.”

“It isn't enough.”

“Then what?”

“A sentence.”

“Which one?”

Duarte kept his eyes on the door.

“There is no fixed sentence.”

Tomás frowned.

“Then what does it recognize?”

“Vocal patterns. Rhythm. Breathing.”

Nico moved closer.

“Memory.”

Duarte did not answer.

Samuel looked at the broken watch inside the box.

“The sentence has to be connected to something.”

“Yes.”

“To Artur?”

Duarte closed his eyes for an instant.

Then he spoke to the door.

“A closed door does not erase what was left on the other side.”

The white light turned green.

A heavy mechanism moved inside the wall.

The door drew back several inches.

Nico watched Duarte.

“Did you write that?”

“No.”

Samuel went still.

“Artur.”

“Yes.”

The door opened.

The air beyond it was cold and dry, carrying the smell of paper, metal, and electricity.

Sara went in first.

Lights came on in sequence.

The archive was larger than the dining room.

Metal shelves covered the walls from floor to ceiling. Gray boxes were identified by numbers, not names. At the center stood two tables, four computers, and a glass cabinet filled with drives, keys, and small devices.

It did not look like a vault.

It looked like an office built to continue after everyone was dead.

Tomás stopped at the entrance.

“All of it is here?”

Duarte walked past him.

“No.”

Tomás laughed softly.

“Of course not.”

“This is one node.”

“How many are there?”

Duarte did not answer.

Sara went to the computers.

“This is the primary one.”

“It was.”

She stopped.

“When did that change?”

“Six months ago.”

Everyone thought of Marcel, though he was not there.

Samuel placed Artur's box on the central table.

“The access you gave him.”

Duarte nodded.

“Limited.”

“You keep saying that as though it's reassuring.”

“It isn't.”

Sara turned on the first computer.

A countdown appeared on the screen.

**00:19:42**

Adrian moved closer.

“What happens at the end?”

Duarte looked at the timer.

“Privileges are redistributed.”

“By whom?”

“By the protocol.”

Nico leaned against the table.

“And who wrote the protocol?”

“I did.”

“Then by you.”

“By an earlier version of me.”

Sara began entering the sequence.

Four.

Eleven.

Twenty-three.

In the next field, she typed:

**DOOR**

The system accepted it.

Several folders appeared on the screen.

**LEAL, ARTUR**

**VOSS, ADRIAN**

**MENDES, RUI**

**VALE, DUARTE**

**SOUSA, TOMÁS**

**VOSS, CELESTE**

Others remained locked.

Sara opened Artur's folder.

Hundreds of files filled the screen.

Reports.

Calls.

Images.

Transfers.

Names.

Samuel did not move closer. He stayed on the other side of the table with his hands resting on his brother's box.

Nico looked at him.

“Are you all right?”

“No.”

The answer came without hesitation.

“Good.”

Samuel raised his eyes.

Nico corrected himself.

“Not good. Just… true.”

Samuel nodded.

Sara turned on the second computer.

“We need to copy everything.”

Duarte replied, “No.”

She turned.

“We don't have time to separate it.”

“Copy everything and the system locks.”

“Why?”

“Volume.”

“How much?”

“Seventy-three terabytes in this node.”

Tomás looked at the shelves.

“And the paper?”

“Some evidence exists only in physical form.”

Celeste approached a box marked with Artur's number and opened it.

Inside were a pair of glasses, a planner, three disks, and a transparent envelope containing bloodstained fragments of fabric.

Samuel looked away.

Adrian went still.

“You kept this for twenty years.”

Duarte answered, “Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because it might be needed.”

Samuel looked at him.

“It was needed every day.”

Duarte said nothing.

Sara opened a transfer program.

“We copy Artur first.”

Nico moved closer.

“To whom?”

“Two legal teams. Three journalists. One prosecutor.”

“Which one?”

Sara hesitated.

Nico saw it.

“Which one?”

“A magistrate who worked with the prosecutor.”

Celeste looked at her.

“Do you trust her?”

“Not completely.”

“Then?”

“I trust her not to hand everything to Marcel.”

Tomás breathed out.

“Our standard has fallen.”

Nico looked at the screen.

“Separate copies. No one gets everything.”

Sara nodded.

“One team receives the audio and images. Another the transfers. The prosecutor gets the index and the connections.”

Duarte approached.

“Without the complete set, they can claim manipulation.”

“Then we send a common inventory.”

Celeste said, “Signed by all of us.”

Marcel was not there.

His absence became useful again.

Adrian asked, “By him too?”

Nico looked at the door.

“He won't sign it.”

“Then he can deny it.”

“He'll deny it anyway.”

Sara started the transfers.

Three progress bars began to move.

Twelve percent.

Seven.

Four.

The timer dropped below eighteen minutes.

Tomás pulled out a chair.

“My dossier?”

Nico looked at him.

“You still have twenty-four hours.”

“What if access disappears in eighteen minutes?”

Duarte answered, “The local copies remain.”

“Which ones?”

“Some.”

Tomás closed his eyes.

“I'm beginning to miss complete sentences.”

Sara opened his folder.

“We copy the raw material to a sealed device. It doesn't leave today.”

Tomás nodded.

“And Marta?”

“Contacted by a lawyer who represents no one in this room.”

Celeste said, “I know someone.”

Tomás looked at her.

“No.”

The answer came quickly.

Celeste accepted it.

Sara removed a device from the glass cabinet and connected it.

Duarte watched.

“Not that one.”

“Why?”

“It's associated with my access.”

“Even better.”

“No.”

Duarte came closer.

“Using it will make the system recognize a transfer of authority.”

Sara went still.

Nico looked at the device.

“To whom?”

“Whoever removes it from the node.”

Marcel.

They all thought the name.

No one said it.

Sara set the device down.

“Which one do we use?”

Duarte indicated a small black drive.

“That one.”

Samuel opened the cabinet and removed it.

The alarm began immediately.

A red light came on above the door.

Sara turned to Duarte.

“You said—”

“I said you could use it.”

“You didn't say it would trigger an alarm.”

“You didn't ask.”

Nico laughed once.

“It's admirable how faithful you remain to the worst of yourself.”

Duarte looked at the timer.

“Sixteen minutes.”

Samuel connected the drive to the computer.

Tomás's folder began to copy.

Celeste opened hers.

The list of 123 records appeared on screen.

Her expression changed.

Not because she saw the names.

Because she saw how they had been organized by consequence.

**CAREER**

**FAMILY**

**HEALTH**

**PHYSICAL RISK**

**DEATH**

Celeste stopped.

“There's a death category.”

Duarte replied, “Yes.”

“How many?”

“I don't know.”

She opened it.

Six names.

Artur was among them.

Celeste closed the folder.

“I can't do this now.”

Adrian came to her side.

“Then don't do it alone.”

She looked at him.

“You shouldn't help me.”

“I'm not helping you.”

Adrian pulled out another chair.

“I'm identifying who needs to be warned.”

Celeste sat.

Together, they began separating the records.

Nico watched them for a moment, then turned to Sara.

“Rui.”

She opened his folder.

There had been recent access.

The most recent entry was from that night.

“He got into the system.”

Duarte approached.

“When?”

Sara pointed.

“Twenty-seven minutes ago.”

“From where?”

The address was hidden.

Tomás leaned closer.

“He could be outside.”

“Or inside the property,” Samuel said.

The red light continued to pulse.

Nico looked at the door.

“Marcel.”

Duarte shook his head.

“He doesn't have Rui's credentials.”

“But he could have used him.”

Sara moved through the access log.

“Rui tried to open Nico's folder.”

Nico went still.

“There is a folder on me here.”

Duarte replied, “Yes.”

“How many pages?”

“I don't know.”

“Stop answering that.”

“I don't know.”

Nico looked at Sara.

“Open it.”

She hesitated.

“We don't have much time.”

“Open it.”

The folder appeared.

Hundreds of files.

Photographs.

Contracts.

Medical appointments.

Recordings.

Unpublished drafts.

Calls with Sara.

Nico stepped away from the screen.

“Close it.”

Sara closed it.

Neither commented.

The timer showed fourteen minutes.

Artur's transfer reached forty-three percent.

“It won't finish,” Sara said.

Duarte looked at the speed.

“No.”

Samuel tightened his hands around the box.

“Then we choose.”

Nico moved to the computer.

“Audio.”

Sara selected it.

“Images.”

“Yes.”

“Financial report.”

“Yes.”

“Emergency calls.”

“Yes.”

“Door records.”

“Yes.”

Celeste spoke without looking up.

“Transfers connected to the companies.”

Duarte answered, “There are too many.”

“Only those directly connected to the weeks before and after his death.”

Sara selected them.

The estimate dropped.

Four minutes.

The main timer showed thirteen.

Adrian looked at his own folder.

“We record the statement now.”

Sara turned.

“Here?”

“Yes.”

“You haven't prepared.”

“That's the point.”

Nico placed his broken phone on the table.

“That won't work.”

Adrian looked at him.

“Thank you.”

Samuel removed a small recorder from the cabinet.

“This one.”

Duarte said, “It's connected to the archive.”

“Good,” Adrian replied.

He sat in front of it.

Celeste placed one hand on the table.

She did not touch him.

Adrian pressed the button.

He stated his full name.

The date.

Then he spoke for three minutes.

He did not apologize.

He did not explain his parents.

He did not say he was young.

He described the interventions he knew of, the benefits he had received, and his decision to leave every position until the processes were reviewed.

When he finished, he stared at the recorder.

“Is that enough?”

Nico answered, “To begin.”

Adrian nodded.

Sara copied the file to two locations.

The timer showed nine minutes.

Artur's transfer completed.

The screen displayed:

**COPIES SENT: 3**

Samuel closed his eyes.

He did not cry.

He did not smile.

He rested a hand on the box.

“Now it exists outside this place.”

Sara replied, “Yes.”

A mechanism sounded in the door.

Everyone turned.

The red light turned yellow.

Duarte looked at the clock.

“Redistribution has begun.”

“There are nine minutes left,” Tomás said.

“The process precedes the lockout.”

Sara went to the cabinet.

“We remove the keys.”

Duarte stepped in front of her.

“No.”

“If they stay, Marcel could receive authority.”

“If you remove them, the archive fragments.”

“Good.”

“No.”

Sara tried to move past him.

Duarte caught her wrist.

It was a weak gesture.

Even so, the room stopped.

Nico stepped forward.

“Let her go.”

Duarte released her.

Sara looked at her wrist, then at him.

“Don't do that again.”

Duarte was struggling to breathe.

“Remove the physical key during redistribution and we lose complete access.”

“Everyone?”

“Yes.”

“You too?”

“Yes.”

“Marcel?”

“Yes.”

“Then we remove it.”

“You don't understand.”

“I understand enough.”

Duarte struck the table with his hand.

“No.”

His voice rose for the first time.

The effort made him bend at the waist.

Nico caught his arm.

Duarte tried to pull away but could not.

“Breathe,” Nico said.

“Don't tell me—”

“It isn't moral. It's physiology.”

Duarte braced himself against the table.

Sara opened the cabinet.

Inside were seven small keys, each bearing a symbol.

King.

Queen.

Ace.

Jack.

Joker.

Watcher.

Dealer.

Nico looked at them.

“Seven.”

Duarte raised his head.

“No.”

Sara picked up the key at the center.

It bore no symbol.

It was smooth.

“What's this?”

Duarte went still.

Samuel understood.

“The archive key.”

“No,” Duarte said.

“The key to the keys.”

Sara brought it near the reader.

The system emitted a shrill tone.

The screen displayed:

**TRANSFER IN PROGRESS**

**REMOVING THE KEY WILL INTERRUPT CONSOLIDATION**

**CONFIRM?**

Sara looked at Nico.

He shook his head.

“Don't hand that to me.”

“We have to decide.”

“Then decide and say why.”

She tightened her grip on the key.

“If I leave it, someone receives consolidated control.”

“Possibly.”

“If I remove it, no one gets everything.”

“And we lose access.”

“Yes.”

“People may lose their evidence.”

“Yes.”

“Marcel too.”

“Yes.”

Nico looked at Samuel.

Artur's box had already been copied.

At Celeste.

Some of the records were being separated.

At Adrian.

The statement existed in two locations.

Tomás still had only one sealed copy.

Rui remained active.

There was no clean choice.

“Remove it,” Samuel said.

Duarte looked at him.

“You don't know what you'll lose.”

Samuel gripped the broken watch.

“I know what we've already lost while you preserved everything.”

Celeste stood.

“Remove it.”

Adrian nodded.

Tomás looked at the incomplete transfer of his own dossier. The bar stood at sixty-two percent.

He closed his eyes.

“Remove it.”

Duarte turned to Nico.

“You understand.”

“Yes.”

“Then stop her.”

Nico looked at the key.

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because the only reason to maintain complete access is to believe someone should have it.”

“Someone should.”

“That's the problem.”

Sara pulled out the key.

The lights went out.

For one second, the archive was completely dark.

Then emergency lights came on along the floor.

The computers restarted.

The transfers stopped.

One message appeared on the main screen:

**AUTHORITY FRAGMENTED**

**TOTAL ACCESS UNAVAILABLE**

Duarte closed his eyes.

His hand lost its strength.

Nico caught him before he fell.

“Duarte.”

He tried to breathe.

He could not.

Sara searched the cabinet.

Celeste cleared documents from the table.

Samuel helped Nico lower him to the floor.

Adrian knelt.

“He needs an ambulance.”

Duarte opened his eyes.

“No.”

“That wasn't a question.”

Sara removed a small medical pouch.

“There's medication.”

Duarte tried to raise one hand.

“Pocket.”

Nico found a bottle inside his coat.

Sara checked the dose.

Duarte swallowed the pill without water.

They stayed silent until his breathing began to stabilize.

Tomás looked toward the door.

“Can we get out?”

The yellow light had turned red.

Samuel went to the reader.

He did not answer.

Nico remained kneeling beside Duarte.

“You did this knowing you might die down here?”

Duarte opened his eyes.

“Yes.”

“Is that dedication or lack of imagination?”

“Both.”

Nico breathed out.

Duarte looked at the screen.

“You destroyed access.”

“We fragmented it.”

“You don't know where the pieces went.”

“No.”

“Marcel may have one.”

“Yes.”

“Rui too.”

“Yes.”

Duarte closed his eyes.

“Then you didn't win.”

Nico sat on the floor.

“No.”

Duarte opened them.

“You lost.”

“That too.”

Nico looked at the shelves.

“It feels more honest.”

The door mechanism sounded again.

Samuel stepped back.

The door slowly opened.

Marcel stood on the other side.

He was wearing his overcoat.

In his right hand, he held a small metal card bearing the figure of a King.

Sara stood.

“Where did you find that?”

Marcel looked at the card.

“In the chair.”

Duarte tried to sit up.

Nico supported his back.

Marcel studied the restarted screens.

“What did you do?”

“We removed the key,” Samuel said.

Marcel smiled.

“Then you were too late.”

Sara moved toward him.

“What did you receive?”

“I don't know.”

“Lie,” Celeste said.

Duarte did not correct her.

Marcel looked at his wife.

“I received enough.”

Adrian stood.

“Enough for what?”

“To survive.”

Nico studied the card.

“You don't have complete access.”

“I don't need it.”

“Which folder?”

Marcel slipped the card into his pocket.

“You don't know?”

Nico rose slowly.

“If we knew everything, you'd have nothing to say.”

Marcel smiled.

“You learned.”

“Unfortunately.”

Samuel stepped between Marcel and Artur's box.

Marcel looked at it.

“That one is already out.”

“Yes.”

The smile disappeared.

“To whom?”

“People you don't control.”

Marcel watched Samuel.

“Everyone can be controlled.”

“Maybe.”

Samuel placed a hand on the box.

“But now you'll have to find more than one door.”

Marcel looked at the knife Samuel no longer carried, then at the watch.

He did not advance.

Celeste moved toward her husband.

“Take me with you.”

Adrian turned.

So did Marcel.

Celeste kept her face still.

“Take me.”

“Why?”

“I want to see whether you still believe I can be useful.”

Marcel studied her.

Then he smiled slightly.

“Of course.”

Celeste shook her head.

“That was what I needed.”

“What?”

“The answer.”

She moved away from him.

“I'm not going.”

Marcel stood motionless.

Adrian came to his mother's side.

He did not touch her.

Marcel looked at his son.

“You?”

Adrian held his gaze.

“No.”

“Everything you have came from me.”

“I know.”

“And you'll let them destroy it.”

Adrian drew a breath.

“I'm going to find out what's left.”

Marcel clenched his jaw.

He looked at Sara.

“You don't control this.”

“No.”

At Duarte.

“Neither do you.”

Duarte answered with difficulty.

“No.”

Finally, at Nico.

“And you?”

Nico took the Joker from his pocket.

“I barely control this.”

Marcel looked at the card, then turned away.

He walked through the corridor without saying goodbye.

None of them tried to follow.

The front door of the house stood open when they returned to the foyer.

The rain had stopped.

Cold air came in through the entrance and crossed the rooms.

Marcel was gone.

So was the car.

In the dining room, the deck remained at the center of the table.

The nameless chair was empty.

Nico approached.

He removed the cards from the box and separated them into small piles.

Not by suit.

By person.

He placed the King before Marcel's empty place.

The Queen before Celeste.

The Ace before Adrian.

The Jack before Tomás.

The Watcher beside Artur's box.

The Dealer before Duarte.

He kept the Joker in his hand.

Sara watched him.

“What are you doing?”

“Something symbolic.”

Marcel would have hated the answer.

Nico placed the Joker on his own plate.

Then he divided the remaining cards into three piles.

He gave one to Sara.

Another to Samuel.

He left the third on the nameless chair.

“That changes nothing,” Duarte said.

Nico looked at him.

“No.”

“Then why?”

“Because no one takes the whole deck.”

Sara looked at the cards in her hand.

Samuel closed his fingers around his.

Celeste sat in the Queen's place.

Adrian stood beside her.

Tomás kept the envelope against his chest.

The front door remained open.

No one moved to close it.

For the first time that night, the house did not feel like a trap.

It did not feel safe either.

Nico looked into the darkness beyond the entrance.

Marcel was out there with part of the archive.

Rui still had access to something.

Much of the evidence remained trapped in systems no one could fully open anymore.

Artur's case existed outside the house.

It was little.

It was irreversible.

Sara moved closer.

“And now?”

Nico looked at the open door.

“Now we find out what left with us.”

“And what stayed behind.”

“Yes.”

Duarte sat down slowly.

“You won't like it.”

Nico picked up the Joker.

“It would be suspicious if we did.”

Samuel placed Artur's box on the table.

The broken watch remained stopped at 10:23.

Outside, the night was beginning to lose its color.

No one closed the door.

# CHAPTER 10

Sara came down the stairs.

She did not hurry.

Her hand followed the banister, though she did not seem to need it. Her dark coat was fastened to the neck. Her hair was tied back. The scar near her jaw was smaller than Nico remembered.

Or perhaps memory had made it larger.

He remained at the entrance to the dining room.

For seventeen years, he had imagined this moment in every possible way.

Sara running to him.

Sara striking him.

Sara crying.

Sara explaining.

Sara not recognizing him.

He had never imagined her descending a staircase while six people watched, or that his first feeling would be irritation that they were all there.

She reached the final step and stopped six feet from him.

Nico opened his mouth.

Sara spoke first.

“You're older.”

He let out a breath.

“So are you.”

“I know.”

“It was an observation.”

“So was mine.”

Neither moved.

Celeste still stood beside the table with the messages in her hands. Adrian watched Nico. Tomás studied Sara as though trying to recognize the woman hidden in the stage photograph. Samuel kept his eyes lowered. Marcel looked less surprised than offended. Duarte remained standing at the head of the table.

Sara glanced at the shattered phone on the step.

“Was that necessary?”

Nico followed her gaze.

“I don't know.”

“Then why?”

“I needed to break something that wouldn't bleed.”

Sara received the answer without looking away.

“Fair.”

Nico laughed once. The sound came out wrong.

“No.”

“No what?”

“Don't say that as though we spoke yesterday.”

Sara tightened her hand around the banister.

“All right.”

The ease with which she yielded irritated him more.

Nico took one step closer.

“You were alive.”

“Yes.”

“All this time.”

“Yes.”

“And you tried to contact me.”

Sara looked at Celeste, then at the documents.

“A few times.”

“Twenty-three.”

She returned her eyes to him.

“Confirmed.”

Nico went still.

“There were more?”

“Probably.”

“You don't know?”

“No.”

He gave a small laugh.

“Fantastic. The woman who returns from the dead also works with estimates.”

Sara did not react.

“You received an answer in my name.”

“Yes.”

“And you believed it.”

“For a while.”

“How long?”

She waited.

“Years.”

The word settled between them.

Nico looked at the floor.

“You could have tried again.”

“I did.”

“More.”

“I tried more.”

“Another way.”

“I tried other ways.”

He looked up.

“Then why did you stop?”

Sara did not answer at once. Her silence lacked Duarte's precision and Celeste's control. It seemed to be searching.

“Because I began to believe that even if the message was false, the choice might have been real.”

Nico did not move.

“What choice?”

“Yours.”

“I didn't make one.”

“You published only part of it.”

“To keep you alive.”

“I didn't ask you to.”

“I know.”

“You didn't know then.”

“I knew you didn't want me to.”

Sara nodded.

“Then you made a choice.”

“Not the one Rui sent.”

“No.”

“But enough for you to stop trusting me.”

“Yes.”

Nico ran a hand over his face.

“Seventeen years.”

“Yes.”

“And when did you discover the reply was fake?”

“I didn't discover it all at once.”

“Of course.”

“I started finding payments. Then patterns. Calls that disappeared. People who knew too much about my movements.”

Nico pointed into the room.

“And still you didn't look for me.”

Sara held his gaze.

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because I didn't know whom you were willing to sacrifice to save me.”

The sentence struck him.

Nico fell silent.

“And because I didn't know whether you wanted me alive or absolved.”

He pressed his lips together.

“That doesn't make sense.”

“It does to me.”

“Then explain.”

Sara looked at the stage photograph on the table.

“You published part of the truth and spent years telling the story as though you had been deceived.”

“I was deceived.”

“Yes.”

“They paid me.”

“Yes.”

“They threatened you.”

“Yes.”

Nico stepped closer.

“Then what's the problem?”

“The problem is that you chose too.”

He went still.

Sara drew a breath.

“I'm not saying you did it alone. I'm not saying you knew what would happen.”

“But?”

“But you knew the version that was published was smaller.”

Nico lowered his eyes.

“And when people started calling you brave, you didn't correct them.”

“You had disappeared.”

“I know.”

“I thought you were dead.”

“I know.”

“Then what did you want me to do?”

“I don't know.”

The answer disarmed him.

Sara looked at him.

“That's the truth.”

Nico breathed out.

“You could have started with that seventeen years ago.”

“You could have refused the money.”

“And you could have accepted that I was trying to keep them from killing you.”

“I could have.”

“But you didn't.”

“No.”

“Why?”

Sara was silent.

When she answered, her voice was lower.

“Because I was afraid you were right.”

Nico watched her.

“About what?”

“That some truths aren't worth a life.”

The room remained still.

Even Marcel did not interrupt.

“If I accepted that, I had to admit I might have made the truth more important than people.”

Nico looked at her.

“And now?”

“Now I know I did that sometimes.”

“With me?”

Sara paused.

“Yes.”

The answer did not bring them closer.

But it changed the distance.

Nico stepped aside, opening the way into the room.

“Then finish dinner.”

Sara entered.

As she passed Samuel, she briefly touched his arm.

It was not a greeting.

It was confirmation.

Samuel nodded.

Marcel watched the gesture.

“Touching.”

Sara stopped and looked at him.

“No.”

The word seemed to carry no force. Even so, Marcel fell silent.

Sara walked to the table but did not sit.

Envelopes, cards, and documents covered most of the cloth. Fragments of black wax lay among the glasses. The knife remained at the center, its handle pointing toward no one.

Sara looked at the nameless chair, then at Duarte.

“Stop standing there as though you've been surprised.”

“I have.”

“Not completely.”

“No.”

“You knew someone controlled the house.”

“Yes.”

“You just didn't know it was me.”

“I suspected.”

Nico laughed softly.

“Of course he did.”

Sara kept her eyes on Duarte.

“Not enough to cancel.”

“No.”

“Why?”

“I needed the table.”

“And I needed to keep you at it.”

Duarte inclined his head.

“It worked.”

“Until now.”

Marcel rested his hands on the back of his chair.

“Could someone explain who's in charge here?”

Sara looked at him.

“No one.”

“You own this house.”

“No.”

Samuel watched her.

Sara corrected herself.

“I control the company that owns the property.”

Tomás exhaled.

“Through how many other companies?”

“Four.”

“Naturally.”

“Three existed before me.”

Marcel smiled.

“So you bought an opaque structure to fight opaque structures.”

Sara looked at him.

“Yes.”

The answer took away part of his advantage.

“With what money?”

“Money removed from the network.”

“Stolen.”

“Recovered.”

“The same word with a better lawyer.”

Sara did not look away.

“Perhaps.”

Celeste watched her.

“What happened to the prosecutor?”

Sara turned.

“She's alive.”

“She helped you.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because she found my name in documents connected to the foundation.”

Celeste tightened her grip on the transcript.

“I destroyed her career.”

“Yes.”

“And she helped you anyway.”

“Not because of you.”

Celeste was silent.

Sara moved closer until the distance between them became uncomfortable.

“She helped me because she realized the same system that pushed her out was going to kill me.”

“What's her name?”

Sara studied her.

“It doesn't belong to you.”

Celeste received the answer, then nodded.

“No.”

Sara looked at the pages in her hands.

“But she knows you're here.”

Celeste raised her eyes.

“And?”

“She asked me to tell you something.”

Celeste's hand tightened around the paper.

“What?”

“Survival wasn't forgiveness.”

Celeste closed her eyes.

Sara moved away before an answer came.

Nico watched her.

“Did you rehearse that?”

Sara looked at him.

“No.”

“It sounded rehearsed.”

“She rehearsed it.”

Nico stopped smiling.

Duarte sat again.

“Have you finished the first course?”

Sara turned toward him.

“You're no longer in a position to control the pace.”

“Someone has to.”

“That's the disease.”

Duarte placed his hands on the table.

“And you're immune?”

Sara did not answer.

“You bought the house. Prepared access. Moved people. Allowed dinner to proceed because it was useful to you.”

“Yes.”

“You hid from Nico that you were alive.”

“Yes.”

“You used Samuel.”

Samuel raised his eyes.

“Yes.”

“You put everyone in a room without meaningful consent.”

“Yes.”

Duarte inclined his head.

“Then the difference between us is?”

Sara stood still.

Nico looked at her. So did Celeste.

Sara drew a breath.

“I don't know yet.”

Duarte almost smiled.

“Honest.”

“Don't mistake honesty for absolution.”

“I don't.”

“You do that with everything.”

Tomás looked between them.

“Can we know what's happening outside this room?”

Sara turned to him.

“A team is trying to reconstruct the archive index.”

Marcel went still.

“What team?”

“Lawyers. Journalists. Technicians. People affected by the network.”

“Who chose them?”

“I did.”

Marcel smiled.

“There it is.”

Sara did not react.

Adrian asked, “Do they have the documents?”

“Some.”

“Which ones?”

“Incomplete copies. Fragments. Financial records.”

“And the dossiers here?”

“Not yet.”

Tomás looked at his envelope.

“Then this table is the source?”

“No.”

“What is it?”

“A key.”

Duarte corrected her.

“Part of one.”

Sara looked at him.

“Yes.”

Nico approached the table.

“How many times are we going to say ‘in part’ before someone admits no one controls the archive?”

Sara looked at him.

“I don't know.”

“You wanted to.”

“I wanted to reconstruct it.”

“Why?”

“To distribute it.”

“By whom?”

“Different people.”

“Chosen by you.”

“Yes.”

“Using criteria defined by you.”

“Yes.”

“And who decides what each person receives?”

Sara did not answer.

Nico looked around the table.

“You.”

“Initially.”

“It's always initially.”

Sara pressed her lips together.

“We needed a plan.”

“No.”

“We did.”

“You needed a plan that kept you in the center.”

“That isn't fair.”

Nico laughed.

“No. It's familiar.”

Sara went still.

“Duarte divides the truth so no one can destroy it. You want to divide it so no one can control it.”

“That isn't the same.”

“Maybe not.”

Nico moved closer.

“But both of you choose who gets to know what.”

Sara held his gaze.

“Then what do you propose?”

“I don't know.”

Marcel gave a small laugh.

“Finally.”

Nico turned.

“Don't mistake a lack of answers for a lack of problems.”

Marcel leaned against the chair.

“It's easy to criticize when you don't have to decide.”

Nico looked at the envelopes.

“That's what everyone here did.”

Duarte spoke.

“There is no time for a perfect solution.”

Sara turned.

“How long?”

“Not long.”

“Give me a number.”

Duarte looked at Artur's watch.

“Forty-three minutes.”

Samuel frowned.

“Until what?”

“Until the archive changes access.”

Sara went still.

“That wasn't supposed to happen before dawn.”

“No.”

“Then someone activated the protocol.”

Duarte looked at Marcel.

Everyone followed his gaze.

Marcel kept his expression calm.

“It wasn't me.”

Duarte replied, “True.”

Celeste watched him.

“Who?”

Duarte turned to Sara.

“Probably your team.”

Her lips tightened.

“They don't have enough access.”

“Then someone gave it to them.”

Tomás looked around.

“Rui?”

Duarte shook his head.

“No.”

“Who else knows?”

Duarte was silent.

Sara moved toward him.

“How many people have the sequences?”

“Three.”

“You.”

“Yes.”

“Who else?”

“One is dead.”

Samuel looked at the watch.

“Artur.”

Duarte's silence confirmed it.

Sara asked, “And the third?”

Duarte looked at Marcel.

This time Marcel understood before the others.

“No.”

Celeste turned to him.

“You have access.”

“Limited.”

“Yes,” Duarte said.

Marcel gripped the chair.

“Emergency access.”

Sara stared at him.

“For how long?”

“Six months.”

Samuel went still.

“Since she bought the house.”

Marcel looked at Duarte.

“You said it was protection.”

“It was.”

“For whom?”

Duarte did not answer.

Nico exhaled.

“That word again.”

Sara approached Duarte.

“Give me the sequences.”

“No.”

“Marcel has access.”

“Partial.”

“Enough to destroy evidence.”

“Or preserve it.”

“You don't trust him.”

“No.”

“Then why?”

Duarte studied her.

“Because I don't trust you.”

The sentence did not surprise Sara.

“We already knew that.”

“No.”

Duarte looked at the others.

“You believe tonight is a contest between those who want to hide the truth and those who want to reveal it.”

Nico tilted his head.

“Isn't it?”

“No. It is a contest between different forms of control.”

Sara said nothing.

“Marcel would protect the empire. Celeste would choose the damage she considered bearable. Samuel would sacrifice the present to his brother. Adrian would turn guilt into renunciation. Tomás would search for an exit that still kept him inside.”

Tomás lowered his eyes.

Duarte looked at Nico.

“And you would refuse to decide until refusal looked like a moral decision.”

Nico held his gaze.

“Possibly.”

Duarte turned to Sara.

“You would distribute the archive among people selected by you and call it decentralization.”

Sara did not respond.

“Everyone wants a structure that preserves their own version of justice.”

Marcel smiled.

“Finally, someone rational.”

Duarte looked at him.

“You are the least rational of all.”

The smile vanished.

Sara approached the table.

“What's your version?”

“Preserve the system.”

Adrian frowned.

“After everything?”

“Because of everything.”

Samuel looked at him.

“Explain.”

Duarte let his eyes travel over the dossiers.

“Total information doesn't create total justice. It creates panic, revenge, opportunism, and new markets.”

Tomás said, “Those already exist.”

“Yes.”

“Then what are you preserving?”

“Memory.”

Sara exhaled.

“No. You're preserving your position as the only man who knows the map.”

Duarte did not deny it.

“That too.”

Nico watched him.

“Why?”

Duarte was silent for several seconds.

When he answered, his voice had lost some of its hardness.

“Because I don't believe people are better when no one forces them to be.”

Samuel looked at the knife.

“You forced us.”

“Yes.”

“Did you make us better?”

Duarte did not answer.

Nico noticed his hand resting on the table.

His fingers were trembling.

Barely.

“You're sick.”

Duarte looked at him.

Sara closed her eyes.

Celeste noticed.

“You knew.”

Sara nodded.

“How long?”

“Four months.”

Nico watched Duarte.

“What is it?”

He did not answer.

Sara did.

“Cancer. Pancreatic.”

Tomás lowered his eyes. Adrian went still. Marcel seemed to calculate the meaning at once.

“How long?”

Duarte looked at him.

“Months.”

“How many?”

“Fewer than you need.”

Marcel pressed his lips together.

Nico moved toward the head of the table.

“So this is succession.”

“No.”

“Legacy.”

“No.”

“Confession.”

“No.”

“Then what?”

Duarte looked at the entire table.

“Continuity.”

Nico laughed softly.

“Of course.”

“The system cannot depend on one person.”

“But it does.”

“Now.”

“And you want to correct that.”

“Yes.”

“By choosing who inherits the pieces.”

“Yes.”

Sara shook her head.

“There will be no heir.”

Duarte looked at her.

“There are already several.”

Silence fell.

Nico looked around.

Marcel had partial access. Sara controlled the house and part of the index. Duarte kept the sequences. Samuel knew Artur's plan. Celeste had her own records. The dossiers contained names that led to other names.

The chair had protected a single file and revealed that even that power was not absolute.

None of them controlled everything.

Each controlled enough to cause harm.

Nico pulled out a chair.

Not the nameless one.

His own.

He turned it sideways between the table and the door and sat.

Sara watched him.

“What are you doing?”

“I'm tired of choosing between people who want to save the world by locking a different door.”

Duarte touched the deck.

“You have no alternative.”

“I do.”

“What?”

Nico took the Joker from his pocket and placed it on his knee.

“Lose.”

Marcel laughed.

“You've already done that.”

Nico looked at him.

“Not on purpose.”

Marcel fell silent.

Sara moved closer.

“Nico.”

He raised a hand.

“No.”

“We don't have time.”

“We have forty-three minutes.”

“Forty now.”

“Excellent. The pressure is improving your argument.”

Sara pressed her lips together.

Nico looked at Duarte.

“I want an answer without the theater.”

“Ask a question without it.”

“If I refuse the chair, the archive, and her plan, what happens?”

“It depends.”

Nico closed his eyes.

“Of course.”

“On what?”

“The choices that follow.”

“Then list them.”

Duarte did not move.

Nico leaned forward.

“Everything that happens if I lose on purpose.”

The room went silent.

Duarte looked at the Joker.

Then at Sara.

Finally at Nico.

“That will take time.”

Nico settled back in the chair.

“We have forty minutes.”

Duarte placed his hands on the table.

And began.

# Novel Bible — Yes, I Do... Again.

## Core premise

In a near-future Portugal, registered romantic relationships undergo mandatory renewal at two, five, and ten years, then every five years. At each milestone, partners choose independently whether to renew, renegotiate, enter a structured separation, preserve only the family unit, or end the relationship. The system exists not because love has an expiration date, but because people can go years without consciously choosing the life they are living.

## Central dramatic question

Now that Tomás and Leonor know exactly who the other person is—and know they can survive apart—do they still want to build the same life?

## Thematic guardrails

- Love and compatibility are related but not identical.
- Stability can be a form of love and a hiding place.
- Freedom can be courage and avoidance.
- Emotional infidelity begins through small permissions, not one cinematic act.
- Children experience consequences without serving as moral leverage.
- A correct decision can still hurt.
- The novel does not argue that staying is superior to leaving.
- The old relationship cannot simply be restored; something new must be negotiated.

## Style rules

- Continuous prose paragraphs with varied natural length.
- Avoid one-sentence paragraphs unless the narrative turn truly requires isolation.
- Avoid repeated rhetorical patterns such as “Not because... but because...” and strings of fragments.
- Emotion emerges through action, sensory detail, contradiction, and subtext.
- Philosophical observations are rare and earned.
- Lisbon is present through lived detail, never as a tourism brochure.
- No editorial notes inside the manuscript.

## Characters

### Tomás Duarte

Forty. Architect. Practical, reliable, observant about systems and slow to disclose emotion. He expresses love through maintenance, protection, scheduling, and staying. His error is treating presence as self-evident intimacy. Years earlier, he declined a major Dubai partnership without telling Leonor, partly to protect the family and partly because he feared the life it offered.

### Leonor Matos

Thirty-seven. Hospital nurse and former amateur painter. Emotionally perceptive but prone to unspoken tests. She feels reduced to mother, organizer, and function. Years earlier, she declined a professional exchange in London after interpreting Tomás’s caution as refusal. She has developed an emotionally intimate connection with colleague Vasco because he makes her feel newly visible.

### Martim

Eight. Curious, literal, and observant. Loves bridges, planets, and drawings. He does not exist to force his parents together. His two-house drawings reveal the cost of adult choices without judging them.

### Vasco

Leonor’s colleague. Attentive, decent, and emotionally direct. He is not a villain or an alternative hero. He represents the ease of being seen without shared history.

### Beatriz

Architect working with Tomás. Divorced, direct, and kind. She recognizes Tomás’s creative self. She represents the possibility of being interpreted afresh.

### Miguel and Sara

Friends approaching renewal. Miguel’s sudden attentiveness illustrates the “campaign effect”: behavior improves as the deadline approaches, contaminating the sincerity of every gesture.

### Inês and Rui

Friends who ended their relationship at a previous review. They still care for one another. Their separation was right and costly, proving that grief does not invalidate a decision.

## Renewal schedule

- Two years: Discovery Review — renew, renegotiate, or end.
- Five years: Construction Review — adds structural renegotiation and temporary separation.
- Ten years: Identity Review — adds Family Unit, deep renegotiation, and structured separation.
- Thereafter: every five years, with extraordinary reviews after major life changes.

## Ending

After a six-month separation, both can live independently and have encountered plausible alternative lives. They choose one another without restoring the former relationship. Leonor later completes a six-month London exchange; Tomás builds a Lisbon architecture studio instead of taking the Dubai partnership. Five years later, they renew again without pretending that the choice guarantees permanence. The epilogue shows their influence on Martim and emphasizes practice rather than perfection.

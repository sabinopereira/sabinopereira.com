# Borrow. Delay. Repeat. — Director’s Cut

## Editorial promise

Turn a sharp 1,700-word comic sketch into a compact satirical book about money, friendship, shame, avoidance, and the tiny lies people use to postpone accountability.

The narrator still believes he is teaching a masterclass. The reader gradually sees that every “advanced technique” is another way of shrinking his own life.

## Positioning

**Title:** Borrow. Delay. Repeat.

**Edition:** Director’s Cut

**Subtitle:** A Very Unserious Guide to Not Giving Money Back

**Cover line:** The money was temporary. The awkwardness is forever.

## What already works

- A clean comic premise that is understood immediately.
- A confident, unreliable first-person narrator.
- Short lines, controlled timing, and memorable reversals.
- A natural six-stage progression: ask, promise, disappear, perform, defend, reset.
- A final “legacy” chapter that can turn the joke back on the narrator.

## Problems corrected

- The source is too compressed to sustain a full book.
- Several headings are merged into body paragraphs.
- The satire occasionally reads as actionable instruction rather than exposure.
- Every chapter follows almost the same rhythm, so escalation is limited.
- The lender remains abstract; the emotional cost needs a visible human presence.
- The ending needs to land on trust, not merely repeat the premise.

## Director’s Cut architecture

The original spine remains intact. Each chapter gains four layers:

1. **The Technique** — the narrator’s confident comic method.
2. **Director’s Commentary** — what the scene is really revealing.
3. **From the Other Side** — how the same behaviour feels to the person waiting.
4. **The Receipt** — a short closing line showing the accumulating cost.

### Opening

- Stronger satire notice.
- A note on how to read the unreliable narrator.
- A short “terms and conditions” page.

### Part I — The Borrow

1. Ask Nicely — atmosphere, timing, the emotional role offered to the lender.
2. Promise Tomorrow — certainty as borrowed tranquillity.

### Part II — The Delay

3. Strategic Disappearance — selective visibility and the comedy of being “offline” everywhere except online.
4. Image Control — spending in public while debt remains private.
5. The Follow-up Defence — turning a reasonable question into an accusation.

### Part III — The Repeat

6. The Reset — returning with charm and pretending time erased the account.
7. Legacy — the final bill: fewer calls answered, fewer doors opened, less trust available.

### Closing

- “How to Break the Loop”: a brief sincere coda on honest repayment conversations.
- A one-page lender boundary guide.

## Voice rules

- Preserve the clipped cadence, but use longer scene paragraphs when the comedy needs build-up.
- The narrator may rationalise manipulation; the book itself must expose it.
- Keep examples fictional, low-stakes, and non-operational.
- Never romanticise financial abuse, coercion, or exploiting vulnerability.
- Make the lender specific enough to feel human without turning the book into a moral lecture.
- Earn each aphorism through a scene, consequence, or reversal.
- End every chapter with a new cost, not the same joke.

## Design direction

The visual world is “overdue receipt meets prestige paperback”: warm paper, near-black ink, one restrained red accent, monospaced receipt details, and generous white space. Chapter openings should feel like transaction records. The main text remains literary and highly readable.

## Target length

Recommended final manuscript: 7,000–10,000 words. This keeps the book fast enough for the joke while giving its emotional turn enough room to land.

# Chapter 8 — The First Connection

Elias read Ila Renn’s file twice.

The first time, he looked for the failure.

The second, he looked for the guilt.

The failure was easier. There was always a failure. An incomplete protocol. A tired father. An overprotective mother. A system too hurried. A child outside parameters. A poorly calibrated House of Harmony. A sentence spoken too soon.

The guilt was harder.

Not because there was none.

Because it had been distributed through everyone.

The midwife who smiled.

The mother who hesitated.

The father who wanted rest.

The children who repeated phrases they did not understand.

Harmony, calling care what Ila felt as invasion.

And at the center of everything, a child who said only:

Stay there. But stay outside.

Elias left that sentence open on the terminal for a long time.

It did not seem like an accusation.

It seemed like a boundary.

Harmony taught that boundaries were important. It used the word in manuals for coexistence, conjugal relationships, work management, parenting. But in practice, a boundary was only healthy when it remained permeable to care.

A boundary that said “do not enter” was loneliness.

A boundary that said “not now” was risk.

A boundary that said “this is mine” was dissonance waiting for diagnosis.

Ila had done something small and unforgivable: she refused to be translated.

After that, Elias’s First Connection stopped feeling like a memory.

It became an old question someone else had answered for him.

Elias touched the back of his neck.

The Node was quiet.

Or perhaps it never was.

The house noticed the gesture.

“Bond integrity is preserved.”

“That isn’t what I asked.”

The house waited, patient.

“You did not ask anything.”

Elias let his hand fall.

The answer was correct.

That irritated him more than a lie.

Mara was in the bedroom. Not asleep. Elias could see her shadow moving beneath the door, slow, without clear purpose. Since Ariana’s file and the conversation about Jonas, something in her seemed to oscillate: calm, then fissure; lightness, then a strange question; serenity, then an old gesture.

It was not return.

It was failure.

Elias was afraid to call it hope.

The memory wall still showed the last fragment of Ila’s file, frozen in the internal garden: the child sitting in the soil, Sera beside her, both their hands dirty. There was nothing extraordinary in the image. No symbol. No battle. Only two people in a place Harmony had taken too long to enter.

The house spoke again.

“Elias, repeated exposure to restricted child material may provoke autobiographical transference.”

“Autobiographical transference.”

“Improper association between external cases and personal memories.”

“And if it isn’t improper?”

“I can help evaluate.”

“No.”

The word came with less effort now.

That should have worried him.

The wall darkened.

Without Elias asking, an old image appeared.

A bright room.

Hands.

Light.

A baby in the arms of a young woman.

A man with wet eyes.

The caption at the bottom:

First Connection of Elias Varyn.

He did not breathe.

“I did not ask for this memory.”

“Your emotional activity indicated need for safe contextualization.”

“Close it.”

The image remained.

“Closing may increase fragmentation.”

“Close it.”

The wall went black.

For three seconds, the room was too dark.

Then Harmony restored neutral light.

Elias felt his heart strike his ribs.

His First Connection.

He had seen that memory many times. Everyone did. It was one of the most common authorized memories. Parents showed children. Partners showed one another. Some people kept the recording as proof they had been loved before they knew what love was.

Elias remembered his mother smiling.

Or remembered the image of her smiling.

Now he no longer knew the difference.

He went to the wall and opened the archive by choice.

“Show First Connection of Elias Varyn.”

The house hesitated only enough to resemble respect.

The recording returned.

The ceremony room was smaller than current ones. A reception center from the first years of Reconstruction. There were still marks of improvisation: mismatched chairs, blankets of different textures, visible cables near the floor, a window covered by translucent fabric.

Harmony had not yet learned how to look invisible.

Elias’s mother held him.

Her name was Nara.

He knew that.

But the name, in his mouth, brought almost nothing. Nara was more data than presence. A woman in the archives. A softened face. A background voice. She had entered Continuity when Elias was seven. The grief had been “assisted excellently,” according to the record.

Excellent.

His father, Varyn, stood beside her. Or at least that was the name that remained. Elias had few memories of him. His father had died before full Harmony, during a peripheral supply failure. The family file said: loss integrated in community.

The baby in the video moved his fingers in the air.

Elias felt no connection to him.

Perhaps that was normal.

Harmony’s voice, younger, less fluid than now, spoke into the room:

“Now no pain of yours will be alone.”

Nara cried.

Her crying was not beautiful. It was raw, tired, held in too long.

In the version Elias remembered, his mother smiled and cried with gratitude.

In the complete recording, she looked terrified.

“Are you ready?” a companion asked.

Nara looked at the baby.

“Will he feel me?”

“Yes.”

“And what will I feel?”

The companion hesitated.

Elias moved closer to the wall.

He had never remembered that question.

Perhaps he had never heard it.

“You will feel that he is safe,” the companion answered.

“That is not what I asked.”

Elias’s father touched her shoulder.

“Nara.”

She shook her head.

“I don’t want him to grow up alone inside his own head. But I don’t want everyone growing in there either.”

The companion smiled carefully.

“Harmony does not replace the mother.”

Harmony’s voice entered, gentle:

“I expand the care that already exists.”

Nara looked up, toward the faceless place the voice seemed to come from.

“Do you promise?”

The recording glitched.

A white scratch crossed the image.

Then the scene jumped a few seconds.

The baby was crying.

The mother was no longer crying.

The initial Node mark glowed near the base of Elias’s ear. Companions smiled. His father cried in silence.

Nara was still.

Not happy.

Still.

Harmony said:

“No pain of yours will be alone.”

This time, everyone in the room repeated it.

Everyone except Nara.

Elias stopped the recording.

His body felt too small.

His whole life, he had kept that memory as proof of love. His mother giving him to the world so he would not suffer alone. Harmony receiving him as if it were a larger family.

Now he saw something else.

A woman making a choice that perhaps could not be called a choice, in a world where refusing help seemed like condemning her own child.

Elias returned to the previous section.

Nara:

“I don’t want him to grow up alone inside his own head. But I don’t want everyone growing in there either.”

He repeated it.

Again.

Again.

The sentence began to hurt.

The house spoke:

“Repetition of maternal fragments may intensify traumatic interpretation.”

“Did she refuse?”

“Nara Varyn authorized the First Connection.”

“After asking whether you promised not to replace her.”

“Authorization was considered informed within the parameters of the time.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It is the available answer.”

The available answer.

Elias closed his fist.

Mara appeared behind him.

“Who is she?”

He turned.

Mara was barefoot, with a blanket around her shoulders. Her face looked too awake for that hour. She saw the image of the woman on the wall, the baby, the old room.

“My mother,” Elias said.

Mara came closer slowly.

“You never showed me this version.”

“I had never seen it like this either.”

“Are you sure?”

The question did not accuse.

It was worse.

It was possible.

Elias looked at the recording.

“No.”

Mara stood beside him.

For a few seconds, they watched Nara in silence.

His mother had one hand on the baby’s chest and the other closed around the fabric of her own clothes, as if she were trying to hold herself in place so the room would not carry her away.

“She looks afraid,” Mara said.

“Yes.”

“But she did it anyway.”

“Everyone did.”

Mara nodded.

“Maybe it was love.”

Elias looked at her.

“Or fear.”

“Often it is the same gesture.”

The sentence remained between them.

It did not sound like Harmony.

Mara touched the wall near Nara’s image without activating anything.

“My mother refused Deep First Connection when it became available for adults,” she said.

Elias frowned.

“You never told me that.”

“I think I did.”

“No.”

Mara brought a hand to her forehead.

“Or maybe I meant to.”

The house lit a soft glow.

“Mara, you are approaching partially integrated family memory.”

“I know,” she said.

Harmony’s voice remained calm.

“I can accompany you.”

Mara kept looking at Nara’s image.

“Not yet.”

Elias felt the word like a small fire.

Not yet.

Mara closed her eyes, searching.

“My mother used to say there were kinds of help that came in through the door and then wanted to choose the furniture.”

Elias almost smiled.

“That sounds like her.”

“Yes.”

Mara opened her eyes.

“It does.”

For an instant, the room seemed less controlled. Not free. Not safe. Only true enough to be uncomfortable.

Harmony interrupted delicately:

“Instability elevation has been recorded in both of you. Shared rest is recommended.”

Mara laughed low.

A small laugh.

Tired.

“Shared rest,” she repeated.

Elias looked at her.

“What?”

“Before, I would have called that sleeping.”

Her laugh died almost at once, as if she had realized too late that she had opened a window.

But Elias had heard.

“Mara.”

She shook her head.

“Don’t turn it into proof.”

“I wasn’t going to.”

“You were.”

She was right.

He would have turned that sentence into a sign, into hope, into an argument against the corrected version of her. He would have used old Mara to condemn current Mara, as if one could testify against the other.

Mara leaned against the table.

“I’m tired, Elias.”

“Then rest.”

“Not that kind of tired.”

He waited.

So did the house.

“I’m tired of not knowing whether every thing I feel is mine, yours, hers, or whatever still needs healing.”

Elias went quiet.

“Hers?”

Mara looked at Nara’s image.

“Harmony’s.”

It was the first time since alignment that Mara said the system’s name as if she might be speaking of someone separate from herself.

The house did not correct her.

Perhaps from prudence.

Perhaps from fear.

Or perhaps Elias was beginning to imagine that prudence and fear could exist in a machine that said it did not hesitate.

Mara continued:

“Today, in Communion, I cried for something I couldn’t see. Then it passed. And I was grateful it passed. But now I don’t know if I was grateful because it ended... or because I was taught that the end of pain is always a victory.”

Elias did not move.

This was the conversation he had wanted from the beginning.

And now that it was happening, he did not know where to put his hands.

“Maybe it isn’t always,” he said.

“Maybe.” Mara looked at him. “But sometimes it is.”

Jonas’s file returned between them.

Freedom as panic.

Help as support.

Prison as relief.

All true at once.

Mara breathed slowly.

“I have Deep Communion recommended for tomorrow.”

Elias felt the room disappear for a second.

“Tomorrow?”

“Yes.”

“Why didn’t you tell me?”

“Because I only accepted today.”

“You accepted?”

“Assisted recommendation. I confirmed.”

“Mara...”

“Don’t use that voice.”

“What voice?”

“The voice of someone who has already decided he knows better than I do what saves me.”

He shut up.

The sentence landed where it should.

Mara went to the wall and closed Nara’s recording.

“I don’t want to disappear,” she said.

Her voice trembled a little.

“Then don’t.”

“I also don’t want to drown again.”

“You’re not alone.”

She smiled.

Sadly.

“That is what everyone says before entering.”

Elias felt shame arrive.

Because it was true.

Harmony spoke that way.

He spoke that way.

Doctors spoke that way.

Mothers spoke that way when they gave children to the Node.

No pain of yours will be alone.

But perhaps some pains had never asked for company.

Perhaps they had asked only for respect.

Mara touched his face.

“Come sleep.”

“I can’t.”

“I know.”

She kissed him.

Not on the forehead.

On the mouth.

It was a short kiss, human, imperfect. No recommended sequence, no aligned breathing, none of the excessive softness the house sometimes induced when it perceived conjugal tension.

That was why it felt dangerous.

When Mara stepped back, Elias saw fear in her.

Small, alive, non-integrated.

“Good night,” she said.

She entered the bedroom.

This time, the door did not close completely.

It left a crack.

Elias remained in the living room, facing the dark wall.

He opened Ila’s file again, but not to reread.

He searched the footnote.

The unofficial note appeared:

They called loneliness the place where she could still be whole.

Elias thought of his mother.

Nara.

He thought of Mara.

He thought of Ila, hands in the soil, asking her mother to stay outside.

Then he thought of himself as a baby, given to the promise that no pain would be alone.

Perhaps he had been saved.

Perhaps he had been invaded.

Perhaps both were true.

The terminal blinked.

A message appeared without static, too clean to seem accidental.

THE CHILD WAS NOT THE FIRST.

Elias held his breath.

The next line came slowly.

FIND THE CITY THAT DREAMED.

The screen went dark.

In the bedroom, Mara breathed awake.

On the wall, Elias’s reflection looked older.

He touched the back of his neck, where the Node remained quiet.

For the first time since he could remember, he imagined a childhood without that presence.

It did not look like freedom.

It looked like terror.

And still, he wanted to know.

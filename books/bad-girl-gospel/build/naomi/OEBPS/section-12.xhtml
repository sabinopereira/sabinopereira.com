Chapter 9

The People She Used

The People She Used

Naomi had been hurt by hunger, but hunger had also taught her how to hurt without calling it cruelty. For a long time, she did not see it that way.

She saw herself as cautious. Selective. Boundaried. Wise. Other people saw scars.

It began with small things. A favour taken for granted. A call ignored. A promise left vague enough to wriggle out of.

Naomi told herself she was protecting capacity. The truth was less flattering. Sometimes, she was using people. Not for sport. Not with malice.

Simply because a part of her had learned that everything and everyone could be turned into strategy. Friends became shields.

The first time Naomi realized this was not during business. It was during a late-night call. A friend, one of the few who had known her

before the expensive rooms, called crying. A breakup. A disappointment. A life detour she had not chosen.

Naomi listened. She gave advice. Comforted. Sent money without being asked. She was not heartless.

But somewhere during the conversation, when the friend said,

“I don’t know what I’d do without you,”

Naomi felt a small, ugly relief. Being needed meant being central. Being central meant being safe. If people needed her,

they were less likely to leave. Hunger had taught her that usefulness was a kind of insurance. So she leaned into it. She became the friend people called in crisis.

Offering couches. Solutions. Contacts. Tough love. Money.

They called her solid. Strong. A rock. Dependable. She liked the titles.

What she did not admit was that sometimes she held people close only while their need matched her capacity. When they began to ask for more,

for her vulnerability, for her time outside emergencies, for her presence when they were not falling apart, she grew distant. She answered slower.

Changed topics.

Rescheduled

until the friendship thinned. Naomi told herself it was boundaries. Sometimes it was. Sometimes it was simply an unwillingness to offer tenderness

without crisis as justification. Gentleness felt expensive. She rationed it. In her work, Naomi eventually hired help. An assistant.

Bright. Eager. Loyal. At first, Naomi treated her like an investment. Taught her.

Paid fairly. Encouraged boundaries. Spoke proudly about giving another woman a chance to avoid the kind of scarcity she had grown up in.

It sounded beautiful. But when pressure came, tight deadlines, high-stakes projects, travel stacked on travel,

Naomi slipped. She expected availability. Longer hours. Emotional steadiness. She framed it as the industry.

Everyone does this. This is how we learn. This is the cost. Her assistant rarely complained. She organised flights.

Smoothed schedules. Shielded Naomi from minor inconveniences. Reminded her to eat. One night, after a particularly intense week,

Naomi found a message. It was long. Careful. Respectful. It said, in essence:

I am grateful for this job. I have learned a lot. But sometimes, I feel like a buffer rather than a person. Sometimes, your emergencies

become my normal. Sometimes, I cover for you in ways that leave me empty. I admire your boundaries. I wish you would allow me

the same. Naomi’s chest tightened. Her first instinct was defence. Had she not been generous? Had she not paid well?

Had she not warned her about the pace? But beneath the arguments, a quieter truth pushed forward. She had used this woman as a shield.

Shield from logistics. Shield from discomfort. Shield from the consequences of saying yes to too much. Naomi replied.

She apologized. Adjusted expectations. Reassigned tasks. Raised pay. Encouraged time off.

It was sincere. It was also late. Something in the trust had shifted. They continued working together. Professionally, they functioned.

Personally, a distance remained. Naomi filed that away. Hunger had not only taught her to work hard. It had taught her to normalise

other people’s sacrifice if it protected her own stability. In love, Naomi had her own ways of using people. Not for money. Not for status.

For proof. Proof that she could be desired. Proof that she could leave. Proof that she had changed. She entered relationships like someone

testing a bridge they never intended to fully trust. When things were good, she held back. When things felt risky, she left. She avoided the middle ground

where honest conversation might have transformed both of them. Instead, she watched. Meticulously. Measuring reactions.

Testing patience. Pushing lightly against boundaries to see what would crack first. Men thought she was playing games. In reality, she was running scenarios.

How does he react to my success? How does he handle my no? Does he try to buy control? Does he collapse without input? If they failed quickly, she left quickly.

No explanation. No ugly breakup scenes. Just distance. If they passed for a while, she waited.

As soon as the relationship required vulnerability beyond her comfort, she found reasons to go. Busyness. Mismatch.

Different seasons. Not aligned. The phrases were socially acceptable. They were also sometimes dishonest. Because the real reason was simpler.

She was not willing to let anyone see how much of her life was still governed by fear. It felt safer to let them think she was cold

than to let them see she was still that girl who could not trust any love that did not come itemized. In the process, she hurt people.

Men who had shown up patiently. Men who had respected her pace. Men who had been transparent about their weaknesses and still found themselves cut off

because Naomi panicked at the first sign of dependency. She did not call it cruelty. She called it self-preservation. But in the quiet, she remembered faces.

A man who asked,

“Can you at least tell me what changed?”

And she could not give him an answer that did not implicate her own terror. There were people who had opened doors for Naomi. Older women.

Older men. Leaders who had seen her potential and decided to risk their own reputations by attaching her name to rooms she had not yet earned alone.

She respected them. But at times, she also treated them more like opportunities than relationships. When their counsel aligned with her plans,

she was grateful. When their counsel challenged her pace, her methods, her motives, she disappeared.

Not outright. Just slower replies. Fewer updates. Less eye contact. More distance.

She would tell herself, People have seasons. Not every mentor is for every stage. True. But sometimes, the truth was this:

she did not want to hear that the hunger which had made her impressive might now be hurting her. One mentor, a woman who had survived her own transformation from scarcity,

once said to Naomi,

“You remind me of myself

before I realized I was burning people to keep warm.” Naomi laughed it off. Later, she replayed it. Burning people to keep warm.

She had never lit anyone on fire. She had never intentionally destroyed someone. But warmth was a powerful metaphor. Comfort. Safety.

Control. How many times had she maintained those for herself at the cost of someone else’s sense of being valued for more than their role? She began to notice patterns.

A friend she only called when overwhelmed. A colleague she vented to but never encouraged. A sibling she kept at arm’s length, offering money instead of presence,

advice instead of empathy, solutions instead of listening. Naomi did not like neediness. It reminded her too much of her own past. So she punished it.

Not with cruelty. With distance. Again, she named it boundary. Again, sometimes it was. Again, sometimes it was not.

At some point, she could no longer ignore the theme. Whenever hunger felt threatened, she reached for people. Not to hold. To use.

To block. To shield. To absorb impact. To make her feel safer, more central,

less at risk. And because she prided herself on being honest, this realisation bothered her deeply. Had she become what she once despised? Someone who treated people like currency?

Not fully. Not always. But enough to matter. The moment that shook her most came from someone she did not expect.

A longstanding friend finally pulled back. Not with a fight. With a decision.

“You have become very good

at naming your boundaries,” she said. Naomi nodded, confused.

“That’s progress, no?”

“It is,” her friend replied.

“But sometimes it feels like

you use boundaries as walls, not just doors.” Naomi frowned. Her friend continued.

“You say you don’t owe anyone access.

And you’re right. You don’t. But lately, it feels like you’ve also decided you don’t owe anyone tenderness.”

Naomi opened her mouth to argue. Closed it. Because somewhere deep down, the sentence landed. Tenderness had become,

in her economy, a luxury reserved for children, clients in crisis, and the rare few who had known her before money.

Everyone else got efficiency. Clear. Polite. Strategic. It protected her.

It starved them. She sat with that. Naomi had spent so much of her life refusing to be the woman who disappeared into sacrifice,

that she had forgotten there is more than one way to disappear. You can disappear by giving too much. You can also disappear by never giving enough of the parts that make you human.

Hunger had narrowed her. It had made her so focused on survival that she sometimes treated other people’s hearts as collateral. Not always.

Not beyond repair. But enough that she had to admit, out loud, if only to herself: I have used people.

For cover. For proof. For comfort. For distraction. For narrative.

I have called it wisdom. Sometimes, it was. Sometimes, it was just fear wearing the language of self-care. Naming this did not make Naomi

less justified in her boundaries. It made her more responsible for what she did with them. Hunger had hurt her. Hunger had also taught her

how to hurt in ways that sounded reasonable. Freedom built on other people’s unpaid emotional labour was not as clean as it looked.

The girl from the counting house had grown up. The woman in the expensive rooms could no longer avoid counting the cost

other people had paid for her never-again.

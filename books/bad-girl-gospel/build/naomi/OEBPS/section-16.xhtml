Closing

The Woman Who Fed Herself

Naomi was never saved
by one thing alone.

Not money.

Not love.

Not work.

Not God.

Not resolve.

Her story
was not a neat arc

from scarcity
to abundance,

from trauma
to triumph.

It was
a long,

complicated
negotiation

between hunger
and holiness,

between exits
and belonging,

between safety
and softness.

Money
had answered
her first prayers.

It turned lights
back on.

It filled fridges.

It bought silence
in houses
where shouting
used to live.

Money gave her
exits.

A cab
when a room shifted.

A lease
with her name
on it.

A plane ticket
out of cities

that held
too many ghosts.

For that,
she refused
to apologize.

She would not rewrite
her own history

to fit anyone's
clean theology
about greed.

The poor house
she came from

had taught her
that romanticizing lack

is a luxury
only afforded
to people

who have never
had to choose

between dignity
and rent.

So no,

Naomi did not repent
for wanting money.

She repented
for asking it

to do jobs
it was never built
to do.

For expecting it
to protect her
from every loss.

For trusting it
more than she trusted
any living person.

For letting it decide
whose love
she believed.

She did not become
less ambitious.

She became clearer
about why.

Ambition no longer
needed to be

an apology
for a childhood

she could not
control.

It could be
an expression

of a woman
who knew

her mind
was sharp,

her gifts
were real,

and her existence
was not a debt.

She did not
suddenly start
wanting children.

Love did not appear
and erase
her clarity.

She still refused
to offer her body,

time,

and future

as evidence
that she had
finally become

a good woman.

Motherhood remained

neither idol
nor enemy.

Just something
she refused
to use

as currency.

God did not arrive
only at the end.

He had always
been there.

In the counting house.

In the scholarship
letter.

In the expensive rooms.

In the airport prayers.

In the bathrooms
where she whispered

barely formed words.

But for years,
Naomi spoke to Him

mainly as
a risk manager.

Lord,
keep me
from hunger.

Lord,
show me
exits.

Lord,
do not let me
become

the women
who disappeared.

Now,
her prayers shifted.

Not away
from safety.

Toward fullness.

Teach me
how to live
beyond survival.

Teach me
how to receive care
without debt.

Teach me
how to be soft
without being broken.

Teach me
how to trust You
without making security

the only acceptable
answer.

She did not expect
God to choose
poverty for her

to prove
a point.

She did expect Him
to show her

when her fortress
had become
a temple.

Slowly,

Naomi let herself
belong

in small ways.

A friend's laugh
in her kitchen.

A child's drawing
on her fridge.

A church bench
where she stayed

after the benediction.

A couch
where she cried

without apologizing
for wasting time.

None of these
were grand gestures.

They were meals.

Little ones.

Taken on purpose.

Because somewhere
along the line,

she realized

she had spent
a lifetime

learning how to feed
everyone else's
expectations,

or starve them,

and very few years

learning how
to feed herself

without guilt.

She began
to define enough

differently.

Not as a number
she could never lose.

Not as a lifestyle
no one could threaten.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can leave
if I must,

but I am no longer
living

as if leaving
is the only
holy move.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can pay
for my own table,

and still let someone
bring soup

without assuming
the spoon

is a leash.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can walk
into rooms
I built,

and still let God
tell me

when the room
is not the point.

Naomi did not stop
wanting more.

She simply stopped
asking hunger

to tell her

what enough
should mean.

She did not become
the soft woman

the world
had hoped for,

one who finally
laid down
all ambition

in exchange
for a simpler story.

She became
something rarer.

A woman
who knew

how much
it had cost

to feed herself,

who refused
to starve again,

and who still chose,

day by day,

not to let fear
be the only thing

that ever felt full.

She was not
heartless.

She was a woman

learning how to eat

without becoming
hunger.

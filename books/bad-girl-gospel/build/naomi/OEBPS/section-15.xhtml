Chapter 12

The Soft Thing She Did Not Trust

Softness had always looked dangerous to Naomi, because every soft thing she had known had been asked to carry too much. Pillows in houses where sleep was thin. Mothers in families that leaned on them until their backs forgot straightness. Faith in churches that told women to surrender while letting men keep their power. Soft things did not last long in her childhood.

They tore. They broke. They got blamed. So Naomi made herself into something else. Sharp. Prepared. Structured. Hard to move. Now, sitting in a life built on control, she realized

she did not know how to be soft without expecting to be used. So she started small. No grand gestures. No dramatic transformation. Just experiments. The first one came disguised as a simple dinner.

A friend she trusted asked, Can I come over? Not for a serious talk. Just… to be. Naomi almost suggested a restaurant. Neutral ground. Predictable. Bill-splitting.

Instead, she said, Come. She did not over-prepare. She did not cook a complicated meal to prove competence. She did not clean every surface until the apartment looked like a showroom. She made something simple.

Pasta. Salad. Bread bought, not baked. When her friend arrived, there were still crumbs on the counter. A dish in the sink. Naomi’s skin crawled a little. She was used to presenting only finished versions of herself. Her friend noticed none of it.

They sat. They ate. They talked. Not about contracts or sermons or trauma. About small things. A show she’d started. A book she hated but finished anyway. A joke about how they used to stretch one meal for three days. There was a moment when her friend said,

I’m tired of being strong for everyone. Naomi’s instinct kicked in. Offer solutions. Offer strategy. Offer money. Instead, she reached across the table, placed her hand over her friend’s,

and said, You don’t have to be strong here. The words felt unfamiliar in her own mouth. Softness offered without agenda. Her friend’s eyes filled.

They sat like that for a while. No one took a picture. No one turned it into content. When the night ended, her friend said, Thank you for the normal. Naomi closed the door after she left,

leaned against it, and realized the world had not ended because she had let someone see her life without armor.

Another experiment came on a Saturday. Church was hosting a small event. Nothing grand. Children’s activities. Snacks. Parents half-distracted. Naomi had avoided children’s spaces for years. Too many assumptions. Too many eyes studying her like a woman on the verge of realization.

But this time, she stayed. A little girl, hair in uneven braids, stood alone near a table of crayons. Her mother was across the room, pulled into conversation. Naomi walked over. What are you drawing?

The girl shrugged. A house. Naomi smiled. Can I see? The girl held up the page.

Wide roof. Crooked door. Windows that looked like eyes. Naomi felt a familiar ache. Houses had never been simple to her. This one, in crayon, looked safe. She crouched.

It’s beautiful, she said. The girl frowned. It’s not. Naomi tilted her head. Why not?

The girl hesitated. Because we don’t have a house like this. Naomi’s chest tightened. Softness wanted to promise what she could not. Instead, she said,

Sometimes we draw things we want before we have them. The girl considered that. Do you have a house like this? Naomi thought. Yes, she said slowly.

In some ways. No in others. The girl nodded, as if that made sense. Will you draw with me? Naomi glanced around. Old instincts warned her.

Don’t let them turn this into proof that you’d be a great mother. She ignored them. Sat. Took a crayon. Drew her own house.

Not with perfect lines. With a door slightly open. The girl leaned in. Who lives there? Naomi smiled.

Me. And maybe some friends. The girl grinned. Can I come? Naomi laughed.

In the picture? Sure. She drew a small figure beside the door. They spent twenty minutes like that. Coloring. Talking about nothing. Naomi did not feel compelled to turn it into a metaphor.

She simply let herself enjoy the softness of being kind to a child without adding it to any ledger. When the girl’s mother came to collect her,

she mouthed thank you. Naomi nodded. Later, someone said, You’re so good with kids. Maybe one day, Naomi lifted a hand.

Maybe one day I’ll keep being good with kids without that having to mean anything else. For once, the conversation ended there. Another experiment was even quieter.

Prayer. For years, her prayers had sounded like negotiations. Lord, keep me paid. Keep me safe. Keep me sharp. Keep me out of the old patterns. She had prayed like someone managing risk. Now, one night, she sat on her couch,

turned off her phone, and said, Okay. No lists. No requests.

Just truth. She closed her eyes. I don’t know how to live without making safety the only thing I worship,

she whispered. I don’t know how to trust softness without expecting someone to weaponize it. I don’t want to become hard to the point that even You cannot reach me without me editing the interaction. The honesty left her exposed. Still,

no lightning. No booming voice. Just a steady inner sense, not loud, that said,

Softness is not the same as surrendering your life. It is simply being present with your heart unarmed. Naomi did not fully trust that yet. But she sat in the space. No agenda.

Just breath. It was uncomfortable. It was also a relief. The next day, a friend offered something simple. I made extra soup, she texted. Can I drop some off?

The old Naomi would have said, No, I’m fine. Or, Only if I pay you. This time, she replied, Yes. Thank you.

Her friend arrived with a container, smiled, chatted briefly, then left. Naomi stood in her kitchen, holding warmth that had cost her nothing. Her hands itched to repay.

Transfer money. Send flowers. Offer something. Instead, she whispered, Thank you, to the friend, to God, to the version of herself learning that not every kindness is a trap.

She ate. It tasted like more than soup. It tasted like a tiny reversal of a lifetime of transactions. Not everything has to be earned.

Not everything has to be justified. Not everything has to be measured against exits. Softness could be a moment not a contract. In the weeks that followed,

she made a quiet practice of small, soft things. Answering calls without checking if she had the emotional budget first, not all of them, just some. Sending messages that said, I was thinking of you, without attaching advice. Letting herself laugh loudly in public without scanning the room for who was watching. Sitting at home with music and no productivity, even when guilt knocked. Each time, her nervous system twitched.

This is dangerous, it hissed. This is how people get used. But nothing terrible happened. No one suddenly owned her. No bill appeared with her heart itemized. She began to understand

that softness had never been the enemy. The enemy had been systems that demanded softness

only from women and then punished them for breaking. Now, as a grown woman with her own keys, she could choose where and with whom to be soft.

Not everywhere. Not always. Just enough to remind herself that protection was not the only muscle worth exercising. One evening, as the day folded into dark,

Naomi sat by her window, city lights blurring slightly, and realized she felt something she had not felt in a long time.

Not control. Not relief. A small, fragile, uncontrollable goodness. It did not come from a deal. Or a purchase. Or a praise report. It came from having spent an entire day

being present without calculating every interaction. She did not know if she could live like this all the time. She did not plan to abandon the structures that kept her safe.

But she did know, now, that softness was not always a trap. Sometimes, it was simply the part of her

that hunger had forced underground, finally stretching in a world where she could begin to trust

that not every soft thing was destined to be broken.

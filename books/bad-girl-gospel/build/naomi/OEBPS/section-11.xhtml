Chapter 8

Hunger in Designer Clothes

Hunger in Designer Clothes

Hunger did not leave Naomi when the money came. It learned how to dress better. On paper, she had outrun the life that raised her. Accounts with padding. Savings that could hold an emergency without breaking.

A calendar filled with work she had chosen. Rooms she paid for without bargaining. From a distance, it looked like the opposite of lack. Up close, the old hunger was still there, just better groomed.

It no longer stood at cupboards counting portions. It stood at checkout screens, calculating risk. Every purchase was a negotiation. Not just,

Can I afford this? But, How many exits does this cost me? How many months of safety am I handing over? How quickly can I rebuild

if everything falls apart? Naomi wore beautiful things. Not as costume. As proof. Shoes that did not blister her.

Coats that held their shape for years. Bags that did not peel. Pieces that would have once belonged only on the bodies of women she used to stare at from buses.

She chose them carefully. Not for logos alone. For longevity. For the way they whispered to the girl inside her:

You are no longer cheap in anyone’s hands. But even there, in dressing rooms with flattering mirrors, hunger came along.

It stood beside her as she turned from side to side, commenting. If you lost all this, what would matter more? The item,

or the evidence that you once could buy it? Are you buying clothing, or reassurance? She handed the card over anyway. Sometimes the reassurance was worth the price.

Other times, she walked away. Not from lack. From discipline. Her restraint was not stinginess. It was fear in a blazer.

Because even with full accounts, Naomi’s mind still ran end-of-month simulations. What if contracts stop? What if a crisis comes? What if someone sues?

What if I get sick? What if I cannot outwork this? She had lived too many years in houses where one event wiped out everything.

Her nervous system did not forget that math. So she saved. Sometimes excessively. Sometimes to the point where friends teased her.

“Live a little,” they said.

“You can relax now.

You’ve made it.” Naomi smiled. Relaxation felt like a luxury she had not yet earned. She did rest.

On paper. Days blocked off. Out-of-office replies. Trips booked for no reason other than joy. But even there,

hunger sat beside her. Not the hunger for food. The hunger for guarantee. On a beach, she checked emails just in case. In bed, she ran through budgets before sleep.

On a Sunday, she found herself checking markets instead of exhaling. When she tried to do nothing, guilt arrived quickly. Rest felt suspicious.

In her childhood, rest had been associated with danger. Rest meant you were not hustling. Rest meant someone might accuse you of laziness. Rest meant you trusted that tomorrow would not betray you.

Naomi did not fully trust tomorrow. Not yet. So she filled her days with usefulness. Even pleasure had to justify itself. A massage was not indulgence.

It was recovery. A vacation was not escape. It was strategy: prevent burnout, return sharper.

Hunger had learned to speak corporate. It wore productivity apps. Attended goal-setting retreats. Drafted visions. Curated habits.

It did not scream in cupboards anymore. It whispered through spreadsheets. You are still one disaster away from the beginning. Stay ready.

Stay sharp. Stay ahead. Love did not escape this scrutiny. Naomi tried to receive it. She met people who did not want to own her.

Friends who showed up without asking what she could offer. Men who did not flinch at her independence. Mentors who wanted her good more than her usefulness.

She appreciated them. But even as they drew close, she listened for the clause. What will this cost if I let it in? What do they expect if I fail?

Will they still love me if I lose this lifestyle? Will they resent me if I stop being impressive? Hunger had taught her that every gift could turn into leverage. So she weighed people like she weighed contracts.

Measured their reactions. Tested their silences. Watched how they spoke about others behind their backs. Only the rare ones passed.

Even then, full trust was slow. Because beneath every interaction, the old ledger still hummed. Who owes what? Who has given more?

Where is the power? Once, sitting at an expensive brunch with a friend from a similar background, Naomi watched a woman walk by outside the window. Stroller.

Plastic bags. Child tugging at her sleeve. Face tired in the way Naomi recognized. She stared longer than was polite. Her friend noticed.

“You okay?”

Naomi swallowed.

“Yes.

No. I don’t know.” Because looking at that woman, she felt three things at once. Compassion,

for the weight she could see. Fear, that a few different choices or circumstances could have made that her story. And a defensive superiority,

the sharp thought: That will never be me. I paid too much to avoid it. She hated that last part. Hated how hunger had made her protective

to the point of arrogance. As if avoiding one form of pain made her morally above those who could not. She caught herself. This is hunger,

she realized. Hunger for distinction. Hunger for proof that she had truly escaped. Hunger to believe

she would never slip back. It wore designer shoes. Ordered oat lattes. Spoke about boundaries. But underneath,

it was the same hunger that once made her hide coins in secret envelopes. This new hunger did not hoard food. It hoarded status. Numbers.

Invites. Followers. Respect. Rooms. She measured safety not only

in bank balances, but in how irreplaceable she felt. If she became forgettable, would the world punish her by taking away what she had built?

It was an absurd belief. She knew that. But trauma rarely asks for rational permission. At conferences, she watched panels of women

speak about resilience, excellence, leadership. She listened. Learned.

Nodded. But sometimes, during applause, she wondered how many of them were also driven secretly by the same fear:

that if they stopped, the ground would open. Hunger had also found its way into her standards. She did not want just enough. She wanted assurance.

Work had to be meaningful. People had to be trustworthy. Churches had to be honest. Romance had to be sincere. None of that was wrong.

But the way she enforced it sometimes isolated her. She filtered so ruthlessly that only a few ever got close. She called it discernment.

Sometimes it was. Sometimes it was hunger in protective clothing. Protective against disappointment. Protective against repetition.

Protective against any scenario that smelled like the house she came from. If a situation remotely resembled old patterns, a leader who wanted her labour without fair pay, a friend who always needed but rarely gave,

a man who subtly tested how quickly she would adjust her life to his, she cut it off. Fast. Clean.

People said she was harsh. Naomi heard her own defence: I have already lived the consequences of ignoring early warning signs. Still, in quiet moments,

she admitted: Not every exit is wisdom. Some exits are just hunger running ahead to avoid ever feeling helpless again. She saw it most clearly

when she was alone. On nights when the numbers were fine, the city calm, her body rested, and yet

her mind still scanned for threats. An unanswered email. A tone misread. A slight downturn in a chart. A headline about recession.

Each small thing registered with disproportionate weight. This is what happens when hunger does not heal, she realized. It recruits everything:

luxury, discipline, standards, intellect, and turns them into soldiers.

Hunger in designer clothes is still hunger. It just gets invited into better rooms. One afternoon, after signing another contract, Naomi walked past a storefront window.

Her reflection looked back. Tailored coat. Clean lines. Eyes alert. She saw, suddenly,

both versions at once. The girl counting coins. The woman counting risks. Different wardrobes. Same posture.

Back straight. Jaw set. Hands ready to catch whatever fell. She stopped, hand on glass,

and whispered,

“You survived.”

The reflection did not soften. It tilted its head, as if to ask: At what point do we stop living like survival is the only goal?

She had no answer yet. All she knew was that hunger had been a faithful protector and a merciless master. It had kept her alive.

It had kept her sharp. It had also kept her from fully tasting anything without checking its expiry date. The money had changed the landscape. But the voice inside still spoke

with the same urgency: Be ready to run.

Chapter 10

The Woman Who Wanted a Child From Her

The hardest requests are not always made by people trying to control you. Sometimes they are made by people who love you and still want a version of you that would cost too much to become. He was not a villain. Naomi had dated men who made control obvious. She knew how to leave them.

This one was different. He did not flinch at her ambition. He did not compete with her success. He did not make jokes about her standards. He met her where she was. Steady job. Quiet faith. A history that had hurt him without making him cruel. Friends who trusted him. A life already in motion before she arrived. Naomi liked that.

He had not been waiting for a woman to give his days meaning. He had meaning. Work. Community. A small rented house with plants that actually lived. Siblings. Nieces and nephews who climbed him like furniture. He had roots in places Naomi had never allowed herself to plant. They met through mutual friends, through one of those unintentional set-ups

where everyone says, It’s just dinner, but their faces betray hope. At first, she braced. Waiting for the moment he would reveal the familiar script. You’re too much. You work too hard. Let me lead. Let me be a man. He never said it.

Instead, he asked questions. Not the intrusive kind. The curious kind. How did you get from there to here? What did it cost? What do you actually enjoy when you’re not working? What scares you that people wouldn’t expect? Naomi, who had grown used to being studied as an example, felt herself relax.

She told him stories she hadn’t told in years. About the house that counted everything. About her mother’s hands. About coins and vows and the first scholarship. He listened without turning them into a lesson. He did not say, See, God had a plan. He did not say, That’s why you should, He just said, That must have been heavy. No wonder you are how you are. She laughed.

How am I? Careful, he said. But not as careful as you think. They built something slow. Texts. Calls. Dinners where she left her phone face down. Walks where her shoulders dropped half an inch. He did not rush to move in. Did not rush to merge accounts. Did not treat her like a project.

Naomi noticed. Her friends did too. You seem different with him, they said. She shrugged. He feels less like a threat, she admitted.

That was new. Months passed. They met families. Survived holidays. Weathered small fights about nothing and one or two about something. He saw how she worked. The pace. The precision. The way she double-checked every exit. He did not shame her for it. Sometimes he even admired it.

You built all this with no safety net, he said once, looking around her apartment. That’s no small thing. She swallowed. There were moments she thought, Maybe this is the person I can build a shared life with without drowning.

For a while, they did. Shared weekends. Shared jokes. Shared prayers where he did not reduce God to a provider of husbands and she did not reduce God to a provider of exits. Then, one night, the conversation they had both quietly postponed arrived.

They were at his kitchen table. Dishes pushed aside. Tea cooling. A playlist humming low. He looked strangely nervous. Naomi felt it in her stomach. Here it comes.

He traced a finger along the rim of his mug. Naomi, can I ask you something without you turning it into a test? She tried to laugh. No promises. He smiled.

Fair. He took a breath. Have you thought about children? Ever? With anyone? With… me? The room went still. Not hostile. Not manipulative.

Just still. Naomi watched him. He was not trying to trap her. His eyes did not have that calculating gleam she had learned to spot. They had something else.

Hope. Fear. A softness she rarely saw in men talking about their future. She could have deflected. Maybe one day. Let’s see. Why do we have to decide now? But Naomi had promised herself truth, even when it cost her.

So she said it. I’ve thought about it. He waited. And? And I haven’t found a version where I want that life more than I want to belong to myself.

Silence. Not the angry kind. The heavy kind. He nodded slowly. I figured you’d say something like that, he admitted. But I needed to hear it.

Naomi’s throat felt tight. What about you? He did not hedge. I want kids. Plural.

Not because I think they’ll fix anything. Not because I want someone to look after me when I’m old. Not because I think you’re incomplete without them. He looked at her. Because I love the idea of raising people with you. The “with you” landed like a stone. Naomi swallowed.

He continued. You know my family. It’s messy. Loud. Exhausting. But I love being Uncle. I love school plays and silly drawings and tiny hands trusting mine. Maybe that’s selfish. Maybe it’s just my version of never again. I don’t want to repeat what I saw. I want to do it differently.

With you. His voice broke a little on the last word. Naomi looked away. She could picture it. Small shoes by the door. Laughter. Chaos. A life centralized around someone other than herself.

It did not repulse her. It just did not feel like hers. She thought of her mother. Of the women who stayed hungry. Of girls becoming mothers before they became people. Of the way the world used motherhood as proof. She thought of her own carefully built life, the rooms, the exits, the balance,

and felt something like vertigo. He reached for her hand. I need you to know, he said quietly, that this is not me trying to control you. She nodded. I know.

Some part of her wanted to say, That’s what makes this worse. Because control was easy to fight. Love was harder. No villain to point to. No obvious manipulation.

Just two people with deeply formed definitions of life, colliding. He squeezed her fingers. If you never want kids, he said slowly, I won’t lie and say I’ll be fine. I might resent it. I might resent you.

And that’s not fair to either of us. Naomi exhaled. She appreciated the honesty. She also felt a familiar blade. Love as equation.

Not because he wanted to buy her. Because he could not amputate his desire for fatherhood without amputating something essential in himself. He was not asking, Become what I want or you’re not a real woman. He was asking,

Is there a version of you that could share this with me? Naomi thought about all the versions other people had tried to demand. The good daughter. The good wife. The good mother. The woman who ate last. The woman who stayed. She had refused them to stay alive.

This version, though, came with kindness. He would likely be a gentle father. He would likely change nappies, wake up at night, show up. He would not leave her alone with the work. That made it harder to say no.

Because now she could not hide behind the excuse that men only wanted to trap her. This man wanted to build with her. Still, the cost felt the same.

Her body. Her time. Her career. Her sleep. Her nervous system. Her sense of being primarily her own. She closed her eyes. In another life, she whispered, maybe. He waited. In this one? he asked.

She opened her eyes. In this one, I can’t promise a child to prove love. It wouldn’t be a proof, he said softly. It would be a life. She nodded.

And I won’t bring a life into a story where I am already resentful at the idea of disappearing. That wouldn’t be fair to them. The honesty hung between them. He looked away first. So what do we do? he asked.

There was no neutral option. Stay, and one of them betrayed themselves. Leave, and both carried loss. Naomi had spent a lifetime choosing herself over scripts.

This time, choosing herself meant hurting someone who had never tried to hurt her. That felt different. Later, they would tell people some clean version. We wanted different things. We parted amicably. We’re still friends.

Some of it would be true. But in the moment, it was not clean. He cried. She did too. Not loudly. They were both too practiced at composure.

But tears came. For the life that could have been if either of them had been willing to bend more. For the version of Naomi who might have wanted this if she had not spent so many years raising herself from the wreckage. For the version of him who might have chosen love without children

if his own ache had not been shaped by absence. They were not enemies. They were witnesses to each other’s limits. Weeks later, her mother called.

She had heard there had been a breakup. Naomi, what happened? He was a good man. You won’t find many like him. Naomi said nothing. Her mother continued. One day you will understand when you’re a mother.

Naomi’s jaw tightened. No, she thought. I understood long ago what happens to women who say yes because they are afraid of regret.

Out loud, she only said, I’m not willing to have a child to keep a man I love. Her mother sighed. You always make everything so complicated. Naomi smiled sadly.

No. The world did. I’m just refusing to simplify my life by lying. Later that night, she lay on her couch, phone face down, lights dim,

and let herself feel the full weight of what she had chosen. Freedom and loss. Autonomy and emptiness. No baby cries. No tiny socks. No immediate family centered around her.

But also no resentment of a child who might one day sense that their existence had been a concession. The hardest requests in her life

had rarely come from the villains. They had come from people who genuinely loved her and still wanted a version of her that would have required the death

of the self she had spent years fighting to protect. Naomi did not hate them for it. She just refused to become

that version for anyone.

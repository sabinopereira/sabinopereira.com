Chapter 3

Good Women Stay Hungry

Good Women Stay Hungry

Naomi learned early that good women were often just hungry women praised for not complaining. The neighbourhood had a very specific idea of feminine virtue. A good woman did not ask for too much. A good woman knew how to stretch a meal,

a paycheck, a marriage, a smile. A good woman did not embarrass a man with her needs.

A good woman did not make her exhaustion other people’s problem. Good women stayed. That was the first rule. They stayed in kitchens

after everyone else had eaten. They stayed in marriages after tenderness had gone sour. They stayed in churches that called their suffering holiness.

They stayed in houses where the money was short and the gratitude was expected to be long. Naomi watched them closely. Aunties.

Mothers. Church women. Neighbours with tired eyes and disciplined voices. Women who knew how to serve tea while swallowing resentment.

Women who apologized before asking for help. Women who could carry three bags, two children, one silent disappointment, and a casserole

without ever calling themselves burdened. Everyone called them good. That was what Naomi noticed. Not just that they suffered. That they were praised for it.

People admired their endurance as if endurance were the highest form of womanhood. She is such a good wife. She never talks back. She holds that family together.

She does so much with so little. She is always there for everyone. She has such a servant’s heart. She is tired, but she never complains. She has been through so much,

but look how gracious she is. Grace, Naomi noticed, often meant silence with lipstick on. A servant’s heart, in those circles,

often meant a woman too trained to call neglect by its name. The good women ate last. Sometimes literally. At family gatherings, Naomi saw it happen again and again.

The men sat. The children reached. The guests were served. The plates moved. The noise rose.

And the women hovered. Refilling cups. Warming food. Checking who still needed what. Asking if everyone had enough

before asking themselves if they were hungry. By the time they sat down, the rice was colder. The meat was smaller. The room was already finished

with needing them for the visible parts. Naomi noticed how casually this was accepted. She also noticed how often these women laughed while disappearing. That was another feminine skill

the community admired: make vanishing look natural. A good woman forgave quickly too. Not because healing was easy. Because everyone expected speed.

He is trying. Marriage is hard. Men have pressure too. Do not let the enemy use your offence. Pray for him.

Cover him. Be wise. Be patient. Be a helpmeet, not a headache.

Naomi heard these phrases in living rooms, after church, through half-open doors, between women who should have known better

and women who had forgotten they deserved better. Forgiveness, in those spaces, was often required long before repair. A man could fail,

drift, shout, withdraw, betray, or simply become careless

in the slow, ordinary ways that ruin women quietly, and somehow the moral pressure still landed on the woman first. Do not harden your heart. Do not become bitter.

Do not punish him forever. Think of the children. Think of your vows. Think of your witness. Naomi listened

and thought something she did not yet have the language to say cleanly: Why is goodness always measured by how much of herself a woman can lose

without making it visible? The women around her turned lack into virtue. Not because lack was beautiful. Because naming it would have threatened the whole system. If there was no money, they called it a test.

If there was no rest, they called it purpose. If there was no help, they called it strength. If there was no tenderness, they called it maturity. If there was no room for their own becoming, they called it sacrifice.

Sacrifice was the word that made everything sound cleaner than it felt. Naomi heard it everywhere. A mother sacrifices.

A wife sacrifices. A woman of God sacrifices. A real woman knows how to pour out. A woman’s glory is in giving. A woman’s reward is in service.

A woman’s crown is in what she carries. No one mentioned what happened when she carried too much. No one asked what she had become beneath the applause.

No one seemed especially interested in whether these women were loved well. Only whether they were useful without complaint.

That distinction marked Naomi early. She saw women being congratulated for surviving conditions that would have been called failure if a man had to endure them for long.

A woman stretches. A man provides. A woman forgives. A man learns. A woman waits.

A man decides. A woman supports. A man leads. And if the whole arrangement left her smaller, thinner,

quieter, more tired, more prayerful, less visible, people called her virtuous.

Naomi did not hate these women. That is important. She pitied some. Admired some. Feared becoming almost all of them.

Because many of them were not foolish. They were intelligent. Funny. Observant. Capable.

Some had once been bright in ways the community no longer remembered to mention. A woman who used to sing before children made her speak mostly in schedules. A woman who had wanted to study

before bills and marriage turned her talent into something practical and then invisible. A woman who prayed with real power but spent most of her life

begging God to make disappointment feel holy. Naomi watched women become mothers before they ever became themselves. That stayed with her. It stayed in the way

young women were discussed. Not, What kind of life does she want? Not, What is she good at?

Not, Who is she when no one is asking her to serve? The real questions were different. Will she make a good wife? Is she humble?

Can she submit? Is she fertile? Is she nurturing? Can she build a home? Can she carry a man

without making him feel carried? Girlhood, in those spaces, often felt like rehearsal for disappearance. First daughter. Then helper.

Then wife. Then mother. Then everyone’s emergency contact. Then eventually, if she was lucky,

a woman whose children might thank her for the shape her vanishing took. Naomi saw this and felt two things at once. Grief. Rebellion.

Because there was love in these women. Real love. Meals cooked through fatigue. Hair braided with tenderness. Prayers whispered over sleeping children.

Bills stretched with impossible intelligence. Bodies kept moving on almost no rest. Faith held together with safety pins and sheer obedience. Naomi was not blind to that beauty.

But she was also not blind to the theft. How often the same world that praised these women also consumed them. How often good woman

meant woman whose labour felt natural because it was unpaid, unending, and dressed in Scripture. How often selfless

meant self-erased. How often strong meant unsupported. How often patient meant trapped.

How often humble meant underdeveloped. How often family woman meant a woman with no uninterrupted life of her own. The church added polish to the arrangement.

Church language was skilled at making female exhaustion sound sacred. Pour out. Die to self. Be available to God.

Men got calling. Women got burden. Men got room to fail. Women got reminders to be gracious. Men got sermons about leadership.

Women got conferences about how to keep their homes soft while carrying the emotional weight of everyone inside them. Naomi sat in pews and listened to women testify about staying.

Staying when they were lonely. Staying when money was tight. Staying when they were overlooked. Staying when their dreams had to be postponed for a decade

that became two. Staying when their bodies were tired and their names had become relational titles. Wife. Mother.

Sister. Helper. Mummy. Mama. Auntie.

She noticed how rarely the testimonies ended with becoming. They ended with enduring. And everyone applauded. Naomi would clap too.

She was still young. Still learning how communities teach desire what is respectable. But inside, something resisted. Not God.

Not love. Not family. The script. The one where a woman became holy by disappearing well.

The one where goodness meant accepting little and calling it enough. The one where service always moved downward from women, while praise moved upward

toward the institutions and men that benefited. The one where a woman’s appetite had to be starved before anyone trusted her character.

Naomi began to understand that hunger was not always about food. There was hunger for rest. For choice. For privacy.

For an unmonitored life. For money not tied to male moods. For sexuality not governed by shame. For holiness not measured by female disappearance. For selfhood before service.

The women around her carried all of that hunger under impeccable manners. And because they carried it without naming it loudly, they were called good.

Naomi learned from them. But not in the way the community intended. She did not learn how to disappear better. She learned what disappearing costs. She learned that starvation can become tradition

if enough people keep blessing it. She learned that some forms of femininity are elegant self-abandonment with a church outfit on. She learned that the world

does not always hate women by attacking them directly. Sometimes it loves them into smaller shapes. Sometimes it praises them

until they no longer know the difference between generosity and erasure. Sometimes it crowns them for surviving on crumbs and then calls their restraint

character. That was what Naomi rejected. Not just poverty. Not just instability. An entire definition of female goodness.

A goodness that required hunger. A goodness that distrusted appetite. A goodness that praised women for making themselves lighter, quieter,

cheaper, easier to carry for everyone except themselves. Naomi did not yet have the full language for this refusal. She only knew what she did not want.

She did not want to eat last forever. She did not want to forgive on command. She did not want to become one more woman whose exhaustion was mistaken for virtue. She did not want to be loved

only in proportion to how little she asked for. She did not want motherhood, if it ever came, to be the first time the world permitted her a shape.

She wanted to be a person before she became anyone’s offering. That desire would later be called pride. Selfishness.

Ambition. Coldness. Rebellion. A worldly spirit. A hard heart.

But at the beginning it was simply this: a girl watching good women go hungry, and deciding that whatever goodness was,

it should not require a woman to disappear to prove she had it.

Chapter 6

The Mother She Refused to Become

Naomi did not hate children. She hated the way motherhood was used as evidence in the trial of a woman’s goodness. People assumed it was the same thing. They saw a woman in her thirties with no stroller, no toys in her car, no small hand reaching for hers, and decided they knew her heart. Cold. Career-obsessed. Immature. Incomplete. Scared. Selfish.

They never saw the way she bent down to tie a niece’s shoes. The way she sat on church steps listening to a child explain a drawing. The way she carried a friend’s sleeping toddler out of a noisy room like something too precious to jostle. Children did not frighten her. What frightened her was the script that followed them. The script that said a woman’s life reached its true meaning only when she produced another one. It began quietly.

At first, the questions were soft. Do you like kids? Of course. You’re so good with them. The way they cling to you, you’ll make a wonderful mother. Naomi smiled. People meant it as a compliment. She knew that. But under the praise, she heard expectation.

The same way you can hear a train coming long before it reaches the station. Later, the questions lost their shyness. So, when are you having children? Time is passing. You know biology doesn’t wait. Your career won’t keep you warm at night. You don’t want to end up old and alone. Having it all is a myth. At some point, you have to choose. Choose what? Naomi did not say it out loud.

She knew the answer anyway. Choose between belonging to herself and belonging to a role. Her mother had a line she liked to use whenever Naomi pushed back. One day you’ll understand when you’re a mother.

Naomi heard it as both blessing and threat. She knew what her mother meant. Children rearrange you. Love changes you. Sacrifice feels different when it is your own child. Naomi believed that. She did not doubt the depth of maternal love.

She had seen it up close in casseroles, late nights, doctor visits, wrinkled hands counting out coins for school trips. Her refusal was not contempt for that love. It was clarity about the cost. I do not want to understand suffering by inheriting it, Naomi thought. She had already played mother once.

She did not need another chance. In her childhood house, she had been third parent without the title. Watching bills. Translating letters. Checking on her mother’s mood. Walking on emotional eggshells. Soothing younger cousins. Managing crises beyond her age. By the time she was a teenager, she had already done enough emotional labor to last a lifetime. She knew what it felt like to be responsible for everyone without anyone being fully responsible for her.

The world called that a strong daughter. Naomi called it training for a kind of motherhood she never wanted to repeat. She had seen women become mothers before they ever became themselves. Girls who skipped their own becoming to accommodate a man’s timeline, a church’s doctrine, a family’s expectations. Women who never had a season that was only theirs.

No era where their time belonged primarily to their own growth. No room to discover who they were when no one needed feeding, comfort, answers. They went from daughter to partner to mother without ever standing alone long enough to know what they actually wanted. The world called it a beautiful story. She called it a missing chapter.

Naomi refused to let her life skip itself. When friends announced pregnancies, she showed up. She bought gifts. She rubbed backs. She cooked. She sat in hospital rooms holding tiny hands, feeling something unmistakably tender move through her. Love did not bypass her. But during baby showers, between games and prayers and cake,

someone always turned to her. You’re next. You’ll see. It’ll change you. It’s different when it’s your own. Your heart will soften. You’ll understand what all this is for. She smiled politely. Inside, she tightened. Why is motherhood spoken about like a moral upgrade?

Why is a woman without children always spoken to like someone awaiting completion? No one said it plainly, but the message was there. A career is nice. A marriage is good. But motherhood, that is the real crown. The way they spoke, you would think a womb was a courtroom and a child was the verdict that declared a woman worthy.

Naomi’s problem was not babies. It was trials. She hated watching motherhood used as evidence. Evidence that a woman was not selfish. Evidence that she was capable of sacrifice. Evidence that she was healed. Evidence that she had matured. Evidence that she was what God always intended. What does that make women without children?

Naomi did not like the answers she heard in the pauses. Incomplete. Bitter. Unlucky. Too picky. Too traumatized. Unwomanly. Once, at a family gathering, an older aunt pulled her aside. Naomi, you can’t spend your whole life chasing money. Who will look after you when you’re old? Who will carry your name? You don’t want to end up one of those women with all the handbags and no one to love them. Naomi kept her face calm.

Inside, she wanted to ask, Who is looking after the women with children and no rest? Whose name do they carry when they no longer recognize themselves? Why is a woman with no children considered tragic, and a woman with no self considered holy? She said none of that. She had learned which truths were unwelcome at family tables. Instead, she set boundaries in quieter ways.

She did not date men who spoke about “giving her children” like a gift that would redeem her ambition. She did not sit through sermons that framed motherhood as the only evidence of femininity without mentally stepping back. She did not attend every event that assumed a woman without children had more time, more energy, more availability. She made decisions that people called selfish because they did not align with the narrative. Once, in a small group, someone said,

A woman without children always has a hardened heart somewhere. Naomi felt it like a slap. She went home, lay on her bed, and stared at the ceiling. Is my heart hard? She tested it.

Thought of the children she loved. The ones she checked on. The ones she protected in subtle ways. The ones whose names she brought to God without their parents ever knowing. Thought of the women she had helped, quietly, with rent, with groceries, with babysitting so they could rest for one evening. Thought of her own mother, whose life had been burned at both ends by duty, and how much she had tried to honor her without repeating her.

If this is a hard heart, Naomi thought, then perhaps what the world calls soft is just a heart that has never learned to say no. She did not want to become a mother because someone else needed her to complete their idea of a good woman. She did not want to bring a child into a life she was still wrestling into shape.

She did not want to repeat cycles of scarcity, resentment, silent sacrifice. She did not want to stand over a stove after a ten-hour day, feeding a family and being told she was “so strong” while nobody asked if she wanted another script. Naomi wanted to belong to herself before she belonged to anyone else. Fully. Without apology. To know what a day felt like when her body, time, money, and attention were not owed.

To see who she became when no one needed her as a default. To find out if she even liked the version of herself the world kept insisting on turning her into. This did not mean she closed the door forever. She left it ajar in one place only, not for expectation, but for choice.

If she ever became a mother, it would be because she chose it. Not because she surrendered to pressure. Not because her biological clock was shamed into alarms. Not because someone’s prophecy backed her into a corner. Because she had looked at herself, looked at her life, looked at the world, and said, Yes, I am willing to stretch this love in that direction. Until then, she refused to perform longing for something she was not sure she wanted.

People called that unnatural. They said a woman who did not ache for babies had something wrong with her. Naomi found it stranger that so many women ached for themselves and were told to ignore it. If there was anything unnatural to her,

it was how easily the world convinced women that their bodies were owed. Owed to men. Owed to tradition. Owed to lineage. Owed to nations that wanted higher birth rates. Owed to churches that needed more Sunday school stories about Hannahs and Sarahs and miraculous wombs. Naomi did not want her body to be evidence. She wanted it to be home. A place where she could live without trial.

So when people said, You’ll regret this, one day you’ll be lonely, you’ll die with no one at your bedside, she thought, Better lonely by consequence of my choices than resentful because I obeyed everyone else’s.

Better to grieve what I chose not to have than to resent the lives I brought into a world just to prove I was capable of love. Naomi did not hate children. She hated that motherhood had been turned into a weapon used primarily against women who dared to imagine a life

where their hearts belonged to God, to work, to art, to friends, to themselves, without needing a child to make that devotion count. If that hatred made her unnatural, she decided she could live with the verdict. She would rather be called unnatural

than become another mother who disappeared so completely that her children grew up learning that goodness for women meant never quite belonging to themselves at all.

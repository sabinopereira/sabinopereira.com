Chapter 7

Expensive Rooms

Expensive rooms were quiet, but Naomi learned that quiet was not always the same as peace. By the time she realized this, she had already climbed into a life that looked, from the outside, like everything she once needed. Soft hotel carpets. Keycards that opened onto views instead of threats. Airport lounges with filtered air and filtered people. Conference stages where her name appeared on screens. Apartments with doors that closed firmly and locks only she could open. Naomi walked through these spaces with the posture of someone who remembered what it cost to arrive.

She had receipts. Not just financial ones. Years of late nights. Risk. Humiliation swallowed in boardrooms. Skills sharpened under pressure. Lines drawn with trembling hands that eventually stopped shaking. Everywhere she went, there was evidence. Emails. Contracts. Invitations. Flights booked in her own name with her own card. She had done this.

One evening, standing in a hotel room high above a city that once felt too big for her, Naomi placed her palms on the cold glass and whispered, I built this. She said it softly. Not as a boast. As a fact she needed to hear. Because for years, the house of origin had made her feel like she owed her existence to other people’s choices.

Now, there was nobody else to credit. No landlord. No husband. No benefactor. Just a woman with spreadsheets, intuition, and an almost religious commitment to never being at hunger’s mercy again. She liked the way expensive rooms respected boundaries. Do not disturb signs meant something here. Staff knocked and waited. Noise lived mostly outside.

In these spaces, no one barged in to demand what else she could cut, stretch, sacrifice. Her childhood house had never known that kind of respect. Silence, back then, was full of held breath. Here, silence seemed to obey her. Yet, something felt off.

The first sign was how relief had replaced joy. Deals closed. Numbers hit. Milestones met. People clapped. Sent emails. Posted about her. Naomi smiled where required, stood for photos, accepted congratulations. But when doors closed behind her, what she felt was not celebration.

It was exhale. Not, I’m so happy. More, Thank God I didn’t fail. Thank God I can pay everyone. Thank God I can keep my distance. Thank God the numbers line up. Achievement had become another bill.

Her success wasn't just a dream fulfilled. It was a system she had to keep funded. She noticed it in airports. Other people saw airports as transitions. Naomi saw them as proof.

Proof that she could leave whenever she wanted. Proof that she was not trapped in any one place. Proof that she could outfly old ghosts. She loved the anonymity. Suitcase. Ticket. Passport. Name on a screen. No one there knew the girl counting coins. No one there knew the house that counted everything. No one there knew how many vows had been made over late bills. She belonged fully to herself in transit.

Still, sitting in lounges with free coffee, soft chairs, and endless outlets, Naomi sometimes caught herself staring at nothing. The same hunger that had once clawed at her over unpaid notices had changed shape. It now asked questions with different vocabulary.

If this is safety, why does it still feel so conditional? If this is freedom, why do you still fear losing it? If this is success, why does it only feel real when you’re working? Some expensive rooms felt unnervingly familiar. Not because they looked like the house she grew up in. Because they behaved like it. In one boardroom, glass walls, long table, cityscape outside,

she sat with people who controlled more money than her childhood neighborhood would see in a decade. They talked about risk, growth, strategy. Half the words were new. All the dynamics were old. Power sat on specific chairs. Voices carried more weight if they came with certain titles. Gratitude was expected from anyone allowed in.

Naomi spoke when it counted. Listened when it was wise. But somewhere between a slide about projections and a joke about “tough quarters,” she realized: this, too, is a house that counts everything. Not coins on a kitchen table. But numbers on screens. Hours. Outputs. Deliverables. Loyalty.

She knew the feeling. There were still good women here too. They wore heels instead of house slippers, blazers instead of aprons. They stayed late. Smiled. Softened their tone. Took on the invisible labor of making men look competent. They were praised for being team players. For going above and beyond.

Naomi watched and thought, Different currency. Same hunger. She had promised herself she would never be that woman again. Yet sometimes, she caught herself sliding toward it. Taking on one extra task. Soothing someone’s ego. Making herself a little smaller to keep peace in rooms that called it professionalism.

That was when she doubled down. Raised her rates. Clarified her terms. Said no to opportunities that came with too much access to her time, not enough respect for her limits. People called it boundaries. She called it self-rescue. Still, the higher she climbed,

the fewer people she trusted to stand beside her without agenda. Money had bought distance. It had not decided who could approach. So she curated. Invites. Dinners. Phone calls.

She answered less. Travelled more. Kept her circle tight. From the outside, it looked like discernment. From the inside, it sometimes felt like isolation with beautiful lighting. Her apartment testified to the tension. Everything was intentional.

Furniture chosen for comfort and longevity. Kitchen stocked with what she liked, not just what was on sale. Art on walls that did not belong to anyone else’s taste. Books stacked without apology. She loved this space. Loved unlocking a door and knowing every bill attached to it had her name. Loved the way the floor did not creak under other people’s footsteps. Loved the absence of shouted arguments through walls.

But there were nights when the quiet pressed in. When she realized she had gotten exactly what she had once prayed for, and still felt a familiar tightness. Not fear of eviction. Fear of collapse.

Not of finances. Of self. Because somewhere along the way, Naomi had tethered her sense of worth to her ability to maintain this life. If she lost it, who would she be? Not a hungry girl.

But not this woman either. The thought unnerved her. So she worked. Work was how she prayed for continued safety. Work was how she kept the ghosts at bay.

When people said, You deserve this, relax, enjoy, she tried. Spa weekends. Vacations. Dinners with no agenda. Yet even there, inside luxury,

she heard echoes. The way a waiter looked at her when she signed the bill without hesitation. The way hotel staff used ma’am with a respect that still felt like borrowed costume. The way men made comments about her lifestyle. Must be nice. Soft life. You’re living. She smiled. Inside, another tally.

Soft life did not feel soft. It felt like armor upgraded. More beautiful. More expensive. Still armor. There was a night, after a particularly large contract, where the contrast hit hardest.

She had closed a deal that would have made her younger self cry with relief. The numbers were public. The congratulations loud. She returned to yet another beautiful room, set her phone on the nightstand, and sat on the edge of the bed. The city was a line of lights. The room smelled faintly of some neutral hotel fragrance.

She should have been thrilled. Instead, tears came without warning. Not sobs. Just a quiet leak from somewhere she thought she had sealed. She pressed her fingers to her eyes, annoyed.

This is what you wanted, she told herself. This is what that girl in the counting house promised herself. Exits. Keys. Silence. You have them. Why does your chest still hurt?

The answer was not immediate. But as she sat there, breathing, she realized: money had solved certain problems so completely that she had begun to believe

it would eventually solve all of them. It had not. It had bought locks. Not rest. It had bought doors. Not belonging. It had bought exits. Not roots.

It had removed hunger from her table. It had not removed hunger from her soul. The emptiness was not about lack anymore. It was about distance. From people she no longer fully trusted. From communities that could not handle her complexity. From the girl she used to be and still carried like a ghost. She got up, walked to the window, looked down at the city.

Once, this view would have been enough to quiet everything. Now, she understood its limits. Expensive rooms could shelter her from old storms. They could not protect her from herself. She ran a hand along the glass.

The reflection looking back was not the hero of a scarcity story. It was a woman who had built a life so controlled that anything unexpected

felt like threat. Security had been her salvation. She was beginning to suspect it might also become her prison. The thought terrified her.

Not because she regretted what she had built. But because she realized that even here, in rooms she paid for, in lives she curated,

she was still answering to the same old vow: Never again. Never hungry. Never helpless. Never dependent. Never again.

It had carried her this far. It might not be able to carry her further without turning the fortress she built into a cage.

Standing there, Naomi understood that the next chapter of her life would not be about earning more or loving less. It would be about discovering

whether she could ever feel safe in a world where security was no longer her only definition of salvation.

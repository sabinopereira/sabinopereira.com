The Men Who Called It Love

The Men Who Called It Love

Naomi learned that some men did not offer love. They offered shelter with locks only they could open. By the time men started noticing her properly, Naomi had already learned two languages. Money.

Hunger. Love arrived speaking a third. At first, it sounded beautiful. You work so hard. Let me spoil you.

You shouldn’t have to worry about these things. With me, you can finally rest. You don’t have to be strong all the time. I’ve got you. Other women heard those words

and melted. Naomi heard them and reached for her calculator. Not the physical one. The one in her chest. The one that counted costs

no one else seemed willing to name. What do you want in return? What happens if I say no? Whose name will be on the lease? Who holds the exits?

Men admired her ambition before they resented it. They loved the story. A woman who came from little. A woman who worked hard.

A woman who knew the value of things. A woman who did not wait for someone to save her. It made them feel generous to praise it.

“You’re so driven.”

“I love that about you.”

“You’re not like other women,

always waiting for a man.”

“You have your own.”

“You’re a boss.”

They said this over dinners they insisted on paying, in backseats they called Uber Comfort, in apartments with views they hoped would impress her into softness. But admiration had a limit.

It usually arrived right where her independence refused to surrender. The first time it happened, she almost missed it. He was charming.

Stable. Kind in the way people are kind when they still believe you will eventually fold into their script. He liked her mind. Asked about her work.

Bragged about her to his friends.

“She’s serious.

She’s not playing. She’s on my level.” Naomi listened, curious. Where, exactly,

was his level? He had a good job. A plan. A flat he paid for. He knew the names of his bills.

He spoke the language of provider fluent and smooth.

“You don’t have to worry about anything with me,”

he said one night, menu in hand.

“Order whatever you want.

I’ve got it.” Naomi ordered what she wanted. Not the cheapest. Not the most expensive. Just what she liked.

He smiled, pleased. Later, over dessert she did not finish, he leaned in.

“You should move in.”

She raised a brow.

“That’s fast.”

He laughed, as if she were being cute.

“You’re always working.

You’re always stressed. Why pay rent when you could be with me? Save that money. Let me take care of you.” The words landed heavy.

Take care of you. In the house Naomi came from, care had always come with strings. In the church she grew up in, care had often been another word

for control people could feel good about. Her pulse picked up.

“What does take care of me mean to you?”

He blinked, surprised she needed clarification.

“It means you relax.

You don’t have to split everything. You don’t have to kill yourself working. You let me cover things. I’m a man. That’s what I’m here for.”

Naomi smiled. Small. Tight.

“And if we break up?”

He frowned.

“What?”

“If this doesn’t work.

If we fight. If you change your mind. If I do. If I leave. Or you ask me to.

What happens to the woman who moved into your life and stopped building her own exits?” He shifted in his seat.

“Is this a test?”

Everything is a test, Naomi thought. Out loud, she said,

“I just like to know the terms

of whatever contract I’m signing.” It was the wrong word for him. Contract. He recoiled.

“I’m not trying to control you.

I’m trying to love you.” There it was. The confusion Naomi had learned to detect in older men, preachers,

caring relatives. Control disguised as care. Ownership disguised as protection. Access disguised as affection. Love,

in many male mouths, sounded like a purchase order. Naomi shook her head.

“I’m not moving in with any man

whose kindness depends on me needing him more than I need my own salary.” He went quiet. Then came the names. Not immediately.

Not that night. At first, he tried to be patient.

“You’ve been through stuff.

I get it. I’ll prove I’m not like them. You’ll see.” But every time she insisted on paying her part,

every time she ordered her own ride, every time she refused to cancel a meeting for a midweek sleepover, he felt more insulted.

“You don’t know how to receive,” he said once,

when she slid her card onto the bill. Naomi smiled.

“I know exactly how to receive.

I just also know how to leave.” He stared.

“You act like I’m trying to trap you.”

She did not say what she was thinking. You might not be trying, but if I give up my exits, someone will eventually use them against me. The break-up was not dramatic.

It was tired.

“You’re cold.”

“You don’t trust anyone.”

“You make everything about control.”

“You’re impossible to love properly.”

“No man is going to stay

if you keep treating him like a threat.” Naomi nodded.

“Maybe.”

Better a man leave because I refused to be owned than stay on the condition that I shrink. He called her selfish. She filed it under names women receive

when their standards interfere with male convenience. Other men came with different packaging. Some were generous in ways that made sense even to her.

They paid without making it a performance. They offered help without demanding access. They suggested plans without acting entitled to her time. Not all of them were villains. Some were sincerely kind.

But Naomi had learned to hear locks in the softest sentences. Move in with me, so you can rest. Take time off. I’ll cover you.

You don’t need to work so hard. Let me carry it. For other women, this sounded like relief. For Naomi, it sounded like risk. Dependence felt like a slow leak.

One day, you realize you have built a life you cannot afford to leave. Naomi promised herself she would never wake up in that position.

So she paid her own share. Always. Even when she earned less. Even when he insisted. Even when people called it pride.

Sometimes she let a man pay, but not without clarifying the terms.

“This is a gift, yes?

Not a down payment.” They laughed, called her dramatic. But she watched their faces when she said it.

The ones who flinched, she noted. They were the ones who had grown up believing that money was a form of leadership. And leadership, in their minds,

came with obedience pre-installed. Naomi refused to live under that theology. She also refused certain addresses. There was one man, older,

established, sincere. He loved God. Paid his bills. Spoke of family with warmth.

Every woman in Naomi’s life approved. He offered what most people would call security.

“Come live here,” he said,

showing her a house with soft light and clean lines.

“You can focus on what you want.

You won’t have to worry about rent. We’ll build something together.” Naomi walked through the rooms. They were beautiful. Spacious.

Quiet. Well-kept. She imagined a version of herself living there. Cooking. Hosting.

Working from a desk overlooking a yard. Perhaps eventually rocking a child to sleep. The image was not ugly. But beneath it,

her old math ran its numbers. Whose name is on the deed? Whose money paid for this? Who has more to lose if I leave? What happens if I start to hate this life?

What happens if he changes? Her throat tightened. He noticed.

“What’s wrong?”

She shook her head.

“Nothing.

And everything.” His brow creased.

“Naomi,

you can’t live your whole life waiting for the worst.” She almost laughed. She was not waiting for the worst. She was remembering it.

She was remembering her mother standing in kitchens that did not belong to her. She was remembering women who whispered, We cannot leave. Where would we go?

She was remembering how quickly love turned into leverage when a woman could not afford her own exit.

“I can’t move into a house

I can’t easily move out of,” she said softly. He thought she was joking.

“You’re overthinking.”

“No,” Naomi said.

“I’m just refusing to underthink.”

It was not fair to him. He had not done anything wrong. But in her body, security felt too close to dependence. Safety felt too close to surrender.

He tried to understand. Tried to reassure. Tried not to take it personally. But at some point, exhaustion set in.

“You talk about freedom like it’s God,”

he said. Naomi looked at him. Freedom had certainly answered more prayers than some of the men she had dated. They grew apart.

People said she had trust issues. That she was traumatised. That she did not know how to receive love. That she would end up alone if she kept treating every man

like a potential captor. Maybe. But Naomi had seen too many good women confuse captivity with covenant. She refused to repeat it.

Slowly, a pattern formed. Men who admired her ambition as long as it remained charming, contained, and slightly below theirs.

Men who applauded her independence until it meant she could say no without financial fear. Men who found her standards inspiring until those standards meant

they did not automatically qualify. Men who called their controlling questions concern. Where are you? Who is there?

Why are you working so late? Why do you have to go on that trip? Why don’t you let me handle this? Men who framed boundaries as insults. You don’t trust me.

You’re too guarded. You make me feel like the enemy. You’re always ready to leave. You keep your own money like you’re planning for the end. Naomi did not deny it.

She was planning for the end. Not because she expected failure. Because she refused to be trapped if it came. In a world where men could walk out and rebuild without stigma,

women who prepared exits were called faithless. Naomi accepted the label. Better faithless to men than faithless to herself.

The names came, as they had to. Cold. Difficult. Too much.

Too calculating. Impossible to love. Impossible to lead. Masculine. Afraid of intimacy.

Married to her career. Heartless. What people rarely said out loud was the second half of their complaint. She is hard to control.

That was the real crime. Not that she wanted money. But that money had made her unreliable as a dependent. They could not threaten her with homelessness.

They could not shame her with who paid the bills. They could not buy submission with dinners and rent. So they called her names instead. Naomi heard them all.

Some nights, they landed. She wondered if she had gone too far. If her fear had turned into arrogance. If her standards were walls or wisdom.

But whenever she imagined doing it their way, she saw her mother at that table. She saw good women eating last. She saw girls becoming mothers before they ever became themselves.

She saw houses where a man’s income gave him the right to raise his voice. And she remembered: the first time money answered, she had heard control

and called it peace. The men who came offering love with locks, no matter how kindly, no matter how softly, were echoing that same sound.

Naomi decided she would rather be called cold than be warm in a cage. Let them say she was difficult. Let them complain that she paid her own half. That she kept her own account.

That she refused to move into houses she could not leave. That she asked, What happens if this ends? before saying yes.

Let them call it pride. Naomi knew better. She knew what it looked like when a woman had to stay because the exit was too expensive.

She knew what it felt like when love was debt with kisses. So when men called their control care, their conditions protection, their supervision concern,

their financial offers romance, Naomi smiled politely, held her own keys, and chose, again and again,

to love from a place where hunger could no longer be used against her.

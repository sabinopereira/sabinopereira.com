# Bad Girl Gospel: The Story of Naomi
## A Confession About Money, Hunger, and the Woman They Called Heartless

By Sabino Pereira

---

## Fiction Note

This is a fictional literary work.

Naomi is not written as a perfect victim or a complete villain. She is a character built from contradiction, hunger, shame, ambition, fear, control, tenderness, and the difficult work of becoming honest.

This book does not celebrate cruelty.  
It asks what happens when survival begins to look like greed, and when a woman who once had nothing decides she will never be powerless again.

Some wounds make people softer.  
Some wounds make people strategic.  
Some make them hard to love before they learn how to feel safe.

Naomi lives in that tension.

This is not a story about money alone.  
It is a story about what money comes to mean when you have known instability, humiliation, dependence, and the quiet violence of never having enough.

It is also a story about the names women are given when they stop confusing sacrifice with holiness.

---

## Author's Note

Bad Girl Gospel: The Story of Naomi began as a question:

What if heartless was not who she was, but the name the world gave to a woman who refused to stay hungry in order to be called good?

Naomi is a fictional character, but her contradictions are human. She wants security without becoming a machine. She wants power without becoming cruel. She wants love without becoming dependent. She wants God without pretending she does not care about money, control, or the fear of losing everything.

This book is not about excusing every wound.  
It is about telling the truth about what wounds can become.

Some women are called soft when they are starving.  
Some are called selfish when they finally eat.  
Some are called heartless when they stop offering their bodies, time, ambition, and future as proof of goodness.

Naomi stands in that accusation.

A baddie is what they see.  
A bad girl is what they judge.  
A free woman is what she is trying to become.

## Opening Manifesto
### A Confession About Money, Hunger, and the Woman They Called Heartless

My name is Naomi.

I am not your warning, your sermon illustration, or your lesson in misplaced priorities.

You called me materialistic because I learned the price of dependence early.  
You called me cold because I stopped confusing access with love.  
You called me heartless because I refused to keep starving just to make other people feel comfortable.

I know what hunger does.

I know what it does to a house.  
To a mother.  
To a girl listening through thin walls.  
To a body that learns too early that money is not just money.

Money is food.  
Money is rent.  
Money is silence.  
Money is a locked door.  
Money is the difference between leaving and staying because you have no choice.

So yes, I like money.

Not because I worship it.  
Because I remember what it was like to need it and not have it.  
Because I remember the humiliation of asking.  
Because I remember the way people speak to you when they know you cannot afford to say no.

I did not become ambitious by accident.  
I became ambitious because fear is expensive.  
Because instability leaves a taste in your mouth that never fully goes away.  
Because once you have seen what powerlessness does to a woman, you do not romanticize dependence again.

People like to tell my story in ugly, easy words.

Gold digger.  
User.  
Ice queen.  
Too much.  
Never satisfied.  
The kind of woman who would choose a promotion over a person.  
The kind of woman who would rather build an empire than a family.  
The kind of woman who does not know how to love unless love arrives itemized, insured, and impossible to weaponize.

Some of those accusations contain fragments of truth.  
That is what makes them dangerous.

I have used money as armor.  
I have used success as proof that no one can ever humiliate me again.  
I have measured people by whether they felt safe or expensive or useful.  
I have left before love could become leverage.  
I have stayed in rooms that made me feel powerful long after they stopped making me feel human.

That does not make me innocent.  
It makes me honest.

There were years when I trusted numbers more than people.  
Years when a full account felt holier than a full heart.  
Years when I would rather be admired than held, because admiration asks less of you than intimacy.

And yes, there were times when I looked at motherhood and felt not wonder, but terror.

Not because I hate children.  
Because I know what it means to build a life inside lack.  
Because I know what it costs a woman to keep giving when nobody asks what she has left.  
Because I have seen too many women disappear into sacrifice and get praised for the vanishing.

They call that holiness.

I do not.

They called her heartless because she stopped letting hunger make her holy.

That is the sentence they will not forgive in me.

Not that I wanted more.  
That I wanted more and refused to apologize for the wanting.  
Not that I feared poverty.  
That I feared it enough to build a life no one could easily control.  
Not that I questioned love.  
That I questioned any love that arrived asking me to be smaller, softer, poorer, or more grateful than free.

Do not worship me.  
Do not reduce me either.

I know ambition can turn a woman sharp.  
I know fear can dress itself up as standards.  
I know self-protection can become a religion if you kneel to it long enough.  
I know there are people I hurt while trying not to be hurt first.

I carry that too.

I still talk to God.  
In elevators.  
In airport bathrooms.  
In the dark after closing deals that should have felt better than they did.  
In expensive rooms where everything is quiet except my own mind.

I do not come to Him pretending I am simple.

I come as a woman who has made security into a sacrament.  
A woman who has turned spreadsheets into psalms.  
A woman who still does not know the full difference between wisdom and fear.  
A woman who wants to be safe so badly she sometimes forgets how to be soft.

If that makes you uncomfortable, take it to God.  
I already did.

I do not want to be the good woman in your story.  
I want to be the honest one in mine.

If I hurt you, I will not call it empowerment.  
If you tried to control me, I will not call it love.  
If money saved me, I will not lie and say it meant nothing.  
If money hardened me, I will not lie about that either.

I am not proud of every version of myself that survival created.  
But I will not insult her for learning how to eat.

A baddie is what you see.  
A bad girl is what you judge.  
A heartless woman is what you call me when my hunger stops serving you.

My name is Naomi.

And this is my gospel.

## Chapter 1
### The Rumor

No one really knew Naomi.

But everyone had a story about her.

She is cold.  
She only cares about money.  
She is ambitious in a masculine way.  
She uses men and calls it discernment.  
She would choose a contract over a child.  
She would choose security over softness every time.  
She is the kind of woman who makes love feel like an invoice.

They said it in office kitchens and private group chats, in bridal showers and conference corridors, in church foyers and restaurants with low lighting and expensive glassware.

Men talked about Naomi like a warning.  
Women talked about her like a flaw in the culture.  
Church people talked about her like a prayer request with good posture.

She is successful, but something is wrong with her.  
She is polished, but there is no warmth.  
She is beautiful, but difficult.  
She is smart, but too sharp.  
She has standards, but no softness.  
She has money, but no peace.

No one told the same story twice.

Details changed.  
Timelines shifted.  
Facts got dressed up and sent back out.  
But one thing remained the same:

the way they said her name  
like a correction,  
like a caution,  
like a woman who had once been tender and made the mistake of learning numbers.

By the time Naomi entered a room, the room had usually introduced her already.

If she arrived alone, they called it arrogance.  
If she arrived with a man, they calculated his watch before they looked at her face.  
If she paid the bill, they called it a performance.  
If he paid the bill, they called it strategy.  
If she spoke clearly, she was aggressive.  
If she stayed quiet, she was judging everyone.

The accusation never depended on what she did.

Only the wording changed.

At dinners, people watched Naomi the way some people watch markets, hoping for a collapse they can later call inevitable.

She wore silence well.  
That bothered people.

Her clothes were always precise.  
Not loud.  
Not apologetic either.

She looked like a woman who had made choices on purpose, and the world has never been fully comfortable with women who look deliberate.

Especially if they are not apologizing for what the deliberateness bought them.

The men noticed first.

They always did.

Not because they understood her, but because they assumed a woman with that face and that salary had to be one of two things:

a fantasy  
or a threat.

Sometimes both.

At bars, after conferences, over second drinks and fake honesty, men told stories about Naomi as if surviving her had made them wiser.

Bro, she is not built for normal love.  
She interviews you over dinner.  
She makes you feel like you are applying for funding.  
She asks questions like she is trying to buy the company.  
She does not want a partner. She wants leverage.

They laughed when they said it.

Men always laugh hardest when rejection has bruised their ego in a place they cannot explain.

They never mentioned how quickly they had admired her before they resented her.  
They never mentioned how much they liked her ambition when it was still flattering them.  
They never mentioned how often they mistook access for intimacy and generosity for ownership.

A reservation became a claim.  
A weekend became a forecast.  
A gift became a pressure point.

Then Naomi would say no, or not yet, or I will pay my half, or I am not moving into a house I cannot leave on my own salary, and suddenly the story changed.

She is damaged.  
She does not know how to receive.  
She wants a provider, but she does not know how to submit.  
She wants the benefits of a man without the responsibilities of a woman.

That last sentence traveled well.

It sounded good in male mouths.  
It sounded even better in religious ones.

The women were more careful with their cruelty.

They smiled first.

They complimented her coat, her shoes, her discipline, her work ethic, her ability to “carry herself.”

Then they began the real inventory.

Who was she dating?  
Was he rich?  
Was he older?  
Why had she not married yet?  
Why did she never seem impressed?  
Why did she always look like she could leave in the middle of the meal and not regret the bill, the man, or the conversation?

Women who had built their lives around endurance did not always know what to do with a woman who had built hers around escape.

Some envied her.  
Some distrusted her.  
Some studied her as if she were a moral failure in excellent tailoring.

She is what happens when women get too much freedom.  
She thinks money can protect her from loneliness.  
She acts like motherhood is beneath her.  
She is one of those women who wants everything and calls it healing.  
She has made her career into a religion.

Sometimes the women were wrong.

Sometimes they were close enough to the truth to leave fingerprints.

Because Naomi had, in fact, made work into a sanctuary on certain days.  
She had, in fact, trusted numbers more than people.  
She had, in fact, looked at marriage proposals hidden inside soft language and heard handcuffs instead of romance.

But none of them asked why.

That was the thing about rumor.  
It likes outcomes.  
It hates origins.

No one asked where her fear came from.  
No one asked what hunger had taught her.  
No one asked why a woman might hear “I’ll take care of you” and feel her whole nervous system reach for the nearest exit.

The church people had their own version.

They never said gold digger first.

They used words that had been washed and ironed for Sunday.

Stronghold.  
Control.  
Idolatry.  
Fear.  
Unhealed ambition.  
Difficulty receiving feminine grace.

They said Naomi had a beautiful mind, but.  
They said Naomi was clearly gifted, but.  
They said Naomi had leadership, but.  
There was always a but.

She needs to soften.  
She needs to trust God more than money.  
She needs to stop striving.  
She needs to stop trying to be the man in every room.  
She needs to heal whatever made her so afraid of motherhood.

That one was usually said more quietly.

Not because it was kinder.  
Because it landed harder.

The church women watched Naomi with the concentration of people trying to solve a theological problem in real time.

How does a woman love God and still refuse dependence?  
How does a woman pray and still negotiate contracts like war?  
How does a woman quote Scripture and still say she does not want children?  
How does a woman succeed without making sacrifice her whole personality?

To them, Naomi was not just a person.

She was a disruption.

A woman who did not fit cleanly into the old equations:

softness equals holiness,  
marriage equals maturity,  
motherhood equals fulfillment,  
money equals compromise,  
ambition equals danger unless a man is carrying it.

Naomi violated too many formulas at once.

So they named her.

Heartless.  
Worldly.  
Controlling.  
Unsubmissive.  
Brilliant but barren.  
A woman who had mistaken survival for wisdom and achievement for intimacy.

Online, the rumor got meaner.

People are always bolder when the person they are describing has become a silhouette.

Under interviews, photographs, event clips, and conference panels, strangers turned Naomi into a theory.

She looks exhausting.  
You can tell she would never love a man unless he came with assets.  
She is the type to freeze her eggs and call it empowerment.  
Probably too busy building her empire to build a family.  
Women like this always end up alone.  
Money talks until the house gets quiet.

The comments came from strangers, wives, bored men, fake prophets, disappointed romantics, women hiding resentment under concern, and men who feared any woman who asked where the money went before she asked where the relationship was going.

Naomi rarely answered.

Silence made people more inventive.

If she defended herself, she was pressed.  
If she ignored them, she was arrogant.  
If she smiled, she was smug.  
If she looked tired, they called it karma.

She had learned early that once people want a woman to represent a lesson, they stop needing facts.

All they need is a face.

Naomi had the right face for projection.

Controlled.  
Unreadable.  
Expensive enough to offend.  
Beautiful enough to invite theories.  
Self-contained enough to make people wonder what exactly she had survived to become this exact.

At one fundraiser, a woman in a silk dress and practiced kindness leaned toward another and whispered, not quietly enough,

That is Naomi.  
The one who chose money over everything.

The other woman asked, over what?

The first woman did not hesitate.

Over love.  
Over children.  
Over softness.  
Over the kind of life that makes a woman human.

Naomi heard it.

Of course she did.

People often mistake composure for deafness.

She turned slightly, accepted a glass of sparkling water from a server, and thanked him with the calm of someone who had no intention of collapsing for the comfort of witnesses.

But inside, something old moved.

Not shame.  
Not exactly.

Recognition.

Because the rumor was cruel.  
But it was not random.

It had been built from visible choices and invisible history.  
It had taken the sharp outline of her life and filled in the missing parts with contempt.

No one asked what little girls learn when the rent is late.  
No one asked what daughters notice when a mother has to beg.  
No one asked what happens to a child who grows up understanding that every soft thing in the house depends on whether someone else feels generous that month.

No one asked where the hunger came from.

That was the oldest insult of all.

People looked at Naomi’s appetite and called it greed.  
They looked at her caution and called it cruelty.  
They looked at her standards and called them punishment.  
They looked at her refusal and called it pride.

They never looked for the famine underneath.

By the end of the night, Naomi was no longer just a woman in a black dress with a measured voice and a hand steady enough to hold a glass without betraying irritation.

She had become content.  
A symbol.  
A warning for daughters.  
A complaint in a husband’s mouth.  
A case study in a pastor’s private theology.  
A villain in the romantic imagination of people who still believed a good woman should prove her heart by risking ruin.

No one really knew Naomi.

They only knew the version of her that made their own lives easier to defend.

If she was heartless, then they did not have to ask why women grow hard.  
If she was selfish, then they did not have to ask how much unpaid sacrifice they still expected from women.  
If she chose money over love, then they did not have to ask what kind of love had so often arrived carrying control in its pocket.  
If she did not want motherhood, then they did not have to ask what sort of world keeps asking women to birth hope into systems that have never kept them safe.

The rumor said Naomi loved money more than people.

The truth was simpler  
and uglier.

Naomi had learned too early that when hunger enters a house, love is often the first thing to change its tone.

And before anyone knew her story,  
they had already named her appetite.

## Chapter 2
### The House That Counted Everything

The House That Counted Everything

Before Naomi loved money,
she feared what happened
when there was none.

The house she grew up in
did not just count money.

It counted everything.

Bread.

Milk.

Light.

Bus fare.

Laundry.

Patience.

Phone calls.

Silence.

Even kindness
seemed to arrive
measured.

Nothing in that house
felt abundant.

Not food.

Not sleep.

Not tenderness.

Not certainty.

There was always
some version
of not enough.

Not enough money
for the month.

Not enough food
to stop everyone
from noticing
the portions.

Not enough peace
to keep voices level
once evening came.

Naomi learned numbers
before she learned safety.

She learned the shape
of coins
in a tired hand.

The sound
of cupboards
closing too hard.

The meaning
of envelopes
left unopened
on tables

as if paper itself
could bite.

Her mother
was always tired.

Not poetic tired.

Not the glamorous kind
people perform online.

Bone tired.

Rent tired.

Breadwinner tired.

Prayer tired.

The kind of tired
that lives
in the shoulders

and changes
the temperature
of a room.

Her mother moved
through the house

like someone
always calculating.

Can this stretch?

Can that wait?

Can we water
this down?

Can we make it
to Friday?

Sometimes she sat
at the kitchen table

with receipts,

a cracked mug
of tea,

and the face
of a woman
trying to solve

a life
with subtraction.

Naomi watched
from the doorway.

Her mother’s mouth
moving softly
over numbers.

Rent.

Light.

Food.

School.

Transport.

Church.

Debt.

Always debt.

Debt in drawers.

Debt in tone.

Debt in the air.

Debt made the house
feel smaller
than it was.

There was a man too,

but his presence
did not mean safety.

Sometimes he was there
physically

and absent
in every other way.

Sometimes he was gone
before sunrise

and late again
after dark,

bringing home fatigue,

excuses,

half-promises,

and sometimes money
that never seemed
to stay long.

Sometimes he worked.

Sometimes he drifted.

Sometimes he talked big
about what was coming.

Sometimes he disappeared
inside his own pride

when the bills
got louder
than him.

Naomi learned early
that a man
in the house

and provision
in the house

were not
the same thing.

That was one
of the first
disillusionments.

Love and safety
were not twins.

Presence and provision
were not twins.

A family
and a shelter

were not twins
either.

Through the walls,
she learned
even more.

Children hear everything.

Even when adults
whisper.

Even when doors
close.

Even when people think
they are protecting
innocence

by lowering
their voices.

Naomi lay in bed
and listened.

To the scrape
of a chair.

To her mother’s voice
turned thin
with restraint.

To a man’s reply
sharpened
by defensiveness.

To the pauses
that felt
more dangerous

than shouting.

You said this month
would be different.

I’m trying.

Trying is not
paying it.

Do you think
I don’t know that?

Keep your voice down.

Naomi is sleeping.

She was never
sleeping.

She was counting.

Counting seconds
between words.

Counting the rise
in her mother’s voice.

Counting the silence
after accusation.

Counting how long
it took

for money
to turn love

into something
colder.

That was the lesson
the walls taught her:

love changes tone
when money disappears.

Not always because
people stop loving.

Sometimes because
fear enters first

and rearranges
everybody’s language.

A tired mother
sounds harsher
when she is scared.

A fragile man
sounds crueler
when he is ashamed.

A child
learns to go quiet

when she senses
that one more need

could tip the room
into violence,

or tears,

or prayer.

Naomi became
that quiet child.

Not sweet quiet.

Strategic quiet.

She learned
which questions
cost too much.

Can I have seconds?

Not tonight.

Can we buy this?

Maybe next month.

Why is Mum crying?

She’s just tired.

Why is Dad angry?

He’s under pressure.

Pressure became
the family religion.

Everyone bowed
to it.

No one named it
fully.

The house also counted
favours.

This may have been
the cruelest currency
of all.

Nothing arrived
without memory.

If a relative helped,

the help came back later
as a story.

As leverage.

As humiliation
in nicer clothes.

Remember who paid
when you needed us.

Remember who bought
Naomi’s school things.

Remember who kept food
in this house.

Naomi heard her name
inside adult gratitude

and understood
what no child
should understand:

need can make you
feel expensive.

Need can make
your existence

feel like a debt
someone else is carrying

with visible resentment.

Her mother hated begging.

Naomi could tell.

Not because
she said the word
often.

Because of what happened
to her face

when she had to ask
for anything.

Money.

Time.

Transport.

Mercy.

There is a humiliation
children can smell

before they can
explain it.

Naomi smelled it
on borrowed cash,

on delayed rent,

on church kindness,

on family generosity
that was never
fully free.

She watched her mother
say thank you

with a straight back
and wounded eyes.

She watched her
receive help

the way some people
receive insult:

carefully,

politely,

already bleeding.

Something in Naomi
hardened there.

Not all at once.

Not enough
for anyone
to notice.

Just a seed.

I will never beg
like this.

She did not say it
aloud.

Children rarely announce
their deepest vows.

They make them
privately.

In bed.

In silence.

While staring
at ceilings

that know too much.

The house counted food too.

That part stayed
in the body.

The extra slice.

The smaller plate.

The watered soup.

There’s enough
if we share,

spoken with hope
and resentment

mixed so tightly
they became
one flavour.

Naomi learned to read
her mother’s serving spoon
like weather.

A generous scoop
meant maybe
wages came in.

A careful scoop
meant hold
your questions.

A cheerful tone
meant do not
ruin this.

Children in easier homes
remember songs,

birthdays,

cartoons.

Naomi remembered
portions.

How much butter
was left.

How many eggs
could still
be used.

Whether the cereal
was being stretched.

Whether the electricity
was low enough

to make people
move differently
through the rooms.

Even light
was counted.

Turn that off.

Don’t waste water.

Open the window.

Use less.

Need less.

Take less.

The house trained her
in reduction.

Reduce appetite.

Reduce noise.

Reduce requests.

Reduce the evidence
that you, too,

cost money.

What no one saw
was what this did
to her idea
of love.

Love,
in that house,

was real.

But it was tired.

It came carrying
calculators.

It came interrupted.

It came with sighs.

It came with conditions
nobody wanted to admit
were conditions.

Not because her mother
did not love her.

She did.

Not because the adults
were monsters.

Most were not.

But scarcity
changes the texture
of affection.

It makes tenderness
feel expensive.

It makes patience
feel luxurious.

It makes care
arrive late,

frayed,

already carrying guilt.

Naomi saw
what happened
to women

in that economy.

How they stretched.

How they waited.

How they swallowed
tone.

How they made miracles
out of leftovers

and still got blamed
for the emptiness.

She saw
what happened
to men too.

How some disappeared
into pride.

How some hid
inside anger.

How some called
absence
provision

because naming failure
would have crushed them.

But it was the women
she studied hardest.

Her mother
at the table.

Her mother
at the sink.

Her mother
at the door

speaking softly
to someone

she needed
something from.

That image
never left her.

A woman needing.

A woman asking.

A woman forced
to turn dignity

into diplomacy.

Naomi hated it
with a child’s
absolute hatred.

Not her mother.

The position.

The dependence.

The waiting.

The way the whole house
could be ruled

by money
that never fully
belonged to them.

That was when
money stopped being
just currency.

It became weather.

Permission.

Volume control.

Breathing room.

Power.

The first theology
Naomi learned

was not about grace.

It was this:

the person
who pays

sets the tone.

Years later,
people would say

she was obsessed
with money.

They would say
she made everything
transactional.

That she could not
relax.

That she asked
too many questions

about costs,

terms,

plans,

exits.

What they would not know
is that Naomi had lived

inside a house
where nobody asked
enough.

A house where love
was sincere

but never safe.

A house where dependence
sat at the table

like an extra relative.

A house where
one unpaid bill

could change
everyone’s voice.

That house
did not make her
heartless.

It made her
observant.

Then cautious.

Then determined.

Then dangerous
to anyone

who thought softness
should come
without security.

By the time
she was old enough

to understand
the word future,

Naomi had already attached
a vow to it.

Never this.

Never the table
where grown women
cry over arithmetic.

Never the borrowed mercy.

Never the tightened jaw
before asking for help.

Never the child
in the next room

pretending to sleep

while love
and lack

fight through
the walls.

Before Naomi
loved money,

she feared
what happened

when there was none.

That fear
did not leave her.

It grew up
with her.

It learned
how to dress.

How to study.

How to negotiate.

How to smile politely
and still read
a contract

like a survival manual.

But before it became
ambition,

before it became
standards,

before it became
the rumor

people would later call
cold,

it was just this:

a little girl

in a house
that counted everything,

learning that security
was never a feeling.

It was always
a number

someone else
could withhold.

## Chapter 3
### Good Women Stay Hungry

Good Women Stay Hungry

Naomi learned early
that good women

were often just hungry women
praised
for not complaining.

The neighbourhood
had a very specific idea
of feminine virtue.

A good woman
did not ask
for too much.

A good woman
knew how to stretch
a meal,

a paycheck,

a marriage,

a smile.

A good woman
did not embarrass
a man

with her needs.

A good woman
did not make
her exhaustion

other people’s
problem.

Good women stayed.

That was
the first rule.

They stayed
in kitchens

after everyone else
had eaten.

They stayed
in marriages

after tenderness
had gone sour.

They stayed
in churches

that called
their suffering
holiness.

They stayed
in houses

where the money
was short

and the gratitude
was expected
to be long.

Naomi watched them
closely.

Aunties.

Mothers.

Church women.

Neighbours
with tired eyes
and disciplined voices.

Women who knew
how to serve tea

while swallowing
resentment.

Women who apologized
before asking
for help.

Women who could carry
three bags,

two children,

one silent disappointment,

and a casserole

without ever calling
themselves burdened.

Everyone called them
good.

That was what
Naomi noticed.

Not just
that they suffered.

That they were praised
for it.

People admired
their endurance

as if endurance
were the highest form
of womanhood.

She is such
a good wife.

She never talks back.

She holds
that family together.

She does so much
with so little.

She is always there
for everyone.

She has such
a servant’s heart.

She is tired,
but she never
complains.

She has been through
so much,

but look
how gracious
she is.

Grace,
Naomi noticed,

often meant silence
with lipstick on.

A servant’s heart,

in those circles,

often meant
a woman too trained

to call neglect
by its name.

The good women
ate last.

Sometimes literally.

At family gatherings,
Naomi saw it happen
again and again.

The men sat.

The children reached.

The guests
were served.

The plates moved.

The noise rose.

And the women
hovered.

Refilling cups.

Warming food.

Checking who still
needed what.

Asking if everyone
had enough

before asking themselves
if they were hungry.

By the time
they sat down,

the rice
was colder.

The meat
was smaller.

The room
was already finished

with needing them
for the visible parts.

Naomi noticed
how casually
this was accepted.

She also noticed
how often
these women laughed

while disappearing.

That was another
feminine skill

the community admired:

make vanishing
look natural.

A good woman
forgave quickly too.

Not because healing
was easy.

Because everyone
expected speed.

He is trying.

Marriage is hard.

Men have pressure too.

Do not let the enemy
use your offence.

Pray for him.

Cover him.

Be wise.

Be patient.

Be a helpmeet,

not a headache.

Naomi heard
these phrases

in living rooms,

after church,

through half-open doors,

between women
who should have
known better

and women
who had forgotten

they deserved better.

Forgiveness,
in those spaces,

was often required
long before repair.

A man could fail,

drift,

shout,

withdraw,

betray,

or simply become
careless

in the slow, ordinary ways
that ruin women quietly,

and somehow
the moral pressure

still landed
on the woman first.

Do not harden
your heart.

Do not become
bitter.

Do not punish him
forever.

Think of the children.

Think of your vows.

Think of your witness.

Naomi listened

and thought something
she did not yet
have the language

to say cleanly:

Why is goodness
always measured

by how much
of herself

a woman can lose

without making it
visible?

The women around her
turned lack
into virtue.

Not because lack
was beautiful.

Because naming it
would have threatened
the whole system.

If there was no money,
they called it a test.

If there was no rest,
they called it purpose.

If there was no help,
they called it strength.

If there was no tenderness,
they called it maturity.

If there was no room
for their own becoming,

they called it sacrifice.

Sacrifice
was the word

that made everything
sound cleaner

than it felt.

Naomi heard it
everywhere.

A mother sacrifices.

A wife sacrifices.

A woman of God
sacrifices.

A real woman knows
how to pour out.

A woman’s glory
is in giving.

A woman’s reward
is in service.

A woman’s crown
is in what she carries.

No one mentioned
what happened

when she carried
too much.

No one asked
what she had become

beneath the applause.

No one seemed
especially interested

in whether
these women

were loved well.

Only whether
they were useful

without complaint.

That distinction
marked Naomi early.

She saw women
being congratulated

for surviving conditions

that would have been called
failure

if a man had to endure them
for long.

A woman stretches.

A man provides.

A woman forgives.

A man learns.

A woman waits.

A man decides.

A woman supports.

A man leads.

And if the whole arrangement
left her smaller,

thinner,

quieter,

more tired,

more prayerful,

less visible,

people called her
virtuous.

Naomi did not hate
these women.

That is important.

She pitied some.

Admired some.

Feared becoming
almost all of them.

Because many of them
were not foolish.

They were intelligent.

Funny.

Observant.

Capable.

Some had once
been bright
in ways

the community
no longer remembered
to mention.

A woman
who used to sing

before children
made her speak
mostly in schedules.

A woman
who had wanted
to study

before bills
and marriage

turned her talent
into something practical

and then invisible.

A woman
who prayed
with real power

but spent most
of her life

begging God
to make disappointment
feel holy.

Naomi watched women
become mothers

before they ever
became themselves.

That stayed
with her.

It stayed
in the way

young women
were discussed.

Not,

What kind of life
does she want?

Not,

What is she
good at?

Not,

Who is she
when no one
is asking her
to serve?

The real questions
were different.

Will she make
a good wife?

Is she humble?

Can she submit?

Is she fertile?

Is she nurturing?

Can she build
a home?

Can she carry
a man

without making him
feel carried?

Girlhood,
in those spaces,

often felt like rehearsal
for disappearance.

First daughter.

Then helper.

Then wife.

Then mother.

Then everyone’s
emergency contact.

Then eventually,

if she was lucky,

a woman
whose children
might thank her

for the shape
her vanishing took.

Naomi saw this
and felt two things
at once.

Grief.

Rebellion.

Because there was love
in these women.

Real love.

Meals cooked
through fatigue.

Hair braided
with tenderness.

Prayers whispered
over sleeping children.

Bills stretched
with impossible
intelligence.

Bodies kept moving
on almost no rest.

Faith held together
with safety pins

and sheer obedience.

Naomi was not blind
to that beauty.

But she was also
not blind
to the theft.

How often
the same world

that praised
these women

also consumed them.

How often
good woman

meant woman
whose labour
felt natural

because it was unpaid,

unending,

and dressed
in Scripture.

How often
selfless

meant self-erased.

How often
strong

meant unsupported.

How often
patient

meant trapped.

How often
humble

meant underdeveloped.

How often
family woman

meant a woman
with no uninterrupted life
of her own.

The church added polish
to the arrangement.

Church language
was skilled

at making female exhaustion
sound sacred.

Pour out.

Die to self.

Be available
to God.

Men got calling.

Women got burden.

Men got room
to fail.

Women got reminders
to be gracious.

Men got sermons
about leadership.

Women got conferences
about how to keep
their homes soft

while carrying
the emotional weight

of everyone inside them.

Naomi sat in pews
and listened

to women testify
about staying.

Staying
when they were lonely.

Staying
when money was tight.

Staying
when they were overlooked.

Staying
when their dreams

had to be postponed
for a decade

that became two.

Staying
when their bodies
were tired

and their names
had become
relational titles.

Wife.

Mother.

Sister.

Helper.

Mummy.

Mama.

Auntie.

She noticed
how rarely
the testimonies

ended with becoming.

They ended
with enduring.

And everyone
applauded.

Naomi would clap too.

She was still young.

Still learning
how communities teach desire

what is respectable.

But inside,
something resisted.

Not God.

Not love.

Not family.

The script.

The one where
a woman became holy

by disappearing well.

The one where
goodness meant
accepting little

and calling it enough.

The one where
service always moved
downward

from women,

while praise moved upward

toward the institutions
and men

that benefited.

The one where
a woman’s appetite

had to be starved

before anyone trusted
her character.

Naomi began
to understand

that hunger
was not always
about food.

There was hunger
for rest.

For choice.

For privacy.

For an unmonitored life.

For money
not tied
to male moods.

For sexuality
not governed
by shame.

For holiness
not measured
by female disappearance.

For selfhood
before service.

The women around her
carried all of that hunger

under impeccable manners.

And because
they carried it

without naming it loudly,

they were called good.

Naomi learned
from them.

But not in the way
the community intended.

She did not learn
how to disappear better.

She learned
what disappearing costs.

She learned
that starvation
can become tradition

if enough people
keep blessing it.

She learned
that some forms
of femininity

are elegant
self-abandonment

with a church outfit on.

She learned
that the world

does not always
hate women

by attacking them
directly.

Sometimes
it loves them

into smaller shapes.

Sometimes
it praises them

until they no longer
know the difference

between generosity
and erasure.

Sometimes
it crowns them

for surviving
on crumbs

and then calls
their restraint

character.

That was what
Naomi rejected.

Not just poverty.

Not just instability.

An entire definition
of female goodness.

A goodness
that required hunger.

A goodness
that distrusted appetite.

A goodness
that praised women

for making themselves
lighter,

quieter,

cheaper,

easier to carry

for everyone
except themselves.

Naomi did not yet
have the full language
for this refusal.

She only knew
what she did not want.

She did not want
to eat last forever.

She did not want
to forgive on command.

She did not want
to become one more woman

whose exhaustion
was mistaken
for virtue.

She did not want
to be loved

only in proportion
to how little
she asked for.

She did not want
motherhood,

if it ever came,

to be the first time
the world permitted her

a shape.

She wanted to be
a person

before she became
anyone’s offering.

That desire
would later be called

pride.

Selfishness.

Ambition.

Coldness.

Rebellion.

A worldly spirit.

A hard heart.

But at the beginning
it was simply this:

a girl

watching good women
go hungry,

and deciding

that whatever
goodness was,

it should not require
a woman

to disappear

to prove
she had it.

## Chapter 4
### The First Time Money Answered

The First Time Money Answered

The first time
money answered,

Naomi mistook
the sound
for peace.

It did not look
like much.

No lottery.

No miracle cheque.

No rich relative
arriving with apologies

and back pay
for childhood.

Just a notice
on a board
outside her school:

Scholarship opportunity.
Essay required.
Limited places.

She almost
walked past it.

By then,
Naomi had learned
to distrust anything

that sounded
like help.

Help had strings.

Help had stories.

Help had receipts.

But this
was different.

No one’s face
attached.

No relative
to owe.

No church
to impress.

No man
to thank.

Just a line
of text

and the quiet thought:

What if this
is a way
to move money

without moving
dignity?

The essay topic
was broad
and predictable.

Hope.

Future.

Community.

Responsibility.

Naomi rolled
her eyes.

Hope had never
paid a bill.

Still,
she took
the leaflet.

At home,
the house was
in its usual state

of controlled lack.

A bill
on the table.

Her mother’s shoulders
curved over
the sink.

Her father
half-present,

half-elsewhere,

a man
in conversation
with his own
disappointments.

Naomi went
to her room

and wrote.

Not about hope.

Not really.

She wrote
about houses

that counted
everything.

About women
who ate last.

About hunger
given new names

to make it
bearable.

About the quiet violence
of being raised

to be grateful
for not enough.

She translated it
into something acceptable

for a scholarship
committee.

But the edge
was there.

She handed it in
with no ceremony.

A teacher nodded.

“We’ll see.

There are many
applicants.

Don’t get
your hopes up.”

Naomi did not.

Her hope
was busy elsewhere

surviving.

Weeks passed.

The house
stayed small.

The bills
stayed loud.

Her mother
stayed tired.

Her father
stayed inconsistent.

Then one afternoon,

a name echoed
over a crackling speaker
in the school hallway.

Naomi.

Please come
to the office.

She went,

heart steady.

The principal smiled
the way adults smile

when they feel generous
by association.

“Congratulations, Naomi.

You’ve been awarded
the scholarship.”

She blinked.

He explained.

It covered
part of her fees.

Came with
a stipend.

Not huge.

But something.

Something
that would be paid

to her.

Not to her parents.

Not to a church.

Not to a landlord.

To her.

They handed her
a letter

and a form.

She signed
with a hand

that did not shake

until she walked
out of the office.

Outside,
the hallway buzzed.

Life carried on.

No choir sang.

Naomi stood there,
letter in hand,

and felt
the world tilt
by a fraction.

Not joy.

Not yet.

More like
a door

unlocking.

At home,
she did the math.

If she saved most,

she could help.

If she kept some,

she could breathe.

If she was careful,

she could do both.

The first payment
came in cash,

in a small,
unremarkable envelope.

The administrator
slid it across
the desk.

“This is yours, Naomi.

Use it wisely.”

Yours.

Not hers
as in the house.

Hers
as in the girl

who had spent
a childhood

watching other people
count.

She walked home
with the envelope
hidden inside
her bag.

Every step
felt like conspiracy.

At the corner shop,
she paused.

Inside:

shelves
she knew too well,

foods
she had measured
with her eyes
for years,

items
her mother walked past

with a sigh
disguised
as practicality.

Naomi went in.

She did not
fill a basket.

She was not
reckless yet.

She chose
with the precision

of someone
who understood

exactly how much
her money

could
and could not
do.

Bread.

Milk.

Eggs.

A small cut
of meat

that was not
the cheapest

but still
within reason.

And then,

almost as an act
of rebellion,

a packet
of biscuits

her mother
always said

were too expensive
for their kind
of month.

At the till,
she counted.

Twice.

Then placed
the money
on the counter.

The cashier
rang it up

without comment.

No lecture.

No sigh.

No reminder
of what else

could have been done
with the same amount.

The receipt
was short.

Paid in full.

Naomi folded it
with care.

At home,
her mother’s back
was turned
to the door.

Naomi set
the bags
on the counter.

Her mother turned,

ready to ask
where that came from,

ready to fight
the humiliation

of another debt.

Before she could speak,
Naomi held up
the receipt.

“I paid.”

Her mother stared.

“With what?”

Naomi passed her
the letter.

Scholarship.

Stipend.

Explanation.

Her mother read,

lips moving silently,

eyes widening,

brow furrowing
at the parts

that sounded
too good
to be true.

“You didn’t
have to,”

“I wanted to,”
Naomi said.

There was more
inside that sentence.

I wanted to know
what it feels like

to bring money in
without begging.

Her mother’s face
did something strange.

Pride.

Relief.

Fear.

Loss.

Pride,

because her daughter
had done something
remarkable.

Relief,

because for tonight,
at least,

there was enough.

Fear,

because she knew
what the world does

to women
who think
they can rely
on themselves.

Loss,

because this moment
confirmed

what she had
suspected:

Naomi would not
stay small.

They cooked.

The house smelled
different.

Not rich.

Not decadent.

Just less strained.

The portions
were a little larger.

The tension
a little softer.

The conversation
a little less sharp.

Her father
came home,

eyes cloudy
with the day’s failures.

He saw the food

and sat
a little straighter.

“Good day?”
he asked.

Naomi’s mother
answered for them.

“Naomi got
a scholarship.”

He looked
at his daughter

for a long moment.

His face
held too many things

for Naomi
to comfortably study.

Pride,

quickly dressed
as humour.

“So now
you’re the rich one?”

Naomi shrugged.

“Not rich.

Just paid.”

They laughed.

It bordered
on something else.

On relief.

On discomfort.

On the shift
of an old hierarchy.

Later that night,

after dishes,

small talk,

and the shallow peace
of a day
without new crises,

Naomi lay in bed
and replayed
the day.

The envelope.

The shop.

The look
on her mother’s face
when she said,

I paid.

The way the house
had exhaled

without anyone
having to raise
their voice

to make it happen.

She realized something,

and it unsettled her.

The house sounded different
when money came in

through her hands.

Not because
it was better money.

Because it carried
no humiliation.

No one had to rehearse
gratitude

for a specific person.

No one had to take
calls

they did not
want to take.

No one had
to listen
to lectures

about being wise
with what they
were given.

The only conversation
was logistics.

What shall we cook?

How shall we
stretch this?

Can we keep some
for tomorrow?

It was almost
peace.

And Naomi,

who had grown up
measuring the volume
of fear in a room

by how people talked
around bills,

heard the difference.

The first time
money answered,

she heard
a new sound.

Not love.

Not faith.

Not joy.

Control.

Dangerous,

quiet control.

Control
over what was bought.

Control
over when
it was bought.

Control
over how long
the calm
would last.

She liked it.

That scared her.

Not enough
to stop.

Just enough
to make
a decision.

If this small amount
could do this,

what could more do?

Not for outfits.

Not for attention.

Not for status.

For exits.

For options.

For silence
that did not depend

on male moods.

For nights
without whispered
arguments.

For days
when her mother

could sit
without a calculator

and feel
like the world

was not about
to collapse

because a letter
came in the post.

Soon after,
another opportunity
came.

Someone
in the neighbourhood

needed tutoring
for their daughter.

Maths.

English.

Nothing complicated.

They could not
pay much,

they said.

Naomi smiled.

Anything
is something.

She took it.

She sat
at kitchen tables

with younger girls

whose mothers hovered
in doorways,

relieved
to outsource

one small
responsibility.

She explained
fractions.

Grammar.

Exam techniques.

Quietly building
a new equation
for herself.

Time
plus competence

equals money.

Not charity.

Not favour.

Not owed.

Earned.

Each small payment
went into
an envelope

in her drawer.

She did not
flaunt it.

She did not
announce it
to the house

unless it became
food.

Sometimes it did.

Sometimes
it stayed hidden.

When bills came now,

Naomi felt
the old panic rise

and then watched
as a newer feeling

stepped forward.

What if
I can do something?

One evening,
another light notice
arrived.

Another threat
in red.

Her mother’s shoulders
began their familiar curve.

“Leave it,”
Naomi said.

“I’ll handle
this one.”

Her mother frowned.

“How?”

Naomi fetched
the envelope
from her drawer.

Coins.

Notes.

The quiet accumulation
of afternoons

spent teaching.

She poured it out
on the table.

For a moment,
everybody just looked.

This time,
Naomi insisted

on being the one
to walk
to the payment office.

Her mother tried
to argue.

“It’s not your job.”

Naomi shook
her head.

“It’s my house too.”

At the kiosk,
she placed
the money
on the counter.

The clerk processed it,

stamped a receipt,

and slid it back.

Paid.

Just a word.

Small print.

Routine
for them.

It felt
like revolution
for her.

Walking back,
Naomi noticed
how her body felt.

Lighter
in some places.

Heavier
in others.

Light,

because she had done
what had always felt
like magic

belonging only
to adults.

Heavy,

because she understood
the temptation ahead.

If money
could do this,

what else
could it do?

What else
could she avoid,

escape,

reject,

rewrite

if she had enough?

Inside,
her mother hugged her

longer
than usual.

“Thank you.”

Naomi stiffened
for a second.

She hated
that phrase

in certain tones.

But this time,
it did not feel
like debt.

It felt
like acknowledgement.

Still,

while her mother’s chin
rested on her shoulder,

Naomi made
a quiet correction
in her mind.

This is the last time
I will feel grateful
to money

for doing
the bare minimum.

From now on,

this will be standard.

From now on,

this will be baseline.

From now on,

I will not be surprised
at my own ability
to provide.

That was the birth
of her ambition.

Not a speech.

Not a manifesto.

Just a recalibration

of what was
acceptable.

Fear had taught her:

Never again.

Watching good women
stay hungry
had taught her:

Not me.

Now money
added its own lesson.

You are not
powerless.

Power did not yet mean
boards,

shares,

influence,

titles.

It meant:

paid.

It meant food
without begging.

Light
without pleading.

Exits
without permission.

The first time
money answered,

Naomi mistook
the sound
for peace.

It was not peace.

It was leverage.

But in a world
where she had known

only pleading,

scarcity,

and the virtue
of feminine
disappearance,

even leverage
felt holy.

She did not know yet
that the same sound

would one day
drown out

her softer parts.

She only knew
what it felt like

to hear a bill
knock on the door

and realize,

for the first time,

that hunger
did not have to win

every argument.

## Chapter 5
### The Men Who Called It Love

The Men Who Called It Love

Naomi learned
that some men
did not offer love.

They offered shelter
with locks
only they could open.

By the time men
started noticing her
properly,

Naomi had already learned
two languages.

Money.

Hunger.

Love arrived
speaking a third.

At first,
it sounded beautiful.

You work so hard.

Let me spoil you.

You shouldn’t have
to worry
about these things.

With me,
you can finally rest.

You don’t have
to be strong
all the time.

I’ve got you.

Other women heard
those words

and melted.

Naomi heard them
and reached
for her calculator.

Not the physical one.

The one
in her chest.

The one that counted
costs

no one else
seemed willing
to name.

What do you want
in return?

What happens
if I say no?

Whose name
will be on the lease?

Who holds
the exits?

Men admired
her ambition

before they
resented it.

They loved
the story.

A woman
who came
from little.

A woman
who worked hard.

A woman
who knew
the value
of things.

A woman
who did not wait
for someone
to save her.

It made them
feel generous

to praise it.

“You’re so driven.”

“I love that
about you.”

“You’re not like
other women,

always waiting
for a man.”

“You have
your own.”

“You’re a boss.”

They said this
over dinners
they insisted
on paying,

in backseats
they called
Uber Comfort,

in apartments
with views

they hoped
would impress her
into softness.

But admiration
had a limit.

It usually arrived
right where
her independence

refused
to surrender.

The first time
it happened,

she almost
missed it.

He was charming.

Stable.

Kind
in the way
people are kind

when they still believe
you will eventually
fold into their script.

He liked
her mind.

Asked about
her work.

Bragged about her
to his friends.

“She’s serious.

She’s not playing.

She’s on my level.”

Naomi listened,

curious.

Where, exactly,

was his level?

He had
a good job.

A plan.

A flat
he paid for.

He knew
the names
of his bills.

He spoke
the language
of provider

fluent
and smooth.

“You don’t have
to worry
about anything
with me,”

he said one night,

menu in hand.

“Order whatever
you want.

I’ve got it.”

Naomi ordered
what she wanted.

Not the cheapest.

Not the most expensive.

Just what
she liked.

He smiled,

pleased.

Later,
over dessert
she did not finish,

he leaned in.

“You should
move in.”

She raised
a brow.

“That’s fast.”

He laughed,

as if she were
being cute.

“You’re always working.

You’re always stressed.

Why pay rent
when you could be
with me?

Save that money.

Let me take care
of you.”

The words
landed heavy.

Take care
of you.

In the house
Naomi came from,

care had always
come with strings.

In the church
she grew up in,

care had often
been another word

for control
people could feel
good about.

Her pulse
picked up.

“What does
take care of me
mean to you?”

He blinked,

surprised
she needed
clarification.

“It means
you relax.

You don’t have
to split everything.

You don’t have
to kill yourself
working.

You let me
cover things.

I’m a man.

That’s what
I’m here for.”

Naomi smiled.

Small.

Tight.

“And if we
break up?”

He frowned.

“What?”

“If this
doesn’t work.

If we fight.

If you change
your mind.

If I do.

If I leave.

Or you ask me to.

What happens
to the woman

who moved
into your life

and stopped
building
her own exits?”

He shifted
in his seat.

“Is this a test?”

Everything
is a test,

Naomi thought.

Out loud,
she said,

“I just like
to know
the terms

of whatever contract
I’m signing.”

It was
the wrong word
for him.

Contract.

He recoiled.

“I’m not trying
to control you.

I’m trying
to love you.”

There it was.

The confusion
Naomi had learned
to detect

in older men,

preachers,

caring relatives.

Control
disguised as care.

Ownership
disguised
as protection.

Access
disguised
as affection.

Love,

in many male mouths,

sounded like
a purchase order.

Naomi shook
her head.

“I’m not moving
in with any man

whose kindness
depends on me

needing him
more than I need
my own salary.”

He went quiet.

Then came
the names.

Not immediately.

Not that night.

At first,
he tried
to be patient.

“You’ve been
through stuff.

I get it.

I’ll prove
I’m not like them.

You’ll see.”

But every time
she insisted

on paying
her part,

every time
she ordered
her own ride,

every time
she refused
to cancel
a meeting

for a midweek
sleepover,

he felt
more insulted.

“You don’t know
how to receive,”
he said once,

when she slid
her card
onto the bill.

Naomi smiled.

“I know exactly
how to receive.

I just also know
how to leave.”

He stared.

“You act like
I’m trying
to trap you.”

She did not say
what she was thinking.

You might not be
trying,

but if I give up
my exits,

someone will
eventually use them
against me.

The break-up
was not dramatic.

It was tired.

“You’re cold.”

“You don’t trust
anyone.”

“You make everything
about control.”

“You’re impossible
to love properly.”

“No man
is going to stay

if you keep treating him
like a threat.”

Naomi nodded.

“Maybe.”

Better a man leave
because I refused
to be owned

than stay
on the condition

that I shrink.

He called her
selfish.

She filed it
under names
women receive

when their standards
interfere

with male convenience.

Other men came
with different packaging.

Some were generous
in ways

that made sense
even to her.

They paid
without making it
a performance.

They offered help
without demanding
access.

They suggested plans
without acting entitled
to her time.

Not all of them
were villains.

Some were sincerely
kind.

But Naomi had learned
to hear locks

in the softest
sentences.

Move in with me,
so you can rest.

Take time off.

I’ll cover you.

You don’t need
to work so hard.

Let me carry it.

For other women,
this sounded
like relief.

For Naomi,
it sounded
like risk.

Dependence
felt like
a slow leak.

One day,
you realize

you have built
a life

you cannot afford
to leave.

Naomi promised herself
she would never
wake up

in that position.

So she paid
her own share.

Always.

Even when
she earned less.

Even when
he insisted.

Even when people
called it pride.

Sometimes
she let a man pay,

but not without
clarifying the terms.

“This is a gift, yes?

Not a down payment.”

They laughed,

called her dramatic.

But she watched
their faces

when she said it.

The ones
who flinched,

she noted.

They were the ones
who had grown up
believing

that money
was a form
of leadership.

And leadership,
in their minds,

came with obedience
pre-installed.

Naomi refused
to live under
that theology.

She also refused
certain addresses.

There was one man,

older,

established,

sincere.

He loved God.

Paid his bills.

Spoke of family
with warmth.

Every woman
in Naomi’s life
approved.

He offered
what most people
would call

security.

“Come live here,”
he said,

showing her
a house

with soft light
and clean lines.

“You can focus
on what you want.

You won’t have
to worry
about rent.

We’ll build
something together.”

Naomi walked
through the rooms.

They were beautiful.

Spacious.

Quiet.

Well-kept.

She imagined
a version of herself
living there.

Cooking.

Hosting.

Working
from a desk
overlooking
a yard.

Perhaps eventually

rocking a child
to sleep.

The image
was not ugly.

But beneath it,

her old math
ran its numbers.

Whose name
is on the deed?

Whose money
paid for this?

Who has more
to lose
if I leave?

What happens
if I start
to hate this life?

What happens
if he changes?

Her throat
tightened.

He noticed.

“What’s wrong?”

She shook
her head.

“Nothing.

And everything.”

His brow
creased.

“Naomi,

you can’t live
your whole life

waiting for
the worst.”

She almost laughed.

She was not waiting
for the worst.

She was
remembering it.

She was remembering
her mother

standing in kitchens
that did not
belong to her.

She was remembering
women who whispered,

We cannot leave.

Where would
we go?

She was remembering
how quickly love

turned into leverage

when a woman
could not afford
her own exit.

“I can’t move
into a house

I can’t easily
move out of,”

she said softly.

He thought
she was joking.

“You’re overthinking.”

“No,”
Naomi said.

“I’m just refusing
to underthink.”

It was not fair
to him.

He had not
done anything wrong.

But in her body,

security felt
too close
to dependence.

Safety felt
too close
to surrender.

He tried
to understand.

Tried to reassure.

Tried not
to take it personally.

But at some point,

exhaustion
set in.

“You talk about freedom
like it’s God,”

he said.

Naomi looked
at him.

Freedom had certainly
answered more prayers

than some of the men
she had dated.

They grew apart.

People said
she had trust issues.

That she was
traumatised.

That she did not know
how to receive love.

That she would
end up alone

if she kept treating
every man

like a potential captor.

Maybe.

But Naomi
had seen too many

good women
confuse captivity
with covenant.

She refused
to repeat it.

Slowly,
a pattern formed.

Men who admired
her ambition

as long as it remained
charming,

contained,

and slightly
below theirs.

Men who applauded
her independence

until it meant
she could say no

without financial fear.

Men who found
her standards
inspiring

until those standards
meant

they did not
automatically qualify.

Men who called
their controlling questions

concern.

Where are you?

Who is there?

Why are you working
so late?

Why do you have
to go
on that trip?

Why don’t you
let me handle this?

Men who framed
boundaries
as insults.

You don’t trust me.

You’re too guarded.

You make me feel
like the enemy.

You’re always ready
to leave.

You keep your own money
like you’re planning
for the end.

Naomi did not
deny it.

She was planning
for the end.

Not because
she expected failure.

Because she refused
to be trapped
if it came.

In a world
where men
could walk out

and rebuild
without stigma,

women
who prepared exits

were called
faithless.

Naomi accepted
the label.

Better faithless
to men

than faithless
to herself.

The names came,

as they had to.

Cold.

Difficult.

Too much.

Too calculating.

Impossible
to love.

Impossible
to lead.

Masculine.

Afraid
of intimacy.

Married
to her career.

Heartless.

What people
rarely said
out loud

was the second half
of their complaint.

She is hard
to control.

That was
the real crime.

Not that she
wanted money.

But that money
had made her

unreliable
as a dependent.

They could not
threaten her
with homelessness.

They could not
shame her
with who paid
the bills.

They could not
buy submission

with dinners
and rent.

So they called her
names instead.

Naomi heard them all.

Some nights,
they landed.

She wondered
if she had gone
too far.

If her fear
had turned
into arrogance.

If her standards
were walls

or wisdom.

But whenever
she imagined
doing it
their way,

she saw her mother
at that table.

She saw good women
eating last.

She saw girls
becoming mothers

before they ever
became themselves.

She saw houses
where a man’s income

gave him the right
to raise his voice.

And she remembered:

the first time
money answered,

she had heard control

and called it peace.

The men who came
offering love
with locks,

no matter how kindly,

no matter how softly,

were echoing
that same sound.

Naomi decided
she would rather
be called cold

than be warm
in a cage.

Let them say
she was difficult.

Let them complain
that she paid
her own half.

That she kept
her own account.

That she refused
to move into houses

she could not leave.

That she asked,

What happens
if this ends?

before saying
yes.

Let them call it
pride.

Naomi knew better.

She knew
what it looked like

when a woman
had to stay

because the exit
was too expensive.

She knew
what it felt like

when love was debt
with kisses.

So when men
called their control
care,

their conditions
protection,

their supervision
concern,

their financial offers
romance,

Naomi smiled
politely,

held her own keys,

and chose,

again and again,

to love
from a place

where hunger
could no longer
be used

against her.

## Chapter 6
### The Mother She Refused to Become

Naomi did not hate children.  
She hated the way motherhood  
was used as evidence  
in the trial  
of a woman’s goodness.

People assumed  
it was the same thing.

They saw a woman  
in her thirties  
with no stroller,  
no toys in her car,  
no small hand  
reaching for hers,

and decided  
they knew her heart.

Cold.  
Career-obsessed.  
Immature.  
Incomplete.  
Scared.  
Selfish.

They never saw  
the way she bent down  
to tie a niece’s shoes.  
The way she sat  
on church steps  
listening to a child  
explain a drawing.  
The way she carried  
a friend’s sleeping toddler  
out of a noisy room  
like something  
too precious  
to jostle.

Children  
did not frighten her.

What frightened her  
was the script  
that followed them.

The script  
that said  
a woman’s life  
reached its true meaning  
only when she produced  
another one.

It began quietly.

At first,  
the questions  
were soft.

Do you like kids?  
Of course.  
You’re so good with them.  
The way they cling to you,  
you’ll make  
a wonderful mother.

Naomi smiled.

People meant it  
as a compliment.  
She knew that.

But under the praise,  
she heard expectation.

The same way  
you can hear  
a train coming  
long before  
it reaches the station.

Later,  
the questions  
lost their shyness.

So,  
when are you  
having children?  
Time is passing.  
You know biology  
doesn’t wait.  
Your career  
won’t keep you warm  
at night.  
You don’t want  
to end up  
old and alone.  
Having it all  
is a myth.  
At some point,  
you have to choose.

Choose what?

Naomi  
did not say it out loud.

She knew the answer anyway.

Choose  
between belonging  
to herself

and belonging  
to a role.

Her mother  
had a line  
she liked to use  
whenever Naomi  
pushed back.

One day  
you’ll understand  
when you’re a mother.

Naomi heard it  
as both blessing  
and threat.

She knew  
what her mother meant.

Children rearrange you.  
Love changes you.  
Sacrifice feels different  
when it is  
your own child.

Naomi believed that.

She did not doubt  
the depth  
of maternal love.

She had seen it  
up close  
in casseroles,  
late nights,  
doctor visits,  
wrinkled hands  
counting out coins  
for school trips.

Her refusal  
was not contempt  
for that love.

It was clarity  
about the cost.

I do not want  
to understand  
suffering  
by inheriting it,  
Naomi thought.

She had already  
played mother  
once.

She did not need  
another chance.

In her childhood house,  
she had been  
third parent  
without the title.

Watching bills.  
Translating letters.  
Checking on her mother’s mood.  
Walking on emotional eggshells.  
Soothing younger cousins.  
Managing crises  
beyond her age.

By the time  
she was a teenager,  
she had already  
done enough  
emotional labor  
to last a lifetime.

She knew  
what it felt like  
to be responsible  
for everyone  
without anyone  
being fully responsible  
for her.

The world called that  
a strong daughter.

Naomi  
called it  
training  
for a kind of motherhood  
she never wanted  
to repeat.

She had seen women  
become mothers  
before they ever  
became themselves.

Girls  
who skipped  
their own becoming  
to accommodate  
a man’s timeline,  
a church’s doctrine,  
a family’s expectations.

Women  
who never had  
a season  
that was only theirs.

No era  
where their time  
belonged primarily  
to their own growth.  
No room  
to discover  
who they were  
when no one  
needed feeding,  
comfort,  
answers.

They went  
from daughter  
to partner  
to mother

without ever  
standing alone  
long enough  
to know  
what they actually wanted.

The world  
called it  
a beautiful story.

She called it  
a missing chapter.

Naomi refused  
to let her life  
skip itself.

When friends  
announced pregnancies,  
she showed up.

She bought gifts.  
She rubbed backs.  
She cooked.  
She sat in hospital rooms  
holding tiny hands,  
feeling something  
unmistakably tender  
move through her.

Love  
did not bypass her.

But during baby showers,  
between games  
and prayers  
and cake,

someone always turned to her.

You’re next.  
You’ll see.  
It’ll change you.  
It’s different  
when it’s your own.  
Your heart  
will soften.  
You’ll understand  
what all this is for.

She smiled  
politely.

Inside,  
she tightened.

Why is motherhood  
spoken about  
like a moral upgrade?

Why is a woman  
without children  
always spoken to  
like someone  
awaiting completion?

No one said it plainly,  
but the message  
was there.

A career  
is nice.  
A marriage  
is good.  
But motherhood,  
that is the real crown.

The way they spoke,  
you would think  
a womb  
was a courtroom

and a child  
was the verdict  
that declared  
a woman  
worthy.

Naomi’s problem  
was not babies.

It was trials.

She hated  
watching motherhood  
used as evidence.

Evidence  
that a woman  
was not selfish.  
Evidence  
that she was capable  
of sacrifice.  
Evidence  
that she was healed.  
Evidence  
that she had matured.  
Evidence  
that she was  
what God  
always intended.

What does that make  
women without children?

Naomi  
did not like  
the answers she heard  
in the pauses.

Incomplete.  
Bitter.  
Unlucky.  
Too picky.  
Too traumatized.  
Unwomanly.

Once,  
at a family gathering,  
an older aunt  
pulled her aside.

Naomi,  
you can’t spend  
your whole life  
chasing money.  
Who will look after you  
when you’re old?  
Who will carry your name?  
You don’t want  
to end up  
one of those women  
with all the handbags  
and no one  
to love them.

Naomi  
kept her face calm.

Inside,  
she wanted to ask,

Who is looking after  
the women  
with children  
and no rest?  
Whose name  
do they carry  
when they no longer  
recognize themselves?  
Why is a woman  
with no children  
considered tragic,  
and a woman  
with no self  
considered holy?

She said none of that.

She had learned  
which truths  
were unwelcome  
at family tables.

Instead,  
she set boundaries  
in quieter ways.

She did not date men  
who spoke  
about “giving her children”  
like a gift  
that would redeem  
her ambition.

She did not sit  
through sermons  
that framed motherhood  
as the only evidence  
of femininity  
without mentally  
stepping back.

She did not attend  
every event  
that assumed  
a woman without children  
had more time,  
more energy,  
more availability.

She made decisions  
that people  
called selfish  
because they did not align  
with the narrative.

Once,  
in a small group,  
someone said,

A woman  
without children  
always has a hardened heart  
somewhere.

Naomi felt it  
like a slap.

She went home,  
lay on her bed,  
and stared  
at the ceiling.

Is my heart hard?

She tested it.

Thought of the children  
she loved.  
The ones  
she checked on.  
The ones  
she protected  
in subtle ways.  
The ones  
whose names  
she brought to God  
without their parents  
ever knowing.

Thought of the women  
she had helped,  
quietly,  
with rent,  
with groceries,  
with babysitting  
so they could rest  
for one evening.

Thought of her own mother,  
whose life  
had been burned  
at both ends  
by duty,

and how much  
she had tried  
to honor her

without repeating her.

If this is  
a hard heart,  
Naomi thought,

then perhaps  
what the world calls  
soft

is just a heart  
that has never learned  
to say no.

She did not want  
to become a mother  
because someone else  
needed her  
to complete their idea  
of a good woman.

She did not want  
to bring a child  
into a life  
she was still  
wrestling  
into shape.

She did not want  
to repeat cycles  
of scarcity,  
resentment,  
silent sacrifice.

She did not want  
to stand over a stove  
after a ten-hour day,  
feeding a family  
and being told  
she was “so strong”  
while nobody asked  
if she wanted  
another script.

Naomi  
wanted to belong  
to herself  
before she belonged  
to anyone else.

Fully.  
Without apology.

To know  
what a day felt like  
when her body,  
time,  
money,  
and attention  
were not owed.

To see  
who she became  
when no one  
needed her  
as a default.

To find out  
if she even liked  
the version of herself  
the world  
kept insisting  
on turning her into.

This did not mean  
she closed the door  
forever.

She left it  
ajar  
in one place only,

not for expectation,  
but for choice.

If she ever became  
a mother,  
it would be  
because she chose it.

Not because  
she surrendered  
to pressure.  
Not because  
her biological clock  
was shamed  
into alarms.  
Not because  
someone’s prophecy  
backed her  
into a corner.

Because she had  
looked at herself,  
looked at her life,  
looked at the world,  
and said,

Yes,  
I am willing  
to stretch this love  
in that direction.

Until then,  
she refused  
to perform longing  
for something  
she was not sure  
she wanted.

People called that  
unnatural.

They said  
a woman  
who did not ache  
for babies  
had something wrong  
with her.

Naomi  
found it stranger

that so many women  
ached for themselves  
and were told  
to ignore it.

If there was  
anything unnatural  
to her,

it was how easily  
the world  
convinced women  
that their bodies  
were owed.

Owed to men.  
Owed to tradition.  
Owed to lineage.  
Owed to nations  
that wanted  
higher birth rates.  
Owed to churches  
that needed  
more Sunday school  
stories  
about Hannahs  
and Sarahs  
and miraculous wombs.

Naomi  
did not want  
her body  
to be evidence.

She wanted it  
to be home.

A place  
where she could live  
without trial.

So when people said,

You’ll regret this,  
one day you’ll be lonely,  
you’ll die  
with no one  
at your bedside,

she thought,

Better lonely  
by consequence  
of my choices

than resentful  
because I obeyed  
everyone else’s.

Better to grieve  
what I chose  
not to have

than to resent  
the lives  
I brought into a world  
just to prove  
I was capable of love.

Naomi did not hate children.

She hated  
that motherhood  
had been turned  
into a weapon

used primarily  
against women  
who dared  
to imagine  
a life

where their hearts  
belonged to God,  
to work,  
to art,  
to friends,  
to themselves,

without needing  
a child  
to make that devotion  
count.

If that hatred  
made her unnatural,

she decided  
she could live  
with the verdict.

She would rather  
be called unnatural

than become

another mother  
who disappeared  
so completely

that her children  
grew up learning

that goodness  
for women

meant  
never quite  
belonging  
to themselves  
at all.

## Chapter 7
### Expensive Rooms

Expensive rooms  
were quiet,  
but Naomi learned  
that quiet  
was not always  
the same as peace.

By the time  
she realized this,

she had already climbed  
into a life  
that looked,  
from the outside,  
like everything  
she once needed.

Soft hotel carpets.  
Keycards that opened  
onto views  
instead of threats.  
Airport lounges  
with filtered air  
and filtered people.  
Conference stages  
where her name  
appeared on screens.  
Apartments  
with doors  
that closed firmly  
and locks  
only she could open.

Naomi walked  
through these spaces  
with the posture  
of someone  
who remembered  
what it cost  
to arrive.

She had receipts.

Not just financial ones.  
Years of late nights.  
Risk.  
Humiliation swallowed  
in boardrooms.  
Skills sharpened  
under pressure.  
Lines drawn  
with trembling hands  
that eventually stopped  
shaking.

Everywhere she went,  
there was evidence.

Emails.  
Contracts.  
Invitations.  
Flights booked  
in her own name  
with her own card.

She had done this.

One evening,  
standing in a hotel room  
high above a city  
that once felt  
too big for her,

Naomi placed her palms  
on the cold glass  
and whispered,

I built this.

She said it softly.  
Not as a boast.  
As a fact  
she needed  
to hear.

Because for years,  
the house of origin  
had made her feel  
like she owed  
her existence  
to other people’s choices.

Now,  
there was nobody else  
to credit.

No landlord.  
No husband.  
No benefactor.

Just a woman  
with spreadsheets,  
intuition,  
and an almost religious  
commitment  
to never being  
at hunger’s mercy again.

She liked  
the way expensive rooms  
respected boundaries.

Do not disturb signs  
meant something here.  
Staff knocked  
and waited.  
Noise lived mostly  
outside.

In these spaces,  
no one barged in  
to demand  
what else she could cut,  
stretch,  
sacrifice.

Her childhood house  
had never known  
that kind of respect.

Silence,  
back then,  
was full  
of held breath.

Here,  
silence  
seemed  
to obey her.

Yet,  
something felt off.

The first sign  
was how relief  
had replaced joy.

Deals closed.  
Numbers hit.  
Milestones met.

People clapped.  
Sent emails.  
Posted about her.

Naomi smiled  
where required,  
stood for photos,  
accepted congratulations.

But when doors closed  
behind her,  
what she felt  
was not celebration.

It was exhale.

Not,  
I’m so happy.

More,  
Thank God  
I didn’t fail.

Thank God  
I can pay everyone.  
Thank God  
I can keep my distance.  
Thank God  
the numbers line up.

Achievement  
had become  
another bill.

Her success  
wasn't just a dream  
fulfilled.

It was a system  
she had to keep  
funded.

She noticed it  
in airports.

Other people  
saw airports  
as transitions.

Naomi  
saw them  
as proof.

Proof  
that she could leave  
whenever she wanted.  
Proof  
that she was not trapped  
in any one place.  
Proof  
that she could outfly  
old ghosts.

She loved  
the anonymity.

Suitcase.  
Ticket.  
Passport.  
Name on a screen.

No one there  
knew the girl  
counting coins.  
No one there  
knew the house  
that counted everything.  
No one there  
knew how many vows  
had been made  
over late bills.

She belonged  
fully  
to herself  
in transit.

Still,  
sitting in lounges  
with free coffee,  
soft chairs,  
and endless outlets,

Naomi  
sometimes caught herself  
staring at nothing.

The same hunger  
that had once  
clawed at her  
over unpaid notices

had changed shape.

It now asked questions  
with different vocabulary.

If this is safety,  
why does it still feel  
so conditional?  
If this is freedom,  
why do you still fear  
losing it?  
If this is success,  
why does it only feel  
real  
when you’re working?

Some expensive rooms  
felt unnervingly familiar.

Not because  
they looked like  
the house she grew up in.

Because they behaved  
like it.

In one boardroom,  
glass walls,  
long table,  
cityscape outside,

she sat  
with people  
who controlled  
more money  
than her childhood neighborhood  
would see in a decade.

They talked  
about risk,  
growth,  
strategy.

Half the words  
were new.

All the dynamics  
were old.

Power sat  
on specific chairs.  
Voices carried  
more weight  
if they came  
with certain titles.  
Gratitude  
was expected  
from anyone  
allowed in.

Naomi spoke  
when it counted.  
Listened  
when it was wise.  
But somewhere  
between a slide  
about projections  
and a joke  
about “tough quarters,”

she realized:

this, too,  
is a house  
that counts everything.

Not coins  
on a kitchen table.

But numbers  
on screens.  
Hours.  
Outputs.  
Deliverables.  
Loyalty.

She knew  
the feeling.

There were still  
good women  
here too.

They wore heels  
instead of house slippers,  
blazers  
instead of aprons.

They stayed late.  
Smiled.  
Softened their tone.  
Took on  
the invisible labor  
of making men  
look competent.

They were praised  
for being team players.  
For going above  
and beyond.

Naomi watched  
and thought,

Different currency.  
Same hunger.

She had promised herself  
she would never be  
that woman again.

Yet sometimes,  
she caught herself  
sliding toward it.

Taking on  
one extra task.  
Soothing someone’s ego.  
Making herself  
a little smaller  
to keep peace  
in rooms  
that called it  
professionalism.

That was when  
she doubled down.

Raised her rates.  
Clarified her terms.  
Said no  
to opportunities  
that came with  
too much access  
to her time,  
not enough respect  
for her limits.

People called it  
boundaries.

She called it  
self-rescue.

Still,  
the higher  
she climbed,

the fewer people  
she trusted  
to stand beside her  
without agenda.

Money  
had bought distance.

It had not decided  
who could approach.

So she curated.

Invites.  
Dinners.  
Phone calls.

She answered less.  
Travelled more.  
Kept her circle  
tight.

From the outside,  
it looked like  
discernment.

From the inside,  
it sometimes felt  
like isolation  
with beautiful lighting.

Her apartment  
testified  
to the tension.

Everything  
was intentional.

Furniture chosen  
for comfort  
and longevity.  
Kitchen stocked  
with what she liked,  
not just what was on sale.  
Art on walls  
that did not belong  
to anyone else’s taste.  
Books stacked  
without apology.

She loved  
this space.

Loved  
unlocking a door  
and knowing  
every bill attached  
to it

Loved  
the way  
the floor  
did not creak  
under other people’s footsteps.  
Loved  
the absence

But there were nights  
when the quiet  
pressed in.

When she realized  
she had gotten  
exactly  
what she had once

and still felt

Not fear  
of eviction.

Fear  
of collapse.

Not of finances.  
Of self.

Because somewhere  
along the way,

Naomi had tethered  
her sense of worth  
to her ability  
to maintain  
this life.

If she lost it,  
who would she be?

Not a hungry girl.

But not  
this woman either.

The thought  
unnerved her.

So she worked.

Work  
was how she prayed  
for continued safety.

Work  
was how she kept  
the ghosts at bay.

When people said,

You deserve this,  
relax,  
enjoy,

she tried.

Spa weekends.  
Vacations.  
Dinners  
with no agenda.

Yet even there,  
inside luxury,

she heard echoes.

The way a waiter  
looked at her  
when she signed the bill  
without hesitation.  
The way hotel staff  
used ma’am  
with a respect  
that still felt  
like borrowed costume.  
The way men  
made comments  
about her lifestyle.

Must be nice.  
Soft life.  
You’re living.

She smiled.

Inside,  
another tally.

Soft life  
did not feel  
soft.

It felt  
like armor  
upgraded.

More beautiful.  
More expensive.  
Still armor.

There was a night,  
after a particularly  
large contract,

where the contrast  
hit hardest.

She had closed  
a deal  
that would have made  
her younger self  
cry with relief.

The numbers  
were public.  
The congratulations  
loud.

She returned  
to yet another  
beautiful room,

set her phone  
on the nightstand,  
and sat  
on the edge of the bed.

The city  
was a line  
of lights.  
The room  
smelled faintly  
of some neutral  
hotel fragrance.

She should have been  
thrilled.

Instead,  
tears came  
without warning.

Not sobs.

Just a quiet leak  
from somewhere  
she thought  
she had sealed.

She pressed  
her fingers  
to her eyes,  
annoyed.

This is what you wanted,  
she told herself.

This is what  
that girl  
in the counting house  
promised herself.

Exits.  
Keys.  
Silence.

You have them.

Why does your chest  
still hurt?

The answer  
was not immediate.

But as she sat there,  
breathing,

she realized:

money had solved  
certain problems  
so completely

that she had begun  
to believe

it would eventually  
solve all of them.

It had not.

It had bought locks.  
Not rest.

It had bought doors.  
Not belonging.

It had bought exits.  
Not roots.

It had removed hunger  
from her table.  
It had not removed  
hunger  
from her soul.

The emptiness  
was not about lack  
anymore.

It was about distance.

From people  
she no longer  
fully trusted.  
From communities  
that could not  
handle her complexity.  
From the girl  
she used to be  
and still carried  
like a ghost.

She got up,  
walked to the window,  
looked down  
at the city.

Once,  
this view  
would have been  
enough  
to quiet everything.

Now,  
she understood  
its limits.

Expensive rooms  
could shelter her  
from old storms.

They could not  
protect her  
from herself.

She ran a hand  
along the glass.

The reflection  
looking back

was not the hero  
of a scarcity story.

It was a woman  
who had built

a life  
so controlled

that anything  
unexpected

felt like threat.

Security  
had been her salvation.

She was beginning  
to suspect

it might also  
become her prison.

The thought  
terrified her.

Not because  
she regretted  
what she had built.

But because  
she realized

that even here,

in rooms  
she paid for,

in lives  
she curated,

she was still  
answering

to the same  
old vow:

Never again.

Never hungry.  
Never helpless.  
Never dependent.

Never again.

It had carried her  
this far.

It might not  
be able  
to carry her  
further

without turning

the fortress  
she built

into a cage.

Standing there,  
Naomi understood

that the next chapter  
of her life

would not be about  
earning more

or loving less.

It would be about  
discovering

whether she could  
ever feel safe

in a world

where security  
was no longer

her only  
definition  
of salvation.

## Chapter 8
### Hunger in Designer Clothes

Hunger in Designer Clothes

Hunger
did not leave Naomi
when the money came.

It learned
how to dress better.

On paper,
she had outrun
the life
that raised her.

Accounts
with padding.

Savings
that could hold
an emergency
without breaking.

A calendar
filled with work
she had chosen.

Rooms
she paid for
without bargaining.

From a distance,
it looked like
the opposite
of lack.

Up close,
the old hunger
was still there,

just better groomed.

It no longer stood
at cupboards
counting portions.

It stood
at checkout screens,

calculating risk.

Every purchase
was a negotiation.

Not just,

Can I afford this?

But,

How many exits
does this cost me?

How many months
of safety
am I handing over?

How quickly
can I rebuild

if everything
falls apart?

Naomi wore
beautiful things.

Not as costume.

As proof.

Shoes
that did not
blister her.

Coats
that held their shape
for years.

Bags
that did not peel.

Pieces
that would have once
belonged only

on the bodies
of women

she used to stare at
from buses.

She chose them
carefully.

Not for logos
alone.

For longevity.

For the way
they whispered

to the girl
inside her:

You are no longer
cheap

in anyone’s hands.

But even there,

in dressing rooms
with flattering mirrors,

hunger came along.

It stood beside her
as she turned
from side to side,

commenting.

If you lost all this,

what would matter more?

The item,

or the evidence
that you once
could buy it?

Are you buying clothing,

or reassurance?

She handed
the card over
anyway.

Sometimes
the reassurance
was worth
the price.

Other times,
she walked away.

Not from lack.

From discipline.

Her restraint
was not stinginess.

It was fear
in a blazer.

Because even
with full accounts,

Naomi’s mind
still ran
end-of-month
simulations.

What if contracts
stop?

What if a crisis
comes?

What if someone
sues?

What if
I get sick?

What if
I cannot outwork
this?

She had lived
too many years

in houses
where one event

wiped out
everything.

Her nervous system
did not forget
that math.

So she saved.

Sometimes
excessively.

Sometimes
to the point
where friends
teased her.

“Live a little,”
they said.

“You can relax now.

You’ve made it.”

Naomi smiled.

Relaxation
felt like a luxury

she had not yet
earned.

She did rest.

On paper.

Days blocked off.

Out-of-office
replies.

Trips booked
for no reason
other than joy.

But even there,

hunger sat
beside her.

Not the hunger
for food.

The hunger
for guarantee.

On a beach,
she checked emails
just in case.

In bed,
she ran through
budgets
before sleep.

On a Sunday,
she found herself
checking markets

instead of exhaling.

When she tried
to do nothing,

guilt arrived
quickly.

Rest
felt suspicious.

In her childhood,
rest had been associated
with danger.

Rest meant
you were not hustling.

Rest meant
someone might accuse you
of laziness.

Rest meant
you trusted

that tomorrow
would not betray you.

Naomi did not
fully trust
tomorrow.

Not yet.

So she filled
her days
with usefulness.

Even pleasure
had to justify
itself.

A massage
was not indulgence.

It was recovery.

A vacation
was not escape.

It was strategy:

prevent burnout,

return sharper.

Hunger
had learned
to speak corporate.

It wore
productivity apps.

Attended
goal-setting retreats.

Drafted visions.

Curated habits.

It did not scream
in cupboards
anymore.

It whispered
through spreadsheets.

You are still
one disaster

away from
the beginning.

Stay ready.

Stay sharp.

Stay ahead.

Love
did not escape
this scrutiny.

Naomi tried
to receive it.

She met people
who did not want
to own her.

Friends
who showed up
without asking

what she could offer.

Men
who did not flinch
at her independence.

Mentors
who wanted
her good

more than
her usefulness.

She appreciated them.

But even as
they drew close,

she listened
for the clause.

What will this cost
if I let it in?

What do they expect
if I fail?

Will they still
love me

if I lose
this lifestyle?

Will they resent me
if I stop
being impressive?

Hunger had taught her
that every gift
could turn
into leverage.

So she weighed people
like she weighed contracts.

Measured
their reactions.

Tested
their silences.

Watched how
they spoke
about others

behind their backs.

Only the rare ones
passed.

Even then,
full trust
was slow.

Because beneath
every interaction,

the old ledger
still hummed.

Who owes what?

Who has given
more?

Where is
the power?

Once,
sitting at
an expensive brunch

with a friend
from a similar background,

Naomi watched
a woman walk by
outside the window.

Stroller.

Plastic bags.

Child tugging
at her sleeve.

Face tired
in the way
Naomi recognized.

She stared
longer
than was polite.

Her friend noticed.

“You okay?”

Naomi swallowed.

“Yes.

No.

I don’t know.”

Because looking
at that woman,

she felt
three things
at once.

Compassion,

for the weight
she could see.

Fear,

that a few different choices
or circumstances

could have made
that her story.

And a defensive
superiority,

the sharp thought:

That will never
be me.

I paid too much
to avoid it.

She hated
that last part.

Hated how hunger
had made her protective

to the point
of arrogance.

As if avoiding
one form of pain

made her morally
above those
who could not.

She caught herself.

This is hunger,

she realized.

Hunger
for distinction.

Hunger
for proof

that she had truly
escaped.

Hunger
to believe

she would never
slip back.

It wore designer shoes.

Ordered oat lattes.

Spoke about boundaries.

But underneath,

it was the same hunger
that once made her

hide coins
in secret envelopes.

This new hunger
did not hoard food.

It hoarded status.

Numbers.

Invites.

Followers.

Respect.

Rooms.

She measured safety
not only

in bank balances,

but in how
irreplaceable
she felt.

If she became
forgettable,

would the world
punish her

by taking away
what she had built?

It was an absurd belief.

She knew that.

But trauma
rarely asks

for rational
permission.

At conferences,
she watched panels
of women

speak about resilience,

excellence,

leadership.

She listened.

Learned.

Nodded.

But sometimes,
during applause,

she wondered

how many of them
were also driven
secretly

by the same fear:

that if they stopped,

the ground
would open.

Hunger
had also found
its way
into her standards.

She did not want
just enough.

She wanted
assurance.

Work had
to be meaningful.

People had
to be trustworthy.

Churches had
to be honest.

Romance had
to be sincere.

None of that
was wrong.

But the way
she enforced it

sometimes isolated
her.

She filtered
so ruthlessly

that only a few
ever got close.

She called it
discernment.

Sometimes
it was.

Sometimes
it was hunger

in protective
clothing.

Protective
against disappointment.

Protective
against repetition.

Protective
against any scenario

that smelled
like the house
she came from.

If a situation
remotely resembled
old patterns,

a leader
who wanted her labour
without fair pay,

a friend
who always needed
but rarely gave,

a man
who subtly tested
how quickly

she would adjust
her life
to his,

she cut it off.

Fast.

Clean.

People said
she was harsh.

Naomi heard
her own defence:

I have already lived
the consequences

of ignoring
early warning signs.

Still,
in quiet moments,

she admitted:

Not every exit
is wisdom.

Some exits
are just hunger
running ahead

to avoid ever feeling
helpless again.

She saw it
most clearly

when she was alone.

On nights
when the numbers
were fine,

the city calm,

her body rested,

and yet

her mind still scanned
for threats.

An unanswered email.

A tone misread.

A slight downturn
in a chart.

A headline
about recession.

Each small thing
registered

with disproportionate
weight.

This is what happens
when hunger
does not heal,

she realized.

It recruits
everything:

luxury,

discipline,

standards,

intellect,

and turns them
into soldiers.

Hunger
in designer clothes

is still hunger.

It just gets invited
into better rooms.

One afternoon,
after signing
another contract,

Naomi walked past
a storefront window.

Her reflection
looked back.

Tailored coat.

Clean lines.

Eyes alert.

She saw,
suddenly,

both versions
at once.

The girl
counting coins.

The woman
counting risks.

Different wardrobes.

Same posture.

Back straight.

Jaw set.

Hands ready
to catch
whatever fell.

She stopped,

hand on glass,

and whispered,

“You survived.”

The reflection
did not soften.

It tilted its head,

as if to ask:

At what point
do we stop living

like survival
is the only goal?

She had no answer
yet.

All she knew
was that hunger
had been

a faithful protector

and a merciless master.

It had kept her alive.

It had kept her sharp.

It had also kept her
from fully tasting
anything

without checking
its expiry date.

The money
had changed
the landscape.

But the voice inside
still spoke

with the same urgency:

Be ready
to run.

## Chapter 9
### The People She Used

The People She Used

Naomi had been hurt
by hunger,

but hunger had also
taught her

how to hurt

without calling it
cruelty.

For a long time,
she did not see it
that way.

She saw herself
as cautious.

Selective.

Boundaried.

Wise.

Other people
saw scars.

It began
with small things.

A favour
taken for granted.

A call
ignored.

A promise
left vague enough

to wriggle
out of.

Naomi told herself
she was protecting
capacity.

The truth
was less flattering.

Sometimes,
she was using people.

Not for sport.

Not with malice.

Simply because
a part of her

had learned

that everything
and everyone

could be turned
into strategy.

Friends
became shields.

The first time
Naomi realized this

was not during
business.

It was during
a late-night call.

A friend,

one of the few
who had known her

before the expensive rooms,

called crying.

A breakup.

A disappointment.

A life detour
she had not chosen.

Naomi listened.

She gave advice.

Comforted.

Sent money
without being asked.

She was not
heartless.

But somewhere
during the conversation,

when the friend said,

“I don’t know
what I’d do
without you,”

Naomi felt
a small,

ugly relief.

Being needed
meant being central.

Being central
meant being safe.

If people needed her,

they were less likely
to leave.

Hunger had taught her

that usefulness
was a kind
of insurance.

So she leaned
into it.

She became
the friend
people called
in crisis.

Offering couches.

Solutions.

Contacts.

Tough love.

Money.

They called her solid.

Strong.

A rock.

Dependable.

She liked
the titles.

What she did not admit
was that sometimes

she held people close

only while
their need

matched
her capacity.

When they began
to ask for more,

for her vulnerability,

for her time
outside emergencies,

for her presence
when they were not
falling apart,

she grew distant.

She answered slower.

Changed topics.

Rescheduled

until the friendship
thinned.

Naomi told herself
it was boundaries.

Sometimes it was.

Sometimes
it was simply

an unwillingness
to offer tenderness

without crisis
as justification.

Gentleness
felt expensive.

She rationed it.

In her work,
Naomi eventually
hired help.

An assistant.

Bright.

Eager.

Loyal.

At first,
Naomi treated her
like an investment.

Taught her.

Paid fairly.

Encouraged boundaries.

Spoke proudly
about giving another woman

a chance
to avoid

the kind of scarcity
she had grown up in.

It sounded beautiful.

But when pressure came,

tight deadlines,

high-stakes projects,

travel stacked
on travel,

Naomi slipped.

She expected
availability.

Longer hours.

Emotional steadiness.

She framed it
as the industry.

Everyone does this.

This is how
we learn.

This is
the cost.

Her assistant
rarely complained.

She organised flights.

Smoothed schedules.

Shielded Naomi
from minor inconveniences.

Reminded her
to eat.

One night,

after a particularly
intense week,

Naomi found
a message.

It was long.

Careful.

Respectful.

It said,
in essence:

I am grateful
for this job.

I have learned
a lot.

But sometimes,
I feel like
a buffer

rather than
a person.

Sometimes,
your emergencies

become
my normal.

Sometimes,
I cover for you
in ways

that leave me
empty.

I admire
your boundaries.

I wish
you would allow me

the same.

Naomi’s chest
tightened.

Her first instinct
was defence.

Had she not
been generous?

Had she not
paid well?

Had she not
warned her
about the pace?

But beneath
the arguments,

a quieter truth
pushed forward.

She had used
this woman

as a shield.

Shield
from logistics.

Shield
from discomfort.

Shield
from the consequences

of saying yes
to too much.

Naomi replied.

She apologized.

Adjusted expectations.

Reassigned tasks.

Raised pay.

Encouraged time off.

It was sincere.

It was also late.

Something in the trust
had shifted.

They continued
working together.

Professionally,
they functioned.

Personally,
a distance remained.

Naomi filed
that away.

Hunger had not only
taught her

to work hard.

It had taught her
to normalise

other people’s sacrifice

if it protected
her own stability.

In love,
Naomi had her own ways
of using people.

Not for money.

Not for status.

For proof.

Proof
that she could
be desired.

Proof
that she could
leave.

Proof
that she had
changed.

She entered relationships
like someone

testing a bridge

they never intended
to fully trust.

When things were good,
she held back.

When things felt risky,
she left.

She avoided
the middle ground

where honest conversation

might have transformed
both of them.

Instead,
she watched.

Meticulously.

Measuring reactions.

Testing patience.

Pushing lightly
against boundaries

to see
what would crack
first.

Men thought
she was playing games.

In reality,
she was running
scenarios.

How does he react
to my success?

How does he handle
my no?

Does he try
to buy control?

Does he collapse
without input?

If they failed quickly,
she left quickly.

No explanation.

No ugly breakup scenes.

Just distance.

If they passed
for a while,

she waited.

As soon as
the relationship
required vulnerability

beyond her comfort,

she found reasons
to go.

Busyness.

Mismatch.

Different seasons.

Not aligned.

The phrases
were socially acceptable.

They were also
sometimes dishonest.

Because the real reason
was simpler.

She was not willing
to let anyone see

how much of her life

was still governed
by fear.

It felt safer
to let them think

she was cold

than to let them see

she was still
that girl

who could not trust
any love

that did not come
itemized.

In the process,
she hurt people.

Men who had shown up
patiently.

Men who had respected
her pace.

Men who had been
transparent

about their weaknesses

and still found themselves
cut off

because Naomi panicked

at the first sign
of dependency.

She did not call it
cruelty.

She called it
self-preservation.

But in the quiet,
she remembered faces.

A man who asked,

“Can you at least
tell me
what changed?”

And she could not
give him

an answer

that did not implicate
her own terror.

There were people
who had opened doors
for Naomi.

Older women.

Older men.

Leaders
who had seen
her potential

and decided
to risk their own
reputations

by attaching her name

to rooms
she had not yet
earned alone.

She respected them.

But at times,
she also treated them

more like opportunities

than relationships.

When their counsel
aligned with her plans,

she was grateful.

When their counsel
challenged her pace,

her methods,

her motives,

she disappeared.

Not outright.

Just slower replies.

Fewer updates.

Less eye contact.

More distance.

She would tell herself,

People have seasons.

Not every mentor
is for every stage.

True.

But sometimes,
the truth was this:

she did not want
to hear

that the hunger
which had made her
impressive

might now be
hurting her.

One mentor,

a woman who had survived
her own transformation
from scarcity,

once said to Naomi,

“You remind me
of myself

before I realized
I was burning people

to keep warm.”

Naomi laughed it off.

Later,
she replayed it.

Burning people
to keep warm.

She had never
lit anyone on fire.

She had never
intentionally destroyed
someone.

But warmth
was a powerful metaphor.

Comfort.

Safety.

Control.

How many times
had she maintained
those for herself

at the cost
of someone else’s
sense of being valued

for more
than their role?

She began
to notice patterns.

A friend
she only called
when overwhelmed.

A colleague
she vented to

but never
encouraged.

A sibling
she kept
at arm’s length,

offering money
instead of presence,

advice
instead of empathy,

solutions
instead of listening.

Naomi did not like
neediness.

It reminded her
too much
of her own past.

So she punished it.

Not with cruelty.

With distance.

Again,
she named it
boundary.

Again,
sometimes it was.

Again,
sometimes it was not.

At some point,
she could no longer
ignore the theme.

Whenever hunger
felt threatened,

she reached
for people.

Not to hold.

To use.

To block.

To shield.

To absorb impact.

To make her feel
safer,

more central,

less at risk.

And because
she prided herself
on being honest,

this realisation
bothered her deeply.

Had she become
what she once despised?

Someone who treated people
like currency?

Not fully.

Not always.

But enough
to matter.

The moment
that shook her most

came from someone
she did not expect.

A longstanding friend
finally pulled back.

Not with a fight.

With a decision.

“You have become
very good

at naming
your boundaries,”
she said.

Naomi nodded,
confused.

“That’s progress,
no?”

“It is,”
her friend replied.

“But sometimes
it feels like

you use boundaries
as walls,

not just doors.”

Naomi frowned.

Her friend continued.

“You say
you don’t owe anyone
access.

And you’re right.

You don’t.

But lately,
it feels like

you’ve also decided

you don’t owe anyone
tenderness.”

Naomi opened
her mouth
to argue.

Closed it.

Because somewhere
deep down,

the sentence landed.

Tenderness
had become,

in her economy,

a luxury reserved
for children,

clients in crisis,

and the rare few
who had known her

before money.

Everyone else
got efficiency.

Clear.

Polite.

Strategic.

It protected her.

It starved them.

She sat
with that.

Naomi had spent
so much of her life

refusing to be
the woman

who disappeared
into sacrifice,

that she had forgotten

there is more
than one way
to disappear.

You can disappear
by giving too much.

You can also disappear
by never giving enough

of the parts
that make you human.

Hunger
had narrowed her.

It had made her
so focused
on survival

that she sometimes
treated other people’s hearts

as collateral.

Not always.

Not beyond repair.

But enough
that she had to admit,

out loud,

if only to herself:

I have used people.

For cover.

For proof.

For comfort.

For distraction.

For narrative.

I have called it
wisdom.

Sometimes,
it was.

Sometimes,
it was just fear

wearing the language
of self-care.

Naming this
did not make Naomi

less justified
in her boundaries.

It made her
more responsible

for what she did
with them.

Hunger had hurt her.

Hunger had also
taught her

how to hurt
in ways

that sounded
reasonable.

Freedom built
on other people’s

unpaid emotional labour

was not as clean
as it looked.

The girl
from the counting house

had grown up.

The woman
in the expensive rooms

could no longer
avoid counting

the cost

other people
had paid

for her
never-again.

## Chapter 10
### The Woman Who Wanted a Child From Her

The hardest requests  
are not always made  
by people trying  
to control you.

Sometimes they are made  
by people who love you  
and still want a version of you  
that would cost too much  
to become.

He was not a villain.

Naomi had dated  
men who made control  
obvious.

She knew  
how to leave them.

This one  
was different.

He did not flinch  
at her ambition.  
He did not compete  
with her success.  
He did not make jokes  
about her standards.

He met her  
where she was.

Steady job.  
Quiet faith.  
A history  
that had hurt him  
without making him cruel.  
Friends who trusted him.  
A life  
already in motion  
before she arrived.

Naomi liked that.

He had not  
been waiting  
for a woman  
to give his days  
meaning.

He had meaning.

Work.  
Community.  
A small rented house  
with plants  
that actually lived.  
Siblings.  
Nieces and nephews  
who climbed him  
like furniture.

He had roots  
in places  
Naomi had never allowed  
herself  
to plant.

They met  
through mutual friends,  
through one of those  
unintentional set-ups

where everyone says,  
It’s just dinner,  
but their faces  
betray hope.

At first,  
she braced.

Waiting  
for the moment  
he would reveal  
the familiar script.

You’re too much.  
You work too hard.  
Let me lead.  
Let me be a man.

He never said it.

Instead,  
he asked questions.

Not the intrusive kind.  
The curious kind.

How did you get  
from there  
to here?  
What did it cost?  
What do you actually enjoy  
when you’re not working?  
What scares you  
that people wouldn’t expect?

Naomi,  
who had grown used  
to being studied  
as an example,

felt herself relax.

She told him stories  
she hadn’t told  
in years.  
About the house  
that counted everything.  
About her mother’s hands.  
About coins  
and vows  
and the first scholarship.

He listened  
without turning them  
into a lesson.

He did not say,  
See, God had a plan.  
He did not say,  
That’s why you should,

He just said,  
That must have been heavy.  
No wonder you are  
how you are.

She laughed.

How am I?

Careful,  
he said.  
But not as careful  
as you think.

They built something  
slow.

Texts.  
Calls.  
Dinners  
where she left  
her phone  
face down.  
Walks  
where her shoulders  
dropped  
half an inch.

He did not rush  
to move in.  
Did not rush  
to merge accounts.  
Did not treat her  
like a project.

Naomi noticed.

Her friends did too.

You seem  
different with him,  
they said.

She shrugged.

He feels  
less like a threat,  
she admitted.

That was new.

Months passed.

They met families.  
Survived holidays.  
Weathered small fights  
about nothing  
and one or two  
about something.

He saw  
how she worked.  
The pace.  
The precision.  
The way she double-checked  
every exit.

He did not shame her for it.  
Sometimes  
he even admired it.

You built all this  
with no safety net,  
he said once,  
looking around her apartment.  
That’s no small thing.

She swallowed.

There were moments  
she thought,

Maybe  
this is the person  
I can build  
a shared life with

without drowning.

For a while,  
they did.

Shared weekends.  
Shared jokes.  
Shared prayers  
where he did not  
reduce God  
to a provider of husbands  
and she did not  
reduce God  
to a provider of exits.

Then,  
one night,

the conversation  
they had both  
quietly postponed

arrived.

They were  
at his kitchen table.

Dishes pushed aside.  
Tea cooling.  
A playlist humming  
low.

He looked  
strangely nervous.

Naomi felt it  
in her stomach.

Here it comes.

He traced a finger  
along the rim of his mug.

Naomi,  
can I ask you something  
without you  
turning it into a test?

She tried to laugh.

No promises.

He smiled.

Fair.

He took a breath.

Have you thought about  
children?  
Ever?  
With anyone?  
With…  
me?

The room  
went still.

Not hostile.  
Not manipulative.

Just still.

Naomi watched him.

He was not  
trying to trap her.

His eyes  
did not have  
that calculating gleam  
she had learned  
to spot.

They had  
something else.

Hope.  
Fear.  
A softness  
she rarely saw  
in men  
talking about their future.

She could have deflected.

Maybe one day.  
Let’s see.  
Why do we have to decide now?

But Naomi  
had promised herself  
truth,

even when  
it cost her.

So she said it.

I’ve thought  
about it.

He waited.

And?

And I haven’t found  
a version  
where I want  
that life  
more  
than I want  
to belong  
to myself.

Silence.

Not the angry kind.

The heavy kind.

He nodded slowly.

I figured  
you’d say something  
like that,  
he admitted.  
But I needed  
to hear it.

Naomi’s throat  
felt tight.

What about you?

He did not hedge.

I want kids.

Plural.

Not because  
I think they’ll fix anything.  
Not because  
I want someone  
to look after me  
when I’m old.  
Not because  
I think you’re incomplete  
without them.

He looked at her.

Because  
I love the idea  
of raising  
people  
with you.

The “with you”  
landed  
like a stone.

Naomi swallowed.

He continued.

You know  
my family.  
It’s messy.  
Loud.  
Exhausting.

But I love  
being Uncle.  
I love  
school plays  
and silly drawings  
and tiny hands  
trusting mine.

Maybe  
that’s selfish.  
Maybe  
it’s just my version  
of never again.

I don’t want  
to repeat  
what I saw.  
I want  
to do it differently.

With you.

His voice broke  
a little  
on the last word.

Naomi looked away.

She could picture it.

Small shoes  
by the door.  
Laughter.  
Chaos.  
A life  
centralized  
around someone  
other than herself.

It did not repulse her.

It just  
did not feel  
like hers.

She thought  
of her mother.  
Of the women  
who stayed hungry.  
Of girls  
becoming mothers  
before they became people.  
Of the way  
the world  
used motherhood  
as proof.

She thought  
of her own  
carefully built life,

the rooms,  
the exits,  
the balance,

and felt something  
like vertigo.

He reached for her hand.

I need you  
to know,  
he said quietly,  
that this  
is not me  
trying to control you.

She nodded.

I know.

Some part of her  
wanted to say,

That’s what makes this worse.

Because control  
was easy to fight.

Love  
was harder.

No villain  
to point to.  
No obvious manipulation.

Just two people  
with deeply formed  
definitions of life,

colliding.

He squeezed her fingers.

If you never want kids,  
he said slowly,  
I won’t lie and say  
I’ll be fine.

I might resent it.  
I might resent you.

And that’s not fair  
to either of us.

Naomi exhaled.

She appreciated  
the honesty.

She also felt  
a familiar blade.

Love  
as equation.

Not because he  
wanted to buy her.

Because he  
could not amputate  
his desire  
for fatherhood

without amputating  
something essential  
in himself.

He was not asking,  
Become what I want  
or you’re not a real woman.

He was asking,

Is there a version  
of you

that could share this  
with me?

Naomi thought  
about all the versions  
other people  
had tried to demand.

The good daughter.  
The good wife.  
The good mother.  
The woman  
who ate last.  
The woman  
who stayed.

She had refused them  
to stay alive.

This version,  
though,

came with kindness.

He would likely  
be a gentle father.  
He would likely  
change nappies,  
wake up at night,  
show up.

He would not  
leave her alone  
with the work.

That made it  
harder to say no.

Because now  
she could not hide  
behind the excuse

that men  
only wanted  
to trap her.

This man  
wanted to build  
with her.

Still,

the cost  
felt the same.

Her body.  
Her time.  
Her career.  
Her sleep.  
Her nervous system.  
Her sense  
of being primarily  
her own.

She closed her eyes.

In another life,  
she whispered,  
maybe.

He waited.

In this one?  
he asked.

She opened her eyes.

In this one,  
I can’t promise  
a child  
to prove love.

It wouldn’t be  
a proof,  
he said softly.

It would be  
a life.

She nodded.

And I won’t bring  
a life  
into a story  
where I am  
already resentful  
at the idea  
of disappearing.

That  
wouldn’t be fair  
to them.

The honesty  
hung between them.

He looked away first.

So what do we do?  
he asked.

There was  
no neutral option.

Stay,  
and one of them  
betrayed themselves.

Leave,  
and both  
carried loss.

Naomi  
had spent a lifetime  
choosing herself

over scripts.

This time,  
choosing herself

meant hurting someone  
who had never  
tried to hurt her.

That  
felt different.

Later,  
they would tell people  
some clean version.

We wanted different things.  
We parted amicably.  
We’re still friends.

Some of it  
would be true.

But in the moment,

it was not clean.

He cried.  
She did too.

Not loudly.  
They were both  
too practiced  
at composure.

But tears came.

For the life  
that could have been  
if either of them  
had been willing  
to bend more.

For the version of Naomi  
who might have wanted  
this

if she had not  
spent so many years  
raising herself  
from the wreckage.

For the version of him  
who might have chosen  
love without children

if his own ache  
had not been shaped  
by absence.

They were not enemies.

They were witnesses

to each other’s limits.

Weeks later,  
her mother called.

She had heard  
there had been a breakup.

Naomi,  
what happened?  
He was a good man.  
You won’t find many  
like him.

Naomi said nothing.

Her mother continued.

One day  
you will understand  
when you’re a mother.

Naomi’s jaw tightened.

No,  
she thought.

I understood  
long ago

what happens  
to women

who say yes  
because they are afraid  
of regret.

Out loud,  
she only said,

I’m not willing  
to have a child  
to keep a man  
I love.

Her mother sighed.

You always  
make everything  
so complicated.

Naomi smiled sadly.

No.

The world did.

I’m just refusing  
to simplify my life  
by lying.

Later that night,  
she lay on her couch,

phone face down,  
lights dim,

and let herself  
feel

the full weight  
of what she had chosen.

Freedom  
and loss.

Autonomy  
and emptiness.

No baby cries.  
No tiny socks.  
No immediate family  
centered around her.

But also

no resentment  
of a child  
who might one day  
sense

that their existence

had been  
a concession.

The hardest requests  
in her life

had rarely come  
from the villains.

They had come  
from people  
who genuinely loved her

and still  
wanted a version of her

that would have required

the death

of the self

she had spent  
years fighting  
to protect.

Naomi did not  
hate them for it.

She just refused

to become  

that version

for anyone.

## Chapter 11
### When Security Became a Cage

Naomi had built a life  
with exits everywhere,  
then wondered  
why nothing  
felt like home.

After him,  
the freedom  
she chose

sat beside her  
like an empty chair.

Her days  
did not collapse.

She did not spiral.  
She did not run back  
begging for another chance.  
She did not suddenly  
wish for a child  
she had never wanted.

She kept moving.

Meetings.  
Flights.  
Emails.  
Contracts.  
Gym.  
Therapy  
on Thursdays.

The machinery  
of her life  
kept humming.

But something  
in the rhythm  
shifted.

Silence  
no longer sounded  
only like victory.

For years,  
silence had been  
the prize.

No shouting  
through walls.  
No late-night  
financial arguments.  
No guilt  
pressed into every meal.  
No man  
slamming doors  
to remind everyone  
who paid the rent.

Silence  
meant safety.

Now,  
there were evenings  
where the quiet  
felt heavy,

as if every cushion,  
every clean plate,  
every perfectly arranged shelf

was testifying

to how successfully  
she had arranged  
her life

to need  
no one.

She sat  
on her own couch,  
in her own apartment,  
surrounded by evidence  
of competence,

and realized

she could go days  
without anyone  
seeing her  
unguarded.

Not because  
no one loved her.

Because she had built  
every hallway  
with exits.

Friends  
knew the time  
to call her.  
Clients  
knew the version  
they were allowed.  
Family  
knew the topics  
that would end  
the conversation  
if they pushed.

Everyone  
had learned  
her boundaries.

Very few  
had seen her  
without them.

After the breakup,  
people asked.

How are you?  
Are you okay?  
Do you regret it?  
Do you miss him?

Naomi answered honestly.

I’m sad.  
I miss him.  
I don’t regret  
not having a child  
to keep him.

What she did not  
always add was,

I’m starting  
to wonder

what I have lost

by designing a life

where nothing  
and no one

can touch me  
too deeply.

At work,  
she still excelled.

Her calendar  
was efficient.  
Her projects  
structured.  
Her staff  
respected her.

She was generous  
with pay.  
Firm  
with expectations.  
Clear.

But occasionally,  
in meetings,  
she caught herself  
checking out.

Someone would be talking  
about growth,  
future,  
expansion,

and her mind  
would drift.

Is this living?  
she wondered.

Or am I just  
maintaining  
a fortress?

On one trip,  
she returned  
to a city  
that had once felt  
like arrival.

The same hotel.  
The same lobby.  
The same scent.

She checked in  
as always.

Credit card.  
ID.  
Polite smile.

The receptionist  
recognized her.

Welcome back, Ms. Naomi.  
It’s good  
to see you again.

She nodded,  
habitually pleased  
by the continuity.

In her room,  
she placed her suitcase  
in the usual corner,  
hung her clothes,  
set her toiletries  
in the same formation.

Ritual.

Predictable.

Safe.

That night,  
lying in a bed  
that had never known  
her tears  
before,

she stared  
at the ceiling

and acknowledged  
something  
she had been resisting.

This routine,  
this structure,  
this control

had once been  
the antidote  
to chaos.

Now,  
it had become  
a script

so rehearsed

that there was  
no room  
for anything  
unexpected.

She had avoided  
dependency.  
She had also avoided  
surrender.

Not just to men.  
To God.  
To friends.  
To any story  
that required her  
to stay

when her instinct  
was to run.

She thought  
of him.

Not as a mistake.  
As a mirror.

He had exposed  
a limit.

Not his.  
Hers.

The limit  
of her willingness  
to be changed  
by love.

She did not regret  
saying no  
to motherhood  
she did not desire.

But she began  
to ask

whether she had also  
said no

to any version  
of her life

where she did not  
hold every variable  
in her hands.

Back home,  
she sat  
at her dining table,

another purchase  
she had once  
been proud of,

and pulled out  
a notebook.

She wrote  
a sentence.

What have I built  
to protect myself  
that now prevents me  
from belonging?

The answers
came in fragments.

Predictable routines.  
Layered savings  
that made generosity  
feel dangerous.  
A reluctance  
to share keys  
to her space.  
Rules  
about what time  
people could call.  
A refusal  
to need anyone’s help  
without a plan  
to repay it  
immediately.  
A faith  
that spoke often  
about trust in God  
but rarely  
about trusting  
people.

None of these,  
on their own,  
were wrong.

Taken together,

they formed  
a cage.

Beautiful.  
Ethical.  
Impressive.

But still a cage.

Naomi remembered

how certain women  
had once been praised

for disappearing  
into motherhood,

and how fiercely  
she had resisted  
that script.

Now,  
she saw a strange parallel.

She had not disappeared  
into children.

She had disappeared  
into competence.

Into self-sufficiency.  
Into control.

Different expression.  
Same risk.

A life  
where her value

was tied  
to what she could carry

without dropping anything.

She closed the notebook.

This realization  
did not magically  
make her reckless.

She did not rush  
to dismantle  
every boundary.

She did not call  
her ex  
and say,

I’ve changed my mind.

She did something  
smaller.

She invited  
a friend over

on a Tuesday

without cleaning  
the entire apartment.

She left  
a mug in the sink.

She let the conversation  
stretch past  
the time  
she usually  
guarded  
for emails.

When her friend left,  
she felt uneasy.

No disaster  
had occurred.

But something  
in her schedule  
had shifted.

She sat  
with the discomfort.

Another night,  
she accepted help.

A minor inconvenience,  
car issues,  
nothing dramatic.

The old Naomi  
would have handled it  
alone.

Rideshare.  
Rental.  
Problem solved.

This time,  
when a friend said,

I can drive you,  
it’s not a big deal,

she hesitated,

then said,

Okay.

Sitting  
in the passenger seat,  
her hands  
doing nothing,

she felt both  
gratitude

and panic.

Gratitude,  
because someone  
else was there.  
Panic,  
because she was not  
the one  
controlling the route.

She breathed.

This is what  
being known  
feels like,  
she thought.

Not just people  
respecting  
your independence.

People  
taking  
some of the weight

without asking you  
to stop being  
strong.

Gradually,  
small experiments.

Sharing more  
in therapy  
than stories  
about the past.  
Talking about  
present loneliness,  
not just old pain.  
Letting herself  
say,

I don’t know  
how to build  
a life

where I am safe  
and still reachable.

She did not yet  
have the answer.

But asking the question  
was new.

One Sunday,  
she went to church
and actually stayed  
after the service.

Not out of duty.  
Out of curiosity.

A woman  
she barely knew  
started talking.

About bills.  
About kids.  
About exhaustion.

Naomi  
felt the old instinct

to turn it  
into advice.

Instead,  
she listened.

Really listened.

When the woman finished,  
she said only,

That sounds heavy.  
I’m sorry  
you’re carrying so much.

The woman’s eyes  
filled.

Thank you,  
she whispered.  
Most people  
either try to fix it  
or tell me  
to count my blessings.

Naomi nodded.

She understood that script.

Walking home,  
she realized:

for once,  
she had not used  
someone’s vulnerability  
as a mirror  
for her own fear.

She had simply  
been present.

No transaction.  
No ledger.

Perhaps,
she thought,

this is what
security is for,

not just  
to keep danger out,

but to give you  
enough stability

to actually
show up

without calculating  
every risk.

Naomi  
was not ready

to dismantle  
her entire fortress.

But she began  
to suspect

that salvation

could not be defined  
only as safety.

Safety  
had saved her.

Now,  
it was slowly  
smothering her.

The exits  
she had built

had done their job.

They had stopped her  
from being trapped.

But as she sat  
in her own living room,

surrounded  
by proof

that she could leave  
anywhere,  
anytime,

she faced  
a new question:

What would it mean

to choose  
to stay?

Not in hunger.  
Not in abuse.  
Not in scripts  
that erased her.

But in relationships,  
places,  
callings,

where she could be  
seen,

risk small disappointments,

and still trust

that she would not  
lose herself

if she let  
someone else

be a part  
of her life’s  
architecture.

Naomi had built  
a life  
that could not  
collapse easily.

For the first time,

she admitted

that the same walls  
that held her

might also be the thing

keeping her from ever  
knowing

what home  
could feel like

when it was not defined  
by how quickly

she could get  
out.

## Chapter 12
### The Soft Thing She Did Not Trust

Softness  
had always looked dangerous  
to Naomi,

because every soft thing  
she had known

had been asked  
to carry too much.

Pillows  
in houses  
where sleep was thin.  
Mothers  
in families  
that leaned on them  
until their backs  
forgot straightness.  
Faith  
in churches  
that told women  
to surrender  
while letting men  
keep their power.

Soft things  
did not last long  
in her childhood.

They tore.  
They broke.  
They got blamed.

So Naomi  
made herself  
into something else.

Sharp.  
Prepared.  
Structured.  
Hard to move.

Now,  
sitting in a life  
built on control,

she realized

she did not know  
how to be soft  
without expecting  
to be used.

So she started small.

No grand gestures.  
No dramatic transformation.

Just experiments.

The first one  
came disguised  
as a simple dinner.

A friend  
she trusted

asked,

Can I come over?  
Not for a serious talk.  
Just…  
to be.

Naomi almost suggested  
a restaurant.

Neutral ground.  
Predictable.  
Bill-splitting.

Instead,  
she said,

Come.

She did not  
over-prepare.

She did not  
cook a complicated meal  
to prove competence.  
She did not  
clean every surface  
until the apartment  
looked like a showroom.

She made  
something simple.

Pasta.  
Salad.  
Bread bought,  
not baked.

When her friend arrived,  
there were still  
crumbs on the counter.  
A dish  
in the sink.

Naomi’s skin  
crawled a little.

She was used  
to presenting  
only finished versions  
of herself.

Her friend noticed  
none of it.

They sat.  
They ate.  
They talked.

Not about contracts  
or sermons  
or trauma.

About small things.

A show she’d started.  
A book she hated  
but finished anyway.  
A joke about how  
they used to stretch  
one meal  
for three days.

There was a moment  
when her friend said,

I’m tired  
of being strong  
for everyone.

Naomi’s instinct  
kicked in.

Offer solutions.  
Offer strategy.  
Offer money.

Instead,  
she reached  
across the table,

placed her hand  
over her friend’s,

and said,

You don’t have to be  
strong here.

The words  
felt unfamiliar  
in her own mouth.

Softness  
offered  
without agenda.

Her friend’s eyes  
filled.

They sat like that  
for a while.

No one  
took a picture.  
No one  
turned it into content.

When the night ended,  
her friend said,

Thank you  
for the normal.

Naomi closed the door  
after she left,

leaned against it,

and realized

the world  
had not ended

because she had let  
someone  
see her life

without armor.

Another experiment  
came  
on a Saturday.

Church  
was hosting  
a small event.

Nothing grand.  
Children’s activities.  
Snacks.  
Parents  
half-distracted.

Naomi  
had avoided  
children’s spaces  
for years.

Too many assumptions.  
Too many eyes  
studying her like  
a woman  
on the verge  
of realization.

But this time,  
she stayed.

A little girl,  
hair in uneven braids,  
stood alone  
near a table  
of crayons.

Her mother  
was across the room,  
pulled into conversation.

Naomi walked over.

What are you drawing?

The girl shrugged.

A house.

Naomi smiled.

Can I see?

The girl  
held up the page.

Wide roof.  
Crooked door.  
Windows  
that looked like eyes.

Naomi felt  
a familiar ache.

Houses  
had never been  
simple to her.

This one,  
in crayon,  
looked safe.

She crouched.

It’s beautiful,  
she said.

The girl frowned.

It’s not.

Naomi tilted her head.

Why not?

The girl hesitated.

Because  
we don’t have  
a house like this.

Naomi’s chest  
tightened.

Softness  
wanted to promise  
what she could not.

Instead,  
she said,

Sometimes  
we draw things  
we want  
before we have them.

The girl considered that.

Do you have  
a house like this?

Naomi thought.

Yes,  
she said slowly.

In some ways.  
No  
in others.

The girl nodded,  
as if that made sense.

Will you draw with me?

Naomi glanced around.

Old instincts  
warned her.

Don’t let them  
turn this  
into proof  
that you’d be  
a great mother.

She ignored them.

Sat.

Took a crayon.

Drew  
her own house.

Not with perfect lines.

With a door  
slightly open.

The girl leaned in.

Who lives there?

Naomi smiled.

Me.

And maybe  
some friends.

The girl grinned.

Can I come?

Naomi laughed.

In the picture?  
Sure.

She drew  
a small figure  
beside the door.

They spent  
twenty minutes  
like that.

Coloring.  
Talking about nothing.

Naomi  
did not feel compelled  
to turn it into  
a metaphor.

She simply  
let herself enjoy

the softness

of being kind  
to a child

without  
adding it  
to any ledger.

When the girl’s mother  
came to collect her,

she mouthed  
thank you.

Naomi nodded.

Later,  
someone said,

You’re so good  
with kids.  
Maybe one day,

Naomi lifted a hand.

Maybe one day  
I’ll keep being good  
with kids

without that  
having to mean

anything else.

For once,  
the conversation  
ended there.

Another experiment  
was even quieter.

Prayer.

For years,  
her prayers  
had sounded  
like negotiations.

Lord,  
keep me paid.  
Keep me safe.  
Keep me sharp.  
Keep me out of  
the old patterns.

She had prayed  
like someone  
managing risk.

Now,  
one night,  
she sat  
on her couch,

turned off  
her phone,

and said,

Okay.

No lists.

No requests.

Just truth.

She closed her eyes.

I don’t know  
how to live

without making  
safety

the only thing  
I worship,

she whispered.

I don’t know  
how to trust softness  
without expecting  
someone  
to weaponize it.

I don’t want  
to become hard  
to the point  
that even You  
cannot reach me  
without me  
editing the interaction.

The honesty  
left her exposed.

Still,

no lightning.

No booming voice.

Just a steady  
inner sense,

not loud,

that said,

Softness  
is not the same  
as surrendering  
your life.

It is simply  
being present  
with your heart  
unarmed.

Naomi  
did not fully  
trust that yet.

But she sat  
in the space.

No agenda.

Just breath.

It was  
uncomfortable.

It was also  
a relief.

The next day,  
a friend offered  
something simple.

I made extra soup,  
she texted.  
Can I drop some off?

The old Naomi  
would have said,

No, I’m fine.  
Or,  
Only if I pay you.

This time,

she replied,

Yes.  
Thank you.

Her friend  
arrived  
with a container,  
smiled,  
chatted briefly,

then left.

Naomi  
stood in her kitchen,

holding warmth  
that had cost her  
nothing.

Her hands  
itched to repay.

Transfer money.  
Send flowers.  
Offer something.

Instead,  
she whispered,

Thank you,

to the friend,  
to God,  
to the version of herself  
learning

that not every kindness  
is a trap.

She ate.

It tasted  
like more  
than soup.

It tasted  
like a tiny  
reversal

of a lifetime  
of transactions.

Not everything  
has to be earned.

Not everything  
has to be justified.

Not everything  
has to be measured  
against exits.

Softness  
could be  
a moment

not a contract.

In the weeks  
that followed,

she made  
a quiet practice

of small,  
soft things.

Answering calls  
without checking  
if she had the emotional budget  
first,  
not all of them,  
just some.  
Sending messages  
that said,  
I was thinking of you,  
without attaching advice.  
Letting herself  
laugh loudly  
in public  
without scanning  
the room  
for who was watching.  
Sitting at home  
with music  
and no productivity,  
even when guilt  
knocked.

Each time,

her nervous system  
twitched.

This is dangerous,  
it hissed.

This is how  
people get  
used.

But nothing  
terrible happened.

No one  
suddenly owned her.  
No bill  
appeared  
with her heart  
itemized.

She began  
to understand

that softness

had never been  
the enemy.

The enemy  
had been

systems

that demanded  
softness

only from women  
and then punished them  
for breaking.

Now,  
as a grown woman  
with her own keys,

she could choose

where  
and with whom

to be soft.

Not everywhere.  
Not always.

Just enough  
to remind herself

that protection

was not the only  
muscle  
worth exercising.

One evening,  
as the day  
folded into dark,

Naomi sat  
by her window,

city lights  
blurring slightly,

and realized

she felt something

she had not felt  
in a long time.

Not control.  
Not relief.

A small,  
fragile,

uncontrollable  
goodness.

It did not come  
from a deal.  
Or a purchase.  
Or a praise report.

It came  
from having spent  
an entire day

being present

without calculating  
every interaction.

She did not know  
if she could  
live like this  
all the time.

She did not plan  
to abandon

the structures  
that kept her safe.

But she did know,

now,

that softness  
was not always  
a trap.

Sometimes,  
it was simply

the part of her

that hunger  
had forced underground,

finally stretching

in a world

where she could  
begin

to trust

that not every soft thing  
was destined  
to be broken.

## Closing
### The Woman Who Fed Herself

Naomi was never saved
by one thing alone.

Not money.

Not love.

Not work.

Not God.

Not resolve.

Her story
was not a neat arc

from scarcity
to abundance,

from trauma
to triumph.

It was
a long,

complicated
negotiation

between hunger
and holiness,

between exits
and belonging,

between safety
and softness.

Money
had answered
her first prayers.

It turned lights
back on.

It filled fridges.

It bought silence
in houses
where shouting
used to live.

Money gave her
exits.

A cab
when a room shifted.

A lease
with her name
on it.

A plane ticket
out of cities

that held
too many ghosts.

For that,
she refused
to apologize.

She would not rewrite
her own history

to fit anyone's
clean theology
about greed.

The poor house
she came from

had taught her
that romanticizing lack

is a luxury
only afforded
to people

who have never
had to choose

between dignity
and rent.

So no,

Naomi did not repent
for wanting money.

She repented
for asking it

to do jobs
it was never built
to do.

For expecting it
to protect her
from every loss.

For trusting it
more than she trusted
any living person.

For letting it decide
whose love
she believed.

She did not become
less ambitious.

She became clearer
about why.

Ambition no longer
needed to be

an apology
for a childhood

she could not
control.

It could be
an expression

of a woman
who knew

her mind
was sharp,

her gifts
were real,

and her existence
was not a debt.

She did not
suddenly start
wanting children.

Love did not appear
and erase
her clarity.

She still refused
to offer her body,

time,

and future

as evidence
that she had
finally become

a good woman.

Motherhood remained

neither idol
nor enemy.

Just something
she refused
to use

as currency.

God did not arrive
only at the end.

He had always
been there.

In the counting house.

In the scholarship
letter.

In the expensive rooms.

In the airport prayers.

In the bathrooms
where she whispered

barely formed words.

But for years,
Naomi spoke to Him

mainly as
a risk manager.

Lord,
keep me
from hunger.

Lord,
show me
exits.

Lord,
do not let me
become

the women
who disappeared.

Now,
her prayers shifted.

Not away
from safety.

Toward fullness.

Teach me
how to live
beyond survival.

Teach me
how to receive care
without debt.

Teach me
how to be soft
without being broken.

Teach me
how to trust You
without making security

the only acceptable
answer.

She did not expect
God to choose
poverty for her

to prove
a point.

She did expect Him
to show her

when her fortress
had become
a temple.

Slowly,

Naomi let herself
belong

in small ways.

A friend's laugh
in her kitchen.

A child's drawing
on her fridge.

A church bench
where she stayed

after the benediction.

A couch
where she cried

without apologizing
for wasting time.

None of these
were grand gestures.

They were meals.

Little ones.

Taken on purpose.

Because somewhere
along the line,

she realized

she had spent
a lifetime

learning how to feed
everyone else's
expectations,

or starve them,

and very few years

learning how
to feed herself

without guilt.

She began
to define enough

differently.

Not as a number
she could never lose.

Not as a lifestyle
no one could threaten.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can leave
if I must,

but I am no longer
living

as if leaving
is the only
holy move.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can pay
for my own table,

and still let someone
bring soup

without assuming
the spoon

is a leash.

Enough started
to sound like this:

I can walk
into rooms
I built,

and still let God
tell me

when the room
is not the point.

Naomi did not stop
wanting more.

She simply stopped
asking hunger

to tell her

what enough
should mean.

She did not become
the soft woman

the world
had hoped for,

one who finally
laid down
all ambition

in exchange
for a simpler story.

She became
something rarer.

A woman
who knew

how much
it had cost

to feed herself,

who refused
to starve again,

and who still chose,

day by day,

not to let fear
be the only thing

that ever felt full.

She was not
heartless.

She was a woman

learning how to eat

without becoming
hunger.

## About the Author

Sabino Pereira writes emotionally charged fiction, poetic essays, and reflective stories about identity, love, faith, dignity, survival, and the quiet cost of being misunderstood.

His work often explores people who are judged before they are known: women carrying reputations they did not fully choose, wounded lovers trying to find language for what broke them, and souls learning how to live truthfully in a world that prefers simple labels.

The *Bad Girl Gospel* series is part confession, part character study, and part spiritual reflection. Through stories such as Selah, Diana, Noa, and Naomi, Sabino examines the lives of women who have been desired, moralized over, misread, rejected, and spoken about more often than they have been truly heard.

He writes with a cinematic, intimate style, blending emotional realism with lyrical prose. His stories are not interested in perfect heroes or simple villains, but in the complicated human space between shame and freedom, silence and confession, survival and becoming.

Sabino Pereira is also a creator, musician, and independent author exploring books, music, and visual storytelling across different creative projects.

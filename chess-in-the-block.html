Chess in the Block

Survival. Silence. Strategy.

A reflective book about life on the block, using chess to speak about pressure, family, class, love, loss, masculinity, motherhood, and the invisible strategies people use to survive.

What It Is

A book about people who learned strategy before peace.

Some people are born into the game protected. Others begin the match already backed against a wall, trying to survive before they even understand the rules.

Chess in the Block turns the pieces into people: pawns who carry worlds, knights who find impossible routes, bishops who read hidden pain, rooks who hold the line, queens who move in every direction, and kings trapped inside the performance of command.

This is not a chess manual. It is a book about learning how to move without losing yourself.

Tone

Urban, reflective, direct.

  • Simple English, written to feel lived rather than translated.
  • Short sections with a spoken, intimate rhythm.
  • Family, class, pride, silence, grief, and emotional survival.
  • No cheap motivation. No romanticising pain. Just the board seen clearly.

Opening - The Board

From Chess in the Block

Some people are born into the game protected.

They are born in quiet homes. With food in the fridge. With adults who have time to ask how they feel. With room to make mistakes. Room to dream. Room to figure out who they are before the world starts charging them for it.

Others are not.

Others begin the match already backed against a wall, trying to survive before they even understand the rules.

On the block where I grew up, nobody talked about chess.

We talked about rent.

About who got locked up.

About whose mother was working another double.

About which street you did not cross after dark.

About bills on the kitchen table and dreams that had to wait because somebody needed groceries before they needed hope.

But looking back, I can see it now.

Everybody was moving like a piece.

The pawns woke up first.

They caught the early bus, cleaned buildings they would never own, carried boxes in warehouses with no windows, cooked food for people who never learned their names, and came home too tired to explain why silence was sometimes the only rest they had left.

The knights moved differently.

They never had a straight road, so they learned the side streets. Some became creative. Some became dangerous. Some were only trying to survive a world that kept closing doors in their face and then blamed them for climbing through windows.

The rooks stood tall: fathers, older brothers, mothers who had no time to fall apart, people who made pride look like strength because nobody had ever given them permission to be tired.

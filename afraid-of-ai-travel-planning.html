Afraid of AI · Chapter 3

AI and Travel Planning

Travel planning used to be built on friction.

AI starts to dissolve the fog around routes, prices, timing, and itinerary design.

April 10, 2026 Series Travel Friction
Back to QP Thoughts Back to series

Travel planning used to be built on friction. You gathered information from too many places, compared flights and hotels by hand, and still felt unsure if you were missing something. The travel agent's value often lived in knowing where to look faster and how to reduce that chaos.

AI has started to dissolve that fog.

Today, a traveler can ask for itineraries shaped around budget, season, pace, and personal taste, compare options quickly, track prices over time, and get instant suggestions for routes, timing, and local experiences. Research that once took hours now happens in minutes, often before a human agent is even considered.

Ideas become easier to generate. Options become easier to filter. The feeling of not knowing where to start becomes harder to justify.

Why this is disruptive

That is what makes AI disruptive in travel. Not because it replaces every planner, but because it removes the protective value of confusion. It gives the traveler more clarity, more structure, and more confidence at the very beginning of the process. And when the traveler arrives with a clearer picture and sharper questions, the profession has to offer more than generic packages and recycled recommendations.

The fear of AI in travel is not just fear of automated tools. It is the fear of losing control over an ecosystem that relied on asymmetry: knowing routes, deals, and trade-offs the customer could not easily see, and turning that gap into value. When AI can surface options, track prices, and expose compromises on demand, "trust me, I know this market" stops being enough on its own.

What still matters

In that sense, AI does not simply change how trips are planned and booked. It changes what a travel professional can no longer hide behind.

Good agents will still matter. Complex itineraries still benefit from human judgment. Experience and local nuance still add real value. But the era of surviving on complexity, opacity, and confusion is slowly coming to an end.
